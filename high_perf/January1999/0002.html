I got a similar 533 LX from Microway and NDP FORTRAN. After some testing, I
returned the compiler and converted to DU. Unfortunately, I can't say the
conversion was an overwhelming success. For all its flaws Linux is at least
as good as DU if not better. The performance of the DU compilers is quite
good. Other aspects are less satisfactory. One of my motivations for the
conversion was the very weak FORTRAN support in gdb. As it turns out, the
DU debuggers are as bad or worse. There is no support at all for any intrinsics
or the exponential.

   print sin(x)**b

just produces error messages from ladebug(1). dbx is even worse!

Performance of NDP FORTRAN was rather varied. For simple tests such as dot
products and polynomial evaluations, it was within 10-20% of the DU performance.
For another test, my actual problem, it was 3 times slower. This is because
of the handling of sqrt(). The problem has 3 sqrt() operations per loop
iteration. Interestingly, in some case g77 was faster than NDP, though in
general NDP produced much quicker runs. The documentation of NDP is a bit
weak though. The only real discussion of the compiler flags is the tutorial
that Fried has on the Microway web page.

In retrospect, unless you absolutely have to have the best possible performance
on a wide range of problems, the NDP compiler is a pretty good choice. It
is certainly far cheaper than the almost $2500 license fee for DU & Digital
FORTRAN. Mike Warren et al report using the Gnu tools w/ the dgemm routines.
If your problem is concentrated in a small loop consider just hand optimizing
the code produced by gcc & g77 after you get it written and debugged.

Note that although it appears to be possible to run DU code on a Linux box,
there seem to be licensing issues. I've not looked into these yet. I
will probably simply let DU generate assembly sequences which I will then
move to Linux for assembly and linking.
