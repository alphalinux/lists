On Fri, 25 Jun 1999, Alvin Starr wrote:

> I dropped out of reading the "bashing" thread of messages a while ago but
> just dropped in to see the last few postings.

I did something similar, and I have until now resisted adding to what is a
pointless flamefest. IMHO, blame is assignable to both sides. The reason
I'm writing is to specifically address some of Alvin's points.

> I opened the licence agreement with the 1.2 jdk and was a little supprised
> by what I read.

I have not done this, but I am a Java developer and I have been following Sun's
position relatively closely, so maybe I can shed some light on this. I assume
throughout that you are speaking of the Sun Community Source License (SCSL).

> The first is for "internal use only" and implys that I cannot use this
> software for anything other than evaluation.
>
> The second loosens that a little and says that I can develop and
> distribute code that has been developed with the JDK, but only if I do not
> create any classes.

My understanding is that both points are correct. The whole reason Sun created
the SCSL was to address industry concerns that Sun was not being flexible
enough in its managing of the standard.

Sun's dilemma was to allow interested parties access to the source while not
allowing the standard to fragment (or in other words, keep control of it.)
If they released it under an actual open-source license, someone else could
add their custom changes and market it as an actual product.

Now, someone could do this anyway, with a clean-room implementation (and in
fact Microsoft HAS, which is why Sun is suing them.) But at least Sun doesn't
have to help them out in competing by handing over the Sun JDK as a starting
point.

In reality, the SCSL grants nothing. Sun owns the Java standard and standards
process regardless of the status of their particular JDK implementation, so
even if they HAD released the JDK under, say, the GPL, anyone who modified it
still couldn't market it as Java, but only as Java-derived. The SCSL works
the same way the original AT&T Unix license to universities did: let people
see it, play with it, maybe improve it, while not letting them usurp it. If
you want to actually market your changes, you have to pass Sun's JCK tests to
make sure you're not usurping the standard.

This is the reason Uncle George doesn't like it, and it's a perfectly valid
complaint, especially since you have to pay to license the JCK. This fee is
precisely to make sure that folks like Uncle George can't release a JDK: Sun
wants to make sure that only "serious" parties (i.e. companies with $$) can
release a JDK. With the SCSL, Sun decided they didn't need the Non-Commercial
License anymore and dropped it.

On the other hand, some people don't care about Java source, and only care
that there's a JDK for their platform. This is the blackdown.org
philosophy. I'm sure they'd opt for an open-source license if they could, but
they can't so they settle for the restrictions of the SCSL, especially since
Sun licensed them the JCK for free (which is Sun's "support" of the blackdown
project.) Having a closed-source JDK is better than no JDK in their eyes,
which is also a perfectly valid point.

> Off hand this would seen to be a restrictive licence and leads me to
> believe that dispite sun's hype this is a closed standard.

Without a doubt. Sun touts the Java Community Process which they created to
expedite the creation and functioning of working groups to add standard
extensions to the language, and it works reasonably well. It is not, however,
open: it is totally managed by Sun, though the content of the extensions is
a consensus of a group of experts who may not be Sun employees.

> At the end of the day I wonder if it would not be beter to push the
> develpment of an Open Source, FSF licenced version of the JDK for linux
> and tell sun "so long and thanks for all the fish"

...and here's where I get back on topic for this list. :) If you want a
truly free JDK for the Alpha, you want either Kaffe or Japhar. Kaffe seems
to be further along than Japhar. (Does Japhar even run at all on the Alpha?
I haven't played with it.)

Some people don't like Kaffe, though, since the license holder is Transvirtual,
which also licenses Kaffe under a proprietary commercial license. In fact,
there are effectively two Kaffe codebases: the GPL version and the
Transvirtual version. Transvirtual keeps the two trees strictly separate,
so the noise you hear about Microsoft paying Transvirtual to make proprietary
MS extensions to Kaffe does *not* affect the GPL version of the code. You
can contribute to the GPL version without agreeing to assign your code over to
Transvirtual for use in their product.

Unfortunately, Kaffe is not as far along on the Alpha as it is on Intel. The
JIT does not work on the Alpha, and a few other things need work. Still,
progress is being made. I do use Kaffe as the JVM for the Servlet engine I
have running on my home machine, so it does run, it's just not blazing fast.

On a different note, I was at JavaOne and had the opportunity to spend a few
minutes at the Compaq booth, where they were touting the high-performance
64-bit Java environment they inherited from Digital (AKA, of course, as the
JDK for Digital Unix.)

I asked if, in light of the CPML and compiler releases, we could expect to
see this JDK eventually released for Linux. I was told that that would be
the logical progression of events, but that there are currently no efforts
underway toward that goal. So, maybe (probably?) someday, but not soon.

Anyway, I hope this was useful. Sorry if it was offtopic, and of course
everything herein is IMHO, and according to my understanding. :)
