> > Very good input from Micahel Huntingdon.
> >
> Ditto... but is it just whistling past the graveyard?
>
> > We allso said allmost the same things to Compaq people and they said: No, the
> > Alpha and Itanium designs are very different in 2 places which is basically
> > incompatible: 1. The CPU switching and 2. the 4 procesor states (needed for at
> > least VMS) in Alpha.
> >
> > They said that Msoft would have difficulties making an NT/XP (whatever) for 64
> > bit if current Itanium design is changed to Alpha design.
> >
> These two are OS issues, and spell trouble for VMS (Compaq) vs XP (Microsoft).
> So, who do you suppose Compaq will support?
>
> My problem is quite simple. And I have pointed it out to several people who
> simply cringe when they realize what I have said.
>
> So far N O T H I N G has been said about the Alpha purchase by Intel!
>
> Intel has posted a copy of the press release and pointed everyone to Compaq
> for "other information." No other words have been spoken, let alone written.
>
> The slides posted on the Inquirer site are Compaq's slides, not Intel's
> slides. The Intel IA64 road-map is unchanged.... in 2004 the IA64 still will
> perform slower than today's Alphas.
>
> I've been in this business a long time, but some things have been
> constants.
>
> I used RCA gear when Univac bought RCA... Univac had no clue what they
> bought.
>
> I used VAX and Alpha gear when Compaq bought Digital ... Compaq had no idea
> what they bought.
>
> Now I use Alpha when Intel bought it... and I see zero indication that
> Intel has a clue what they bought.
>
> > Intel did not purchase the technology to eliminate competition. It was
> > purchased because Alpha has many years and a great deal of money already
> > behind the advanced research and engineering. Alpha also has nearly 10
> > years of unsurpassed/stable performance behind it. Sounds reasonably
> > compelling, especially if you are a company who, for quite some time, has
> > been telling the world you'll be providing CPU technology that will offer
> > the best performance in the industry.
> >
> Don't forget, the last sentence in the paragraph has never been true and is
> simply Intel's attempt to fend off the Anti-Trust lawywers. (And except for
> maybe, the very begining, I do mean never.) Other CPUs have always out
> performed Intel ever since RISC happened, and one can easliy argue that the
> VAX CPU also out performed the Intel CPU. And even without Apple, AMD will
> support the "megahertz-myth" proposition. Intel may have offered "good" but
> the masss rarely ever get "the best."
>
> You'll notice that even in the defense -- the fact that Alpha was already
> at the point where Intel could never hope to get is not mentioned; that
> Alpha was 2-3 years ahead of Itanium performance with the gap widening.
> (Although the way some Classic Compaq folks explained the decision to drop
> Alpha -- the IA64 would have had performance which surpassed the Alpha by
> 2004, so why should we bother to compete.)
>
> The difference between a CICS and a RISC instruction set is significant.
> Compilers can "deal with it," and no doubt will. BUT the argument between
> CICS and RISC all along has been we're faster than you are... and RISC won.
>
> The dominant reason for dropping the port of Tru64 to IA64 years ago was
> that the port ran faster on the IA64 emulator running on the Alpha than
> it did on IA64 silicon -- so why bother.
>
> No, I think that you will see an IA64 that simply looks like the existing
> IA64 designs -- but with compilers that can fix the branch prediction that
> the hardware can't do, and maybe the lock-step instructions that Tandem
> needs which should have been in EV7 (they haven't been mentioned that I've
> seen.)
>
> So, will Itanium really be "Alpha inside?" Or will it continue to be "PARC
> inside?" The philosophy between the two is very different, and compilers
> can't change that. Is Intel willing to admit that they "screwed up" and
> start over? Or will they simply try to "fix" what is an inherently flawed
> design. Sadly, history teaches us that it will be the latter.
>
> Until Intel makes a presentation on the future, we will have zero idea what
> that future looks like on the inside. "We" (the Encomapass aka DECUS - UNIX
> TAG - Techincal Advisory Group aka Unix Sig) have asked Q to ask Intel to
> come talk to us at CETS2001 in Ananheim next month. Maybe they will --
> However, I don't think they will, I think we'll just get Compaq telling us
> what their vision of the future is, just like the slides from the Inquirer.
> (The session planner is due to come up today, so we'll see if anything can
> be determined from the session list.)
>
> I'm still afraid that Intel simply got rid of a very nasty competitor.
>
