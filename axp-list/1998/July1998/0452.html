On 07-Jul-98 Wes Nakamura wrote:
>
> While I'm on the subject, what tape backups have people used
> successfully? I'd like to go as inexpensive as possible (which
> probably rules out scsi). I've read that possibly the Travan 5GB
> parallel might work, but what are people using? I'd prefer not to go
> internal, either (hm, does the XL300 even have IDE...?).

My advice would be to go SCSI if you possibly can. You have a lot of relatively
inexpensive options:

Pick up a used 4mm DAT or 8mm Exabyte drive. These can be found on sites such
as:

http://www.compsyscon.com

Prices are in the range of $200-internal. I actually bought a couple of 8mm
drives for $20 each but that was at a Wyoming State Surplus Property Warehouse,
so unless you happen to be in the boonies you'll have to factor in travel (no,
they don't do mail order). These things are temperamental but the usual
problem, dirty heads, can be cured by most 13-year-old model train enthusiasts
:-)

I use a Conner CTMS-3200 scsi drive that uses QIC-3080XLF tapes to get 2Gb.
native. The problem is that the tapes are expensive ($25-$30) and I don't know
if the drive is still made. It has been reliable though, and only cost me $300
new.

I've seen some Seagate SCSI tape drives at $200-$300 prices in the catalogs. I
don't know anything about them but Seagate is generally reliable (these days
anyway) and they *are* SCSI.

Parallel port solutions are iffy at best, and the drives that hang off the
floppy controller are (in my experience) not reliable. Both are also *slow*.

>
> Oh, one other thing. The backup I was doing was onto a jaz, which I
> was formatting in dos format so I could read it from a non-linux
> machine. I'm using mkdosfs which I got from the redhat contrib
> directory, but it doesn't create fs's that linux recognizes. Anyone
> know what's wrong or where I could get a working version?

I wouldn't backup to a DOS format *anything*. The limitations on filenames
alone would make that disasterous, to say nothing of lost ownership and
permissions.

I have formatted Jaz drives for DOS simply using mkfs -t msdos, but again I'd
not do this for backup.

Some friends of mine are backing up to IDE drives. IMHO this is risky because
*when* the drive fails you are lost. With removable drives or tapes you can
always put the media into a new drive. IDE drives are cheap though, and maybe
if you used multiple ones this might be a good solution, certainly faster than
tape.

All just my $0.02 of course.
