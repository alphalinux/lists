Dave Gilbert wrote:

> On Sun, 17 May 1998, Wes Bauske wrote:
>
> > We're not talking about a broken OS here. We're talking about broken
> > applications. Anyone on this list will tell you that picking up a verion
> > that is 'x.0' is the bleeding edge. In general, those people interested
> > in 64 bit computing are the types of folks who are willing to work thru
> > the problems that are guaranteed to exist relating to that situation.
>
> Its not just long ints/pointers that are causing problems; and its not
> just applications. Most people who fiddle around programming and who can
> fix programs and who could fix int/pointer problems may expect at least a
> stable development environment.
>
> It would be nice to have a stable X server, a stable combination of
> compiler/C library/C++ library and stable floating point computations.
> It would be nice for the IDE driver to be able to get a respectable
> throughput on LX boards, or to have a stable fully capable web browser.
>

Since when did the X server, compilers, and browser become partof the OS?? They are important
applications but certainly not
part of the OS. Maybe you're thinking like someone else I won't
mention here.
I use a Matrox and an ATI RAGE II. Both work except for the 20
windows problem. Though I don't use C++ in my line of work, I
thought egcs 1.0.2 was working?? IDE does *work*, just not in
DMA mode. Still haven't been able to confirm DMA mode in the
2.1.xx kernels works on my systems yet though. Course, you could
always put up the $50 for a NCR810 SCSI card. As far as browsers
go, I agree it would be nice to have Netscape stable on Linux Alpha.
For now, I use one of my PPro's. I guess because I run compute
kernels most of the time on Alpha, I don't miss the browser there.

> Once Linux/Alpha distributions have a stable environment in which people
> can work, then people can basically use the machine as a production
> machine and fix porting problems in applications. But at the moment,
> having used Linux/Alpha very happily for 5 months, I would not recommend
> it to be used by anyone on a day-to-day basis.
>

You've used it "happily" but don't recommend it. Seems a contradictionto me.

> In particular I'm disappointed at the X server stability (on my Matrox
> card), and its tendency to sprag the machine at startup occasionally.
> (Yes I am on the latest version), and I'm disappointed at the complete
> mess RH5.0 made of the compilers/C++ libraries.
>

Shouldn't live on the bleeding edge. If this bothers you, go back to RH4.2or wait for RH5.1. Are you
using a Matrox Millenium II or the old
card? Mine is the old card and it works fine. It's interesting to me that
people just expect things to work for their HW setup. I usually go
find out what works and only buy that to start with. Doesn't matter
what OS/HW combination it is. That's pretty much the smartest
way to go to minimize headaches later.

> I'm happy to report bugs and try and fix them where I can; but I can
> understand programmers who just want to fix/port their application and
> basically expect the system and development software to be stable; when it
> is it will move from a system suitable for people who want to play about
> and fix deep and complex things, to the point where normal users and
> programmers can just fix their applications.
>
> Sorry, but its just not their yet.

Well, it's there enough for folks to buy 50-100 systems for compute
farms so I guess I disagree with your conclusion.

I'm personally waiting for the 21264 to arrive. My alpha's are slightly
slower than my RS/6000's (397's). If I ran DU on them, they would
outperform the RS/6000's but with a 21264, there's no doubt they
will be the fastest microprocessor around. Course if you have millions
of bucks, I could find something faster. I'm trying to keep the price
tag a little lower.

My $.02.
