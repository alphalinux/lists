Seems to me the real answer for the ever-popular Netscape issue
would be to include a static-linked executable, not the best
solution, but it "should" work, right?

I would find it hard to believe that Compaq will license a few
libraries/etc. to allow a non-DU licensed box to run DU executables!
What is the incentive to buy DU, if you are only going to run
someone elses code?

I suspect that Compaq/Digital would view the use of DU libraries
as having a negative impact on their sales of Digital Unix OS. Of
course, one could argue that many of the people that would take
advantage of this license would never have bought DU in the first
place, so thye are not, in fact, losing that many sales.

That sounds like a good argument, but let's think it
through - Compaq/Digital decides that they want to offer Red Hat
the right to distribute their DU run-time libraries, and for this
use, Compaq/Digital wants $50,000 (just to pull a number out of
the air) for this - fine, Red Hat then pays this money on the
expectation that this will drive sales of Red Hat for Alpha
through the roof, but they will have to raise the price a bit to
cover the expense, say $10. That means that Red Hat is out money
UNTIL they sell the 5,001 copy of Red Hat for Alpha.

Now, let's make this number real - Compaq/Digital wants a
few thousand dollars for the real, full Digital Unix, so let's say
a copy of DU costs $2,500. Now Compaq/Digital figures they will
lose out on a total of 100 licenses of DU as people buy boxes to
run Red Hat w/DU Libs. (any additional hardware sales for this
platform they would not have had before will be counted as "profit"
- the reason they are in business. Now, that means that Compaq/Digital
will expect to generate $250,000 from it's license activites, and
let's just say that BOTH Red Hat and Debian decide to license these
libraries, and let's further say that a third, as yet unnamed group
puts together an Alpha/Linux port and they too want to use the new
DU libraries - that means that Red Hat will have to pay 1/3 of the
$250,000, or $83,000 +/-. Now, how will Red Hat cover their expenses
for this expensive license, raise prices. How many copies of Red
Hat linux do you think are sold for the Alpha platform? Well, all I
can do is guess, but I think it would be EXTREMELY OPTOMISTIC that
Red Hat sells 83,000 copies of Alpha Linux. I also don't think they
sell 41,500 copies, nor do I suspect they sell 20,750 copies BUT,
let's be optomistic and say they sell 10,375 copies, that means they
would have to raise the price by about $8 a copy, just to cover this
feature, no profit to Red Hat until they sell their 10,376th copy!

NOTE - All following numbers are contrived for the sake of this
example!

But, guess what, Red Hat doesn't sell 10,376 copies of Linux
for Alpha - as best as I can figure, they sell:

Projected Revenues for 1998 $15,000,000

Less commercial Support Contracts $ 1,500,000

Less license fees from Caldera $ 1,000,000

Less license/development fees from Corel $ 500,000

Less fees to include CDs in books $ 1,000,000

Less merchandising (hats/tee shirts/books) $ 250,000
-----------
Total revenue from software sales $10,750,000

Divided by cost of each unit sold Div by 25
(est. to be one-half retail price) -----------

Units sold (all platforms) 430,000

Now what is the breakdown per platform?

Intel/386 95% 408,500 units

Compaq/Digital Alpha 2.5% 10,750 units

Sun SPARC 2.5% 10,750 units

Now, based on my completely made-up numbers, Red Hat would have to
increase the price of Red Hat Linux for Alpha by $23.25 - let's call
it $25 (include a bit of profit for Red Hat).

Would you pay $75 for a copy of Red Hat for Alpha, and do you have
almost 11,000 friends that will do likewise? Every year?

I suspect Red Hat will not spend too much time on this "idea."

BTW, I don't think a company like Compaq/Digital can do anything out
of the ordinary if it involves less than $250,000 in revenue - Think
of the lawyers time to craft the Right-To-Use license, the developers
time to isolate and verify the code they are suppling will work as
intendes, and the contingency support if these packaged libraries
fail and need to be corrected. No, I think Compaq/Digital would
probably need to see something closer to $1,000,000/year to set up
such an arrangement.

Food for thought,
