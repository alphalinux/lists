In response to the welcome remarks of Chris Albertson at 05:42 PM 11/16/98 -0800:
>My Multia has gold sockets also. Just try however to find gold 72pin
>parity RAM. If you do please let us all know where you fond it.
>In my experiance tin SIMMS in gold sockets is not that bad. It you
>run into problems take the RAM out and re-seat it. I'd prefer gold
>too.
  About a month ago, I looked for memory for my first Multia. I found at least 2 suppliers of 72 pin, 8 meg x 36 parity, gold lead simms. The ones I bought were made by Viking. I bought them from a supplier who said he was just down the street from Viking. Using tin simms in gold sockets will usually work for a while. The looser fit of the gold sockets may mean occasional reseating like you say. The galvanic corrosion can take a while, depending on your climate. I want a system that will run for the next 10 years, computing a single math problem, so long term reliability is important to me.
  I don't have the information here on the name of the company I bought from. I will look for it and post it in a couple of days, if you wish. I found them by going to pricewatch.com and searching for prices on true parity simms. I remember that they were one of the more expensive choices, but they were the only ones who knew what I was talking about. The other supplier that I think handles these simms is Crucial Technologies, who are owned by Micron.

  I was referring to true parity simms. With 2 36 bit wide simms, the chipset can generate hamming codes to do error correction. For most PC chipsets, sufficiently fast true parity simms are all you need for error correction.
