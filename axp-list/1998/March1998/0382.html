Offhand I don't know, but the key question to ask is: "Does the card have
NVRAM".
Basically this means that at hardware boot time you can get at the cards
settings and change the setup, turn on and off various options, etc. On
AlphaBIOS based machines like the LX and SX when th machine first sees the
BIOS of the SCSI card it gives you a message to "Press Ctrl-C for setup" or
similar.
I have used the Intraserver and Diamond versions of the 875 chip based
cards with good success. The Intraserver, whil slightly more expensive has
excellent support from their technical people, and they also do driver
support for Linux, DEC UNIX, VMS, and clustering. Diamond, OTOH, make a
more straightforward card with basically Wintel support. Still, the cards
work well, and you should expect to get them for a very reasonable price.
Generally in tests I have done and seen the 875 usually fares about 20-40%
faster than the Adaptec. Further, the auto-termination on the Diamond works
well, whereas on the Adaptec it's a risky business. Only bitch I have with
the Diamond is the fact that the external port is narrow SCSI, so if you
want to use wide external devices you arew forced to loop your internal
cable to the back of the machine to provide another port with wide connector.
The Intraserver does not do auto-termination, but uses jumpers instead.
Personally I like this route better as I like "knowing" what the situation
is with regards to termination. I never really trust auto-termination schemes.
Finally, the card I like the very best is the Intraserver 4140 series.
These have onboard 10/100 ethernet, provided through the use of a DEC
21140AF ethernet chip. You can also get a dual-SCSI and a dual-ethernet
version (or both). The only drawback of this card is the fact that it is
fairly large, possibly limiting it for slot location selection on some
machines, depending on their case configuration..
