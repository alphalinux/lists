"Naturally"? That is why you have 'dd' on your system.

> and I can't find any VMS-type
> filesystem support in the kernel. Even if I could, I'm not sure that
> VMS uses tar/cpio or something else that I could use under Unix to
> extract the data.

Tapes usually do not have any file system structure. There could be
some information on this tape which will allow some utilily (say, cpio)
to split data inteligently into files, but in general you may consider
them to be a string of bytes. The fact that you say "large" may be
to your advantage as it may mean that metadata are relatively absent
and most of the stuff on the tape is what you would like to see.

It is not likely that this tape was written in any format (tar, cpio,
dump,....) used on Unix. It is probably BACKUP (VMS) tape. Moreover VMS
is using a few different file formats but quite likely you are dealing
with a bunch of fixed length records from where you want to remove padding
and put on separate lines. 'dd' has some options which may help to deal
with that but funky VMS stuff may be quite, ahem, "interesting".

It is difficult to read tape if you do not know anything about
parameters with which it was written (density, blocking factor, etc.)
Grab manuals for 'dd' and 'mt', read carefuly, and start experimenting.
If you are patient enough it is pretty likely that you should be able
at least to get a raw tape contents. It may take a while. :-)
