On Tue, 12 May 1998, Matthew Woodworth wrote:
> The Linux machine can ping, telnet, etc to the NT machine but it can't seem
> to get out of my home network.
>
> I am new to routing but I have a few ideas. (please excuse them if I am
> on the wrong track)
>
> 1. Because my home network has IP addresses in the 10.*.*.* network, any
> router
> will simply ignore/drop the packets on the way back. In other words, my
> packets
> are making it out to the target machines but they can't get back.

Correct. The 10.* addresses are not routable on the Internet by
convention. This makes it easy for routers to distinguish external hosts
from internal hosts.

> My questions are:
>
> Can this be done (connecting a home network with 10.*.*.* addresses)? Do I
>
> need "real" IP addresses for my home network (I can get them for free). If
>
> I get them, will simply changing the IP addresses and routing tables fix
> the
> problem?

You can get a block of "legal" addresses for your network, and
communicate with the Internet if your NT machine is designated as the
gateway. NT is operating as a router in this scenario.

However... I am doing sort of the opposite. My Linux (164SX) box is the
Internet gateway for my NT workstation. NT has a private address and can
access the Internet through IP masquerading. This is easier to configure
and does not require a block of routable addresses.

Linux is really much more capable than NT as an Internet gateway. You
might consider dialing out from your UDB with pppd rather than NT RAS.
