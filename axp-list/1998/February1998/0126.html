Jeff,

I'm new to the DEC Multia and certainly no authority, but this is what the
folks at Starship tell me regarding heat. If you have one of those tiny 2.5
inch HDs that fit under the floppy drive you are probably OK heat-wise, but
they advise against installing a 3.5 inch HD in the Multia box (talking DEC
Alpha Multia 166 MHz VX40 boxes here) contrary to what DEC shows in their
"Multia MultiClient Desktop Service Information" (Part Number: EK-MULTS-IN.
C01, October 1995), which you can download at
http://www.starshipcorp.com/udbman.pdf.

According to Starship, "the Alpha chip runs very hot in normal
applications. There is only a small fan cooling the chip, and any normal
3.5" [hard disk] drive will add far more heat in the box than a 2.5" drive
will. The hotter the Alpha chip cooks, the slower it runs and the shorter
it lives." [This (and the following quote), from the Starship Coupon Book,
p 4-1.]

They add, "there is only a 75 Watt power supply in the machine. The Alpha
chip alone takes about half of that power, leaving very7 little for the
floppy disk and the rest of the board, much less powering a juice hungry
3.5" [hard drive]. And if drives are run on less power than they need, the
tend to start malfunctioning."

They conclude the, because (1) the 340 MB 2.5" HD is too small for a full
load of RH (and also more expensive than a larger 3.5" drive -- i.e.,
Starship will be happy to sell you either) and (2) you need a SCSI CDROM to
install RH 5.0, you should consider an external drive box. A two bay box
will give you room for a HD and a CDROM.

I bought a brand new (empty) DEC 4-bay case w/200 W power supply (holds
3.5" or 5.25" half heights) units that Starship sells. They also sell SCSI
cables with the special sub miniature 50-pin connector you need to attach
external SCSI devices to a Multia. The DEC drive box uses standard 50-pin
Centronics connectors.

One way around the connector problem (assuming you aren't going to install
a card in the single PCI bus slot) would be to find a slot cover with
50-pin Centronix and ribbon cable to standard internal 50 pin connector,
install it in place of the slot cover, unplug the ribbon cable going to the
sub mini connector and plug your new cable in its place.

If you stuck with this ramble, here's THE IMPORTANT INFORMATION. Assuming
you follow Starship's advice and use EXTERNAL SCSI devices, you should be
able to run you Multia around the clock. There is another important
consideration, however. Any Unix system that is supposed to be doing useful
unattended task in the wee hours ought to be protected by being run off of
a UPS (Uninterruptable Power Supply.)

APS sell adequate units for under $100 at the local computer super store of
your choice. The thing to remember is that the UPS has a relatively short
(~20 minute) hold up time. It's real purpose is to keep your system (box
and bay, forget the monitor) up long enough for a gracefull shutdown. APS
units have a (?) DB-15 connector on the back that lets you cable to a
serial port on the Multia and notify Unix that the power is down. Although
APS software (most intended for MAC and WinTel machines) probably won't
help you, there are all kinds of daemons available for the free Un*x's that
can not only gracefully shutdown your Multia but also can alert other
machines on a LAN, even dial a beeper to wake you up. A little too much for
me, but then I am just teaching myself Unix by playing with different
flavors of it (NetBSD Alpha, RedHat 5.0, and -- when I can get it --
FreeBSD) on my three Multia boxes. All three incidently use drives in an
external DEC 4-bay box.

Hope this helps...
