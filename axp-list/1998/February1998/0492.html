Alan Wood wrote:
>
> Marc,
>
> Something that I have noticed with the 10pin serial connectors (on Generic PC
> mobos,) is that there are 2 ideas (that I have noticed) about the numbering of them
>
> 1 2 1 6
> 3 4 2 7
> 5 6 3 8
> 7 8 4 9
> 9 10 5 10
>
> it is also possible that someone uses 1 10, 2 9, ......., 5 6 (i.e. anti-clockwise
> numbering)
>
> Until I sat down to check that the connectors that I had were working, I had not
> even considered this possibility. Now I know why I could not get those spare
> connectors to work in that machine !!! When I found a correct cable, all was
> working again.
>
> Therefore, it is easiest to only use the cables that came with the board, or make
> ones that match, but always make sure you know how it is numbered ;-) It looks
> like your board has the other scheme to the one you expected.
>
> BTW are 6 AND 8 on the mobo connected to 8, or is 8 (mobo) connected to 9 ?

The correct pinout is the one in the staggered display below. The
vertical is in error. Sharp eyes! I was so happy that I became sloppy,
and only got half of it right.

This staggered arrangement is a better way to display it anyway ;-)

> The ribbon cable looks like this on the back of the DB9
>
> 9 7 5 3 1
> 8 6 4 2
>
> where the back of the DB9 is again numbered like this:
>
> 5 4 3 2 1
> 6 7 8 9

This is the correct arrangement:
PC noname
1 1
2 9
3 2
4 8
5 3
6 7
7 4
8 6
9 5
10 no connection

Others have pointed out that this pinout is not unique to Digital.
Whether or not Digital invented it, will remain unknown until
someone can come up with an x86 board that predates the noname
and shares the pinout. I admit, it does seem easier to make a
ribbon cable mate to DB9 in this fashion. The split and overlap
form is more of a pain to handle.
