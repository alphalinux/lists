OK, lets try this. Compiling has nothing to very little to do with
floating point, and the Alpha is usually best for floating point. Go
figure that.

> the compiler has to do more work to generate and optimise them - and
> what with the Alpha actually having some registers (unlike x86) it has
> to do more work optimising the use of them.

Ah yes, the register count. Optimizing the registers is an n-p complete
problem. When more registers are added (usually doubled, as in binary
math), the number of possibilities to try (if anything were to try them
all) goes up significantly. One can see factorial-, exponential-,
geometrical-like increases in time.

We must also keep in mind that these Alphas in the UDB are from a time
when 66MHz was considered pretty good, then Digital rolled out the then
new Alpha at 150 and 166Mhz speeds.
