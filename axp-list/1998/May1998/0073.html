With regards to your message at 11:51 AM 05-08-98 -0500, Wes Bauske. Where
you stated:
> Bottle necks are relative. What applications are you running on your
>desktop that need more than 7MB/sec? The average UW disk is
>only good for around 8MB/sec. So, you only lose a MB/sec.
>I've used IBM/Quantum/Micropolis/WD/Seagate drives and only
>the very latest HH 9GB and higher drives seriously exceed the speed
>of a 2940. Servers are a different story. I use multi-GB files and
>can easily live with 7MB/sec. Last one I messed with was 12GB
>and if you do the math, it only takes half an hour to read it in at
>7MB/sec. Assuming you do FP calculations on that data, it's not
>hard to come up with half an hour of computation. Course what I
>do actually combines the data into a much smaller output volume.
>But, even so, I doubt many people are screwing with large files,
>particularly since it's only been supported since about 2.1.92 so
>all you're looking at for most folks is 2GB, tops which is about
>5 minutes to read, and another 5 minutes to write.

Interesting. I have run Symbios53C875 based controllers (Intraserver 3140U)
on LX533/600 and on PC164-500 machines and got a sustained 14MB/sec into a
RAID array. On narrow/FAST SCSI disks I got about 2.4MB/sec, on wide 3.5,
and on Ultra/Wide of 6.5.
This is with large transfers, big enough to overflow any memory buffers or
disk caches.
On the other hand I got about 30 to 50% less on Adaptec 2940UW, and 50 to
65% less on 2940.

>As to whether Alpha is a desktop box. If that's not what DEC intended
>for the 164SX, it would truly surprise me. If you've listened to this
>mailing list, there is a strong interest in office suites and browsers.
>I too would like both to be available. As it is, using em86 is an option,
>but it sounds like serious performance degradation. Now you have
>to say THAT is a CPU bottleneck.... even on an Alpha.

Yes, you are right.
Personally I think the SX is a waste of time. Especially one with IDE disks.
As for office software, it will happen IF there are enough users.
To accomplish that there needs to be a form of user registration to prove
the numbers, which would require support and co-operation of the Alpha
vendors.
Secondly there would need to be a solid (stock) distribution of Linux for
it.
As this does not now exist, I can only hope for big improvements in Red Hat
5.1 and other supposedly imminent releases of Alpha Linux.
