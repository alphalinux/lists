> IPv4 address is really a 32-bit long unsigned number. It is divided
> into four octets, separated by dots, simply for a better readability
> by humans. :-) Netmask splits your address into a "fixed" (all those
> bits in your address which have a bit "on" in your netmask) and
> "movable" parts. If you 'and' your machine address with a netmask
> you should get your network address. Replacing in this address all
> those bits which in netmask are 0 with 1's will give you your broadcast
> address. Other combinations of bits in the "movable" part provide
> valid addresses for hosts on your subnetwork. It follows from that
> that a minimal subnetwork may have four addresses (two bits, one
> network address, one broadcast and two hosts) and that you may split
> a C class address into 28 subnetworks of that kind wasting 198
> host addresses in the process.
>
> Of course nothing says that all your subnetworks have to be of the same
> size. :-)
