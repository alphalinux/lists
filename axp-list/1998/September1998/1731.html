On Fri, 16 Oct 1998, Christopher Curtis wrote:

> The PAS is an awesome card and I still use it today. Not only can you
> slap two of these things together, but it has (rudimentary) SCSI (good for
> an old CD-ROM or maybe tape), a 48k sample rate, and excellent sheilding
> (granted, it's no Turtle Beach, but still better than many cards being
> produced today). Do watch out that you don't fry your amp though, as it
> has a 4 watt per channel preamp output.

Thanks for sticking up for the board. Yes, it takes resources, but it
does give many benefits. I don't remember the 48k sample rate, but if it
works, more power.

PAS 16 units have dual sound chips - a benefit is that it can be set up as
full duplex, among the first if its kind. It also (at least mine) has
dual line-ins (plus an additional mic input!) which was useful for me. I
had no problem using my card in my Miata, in parallel with the built-in
ESS sound chip. The NT driver is old and possibly designed for 3.5, so
that is a bit buggy. If you want to save resources, use only PAS 16, not
SB emulation as the last person suggested.

> > many system resources the PAS consumed I gave up on them. So did many
> > others apparently, as the PAS and other products from MediaVision are now
> > "historic".
>
> MV went chapter 11 some years ago and never reentered the consumer market.
> They had the first 3D sound card and a nice video capture board that's
> basic but decent that you can find for about $50. The PAS is still a fine
> card, and you shouldn't be afraid to use it. However, there's no need to
> use the onboard SB chip. Just don't bother.

I have a PAS variation called the 'Memphis'. It is basicly a multimedia
station - it had an external base that housed the SCSI CD-ROM, RCA line in
and out, DIN MIDI jacks and detachable BOSE speakers. I still won't give
it up for the original price - 400$ five or six YEARS ago. And the CD-ROM
is dead - blast NEC crap - those things know when the warranty expires
and promptly mess up after that it seems.

My only frustration is that it's hardware is *slightly* different from the
plain PAS16 so the Linux PAS 16 SCSI driver *never* worked for me and I
don't know how to fix that. So it just locks my system when I tried to
mount an attached drive.

The Media Vision team went to work under the company name 'Aureal', which
they make A3D chips and standards. Their newest (DSP) sound mixing chip
is capable of 300 MIPS I think. I wonder if anyone's tried to make Linux
drivers or programs.

A different company bought the 'MV' name, but I don't know if they ever
did it justice.

As for true Sound Blaster - I've never touched them. Creative won't back
them up with a warranty longer than 90 days. Most people don't have any
trouble with the cards, but if they were truly any good, 1 year wouldn't
hurt them any. I had a Creative CDROM that wouldn't spin down after 2
hours of idle time, so I thought that the warranty wasn't going to help me
if the thing died, promptly returning them.
