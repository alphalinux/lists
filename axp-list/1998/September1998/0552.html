A van Kessel wrote:
> The #defines exist to hide the magic constants behind symbolic names.
> Once compiled the symbolic names disappear. That is no big deal:
> once compiled (for a specific platform) the exact values are correct
> *for that platform* .

Right, and in this case the idea is that the resulting binaries are
compatible with both Linux and OSF/1 without having to munge OSF/1
binaries to the Linux format. I agree that this is a good idea.

I'm not concerned that Linux/Alpha attempts to be OSF/1 compatible,
I'm concerned that Linux/Intel has signals that Linux/Alpha does not.
As things currently stand, Joe Programmer not only has to do an
#ifdef LINUX but also an #ifdef ALPHA; that is this particular anomaly
is requiring developers to not only specifically handle Linux, but to
specifically handle Linux/Alpha, and Linux/Intel, and maybe Linux/SPARC,
etc. Yeah, the #defines give you the right numbers *for the platform*,
but if the signal isn't even present in signum.h, you can't get a
platform-specific number for it.

Perhaps I misrepresented myself a bit; I'm by no means saying that
OSF/1 compatibility is bad. I'm just saying that IMHO it's worth
considering working toward signal compatibility with Linux/Intel,
since that's where the biggest things are happening. Especially since
this particular issue can probably be solved very easily by increasing
_NSIG and adding in the missing Linux/Intel signals. (And, of course,
perhaps recompiling anything that relies on _NSIG, but this could be
a change migrated into a future release.)

Again, this is a fairly arcane issue, but any time we add to the work
that developers must do to port to our platform, we're hurting ourselves.
