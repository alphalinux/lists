Paul,

pjlahaie@atlsci.com said:
> That number seems quite high. I remember looking at SCO Unix for
> ~8k (roughly the same price point as Novell NetWare) back in the
> mid-80s. Also available at the time was Dell UNIX and several other
> SysV Unix systems. Perhaps the source license was $160k.

When *I* started with Unix, and when I tried to buy it for my school in 1977,
source was all that was available. It was the commercial Unix companies:

o Sun
o Digital
o HP
o IBM
o SCO

that brought out the binary-only distributions at "reasonable" prices.

> The reason I recall this is that, at the time, a small canadian
> company had a DOs/Unixy OS that was "unlimited" user and sold for
> less than half of a commercial UNIX. At around $3k, QNX was still
> quite expensive.

I think you will find current-day Unix systems less expensive than $3K,
particularly for the desktop. But unlimited users, compilers, the complexity
of servers and low volumes (compared to MS products) etc. raise the price.

> Except you can't get Win32 branding for you OS, even if you did
> pass all the APIs properly. Instead you'd probably burdened with a
> bunch of lawsuits. In that regard, Unix is pretty "Open".

Granted, and that is one reason why I stayed with Unix all these years.
