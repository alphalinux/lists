36 bit doesn't mean parity. Some ram is sold as parity but it has a non-ram chip
that fools some computers into thinking the ram is parity and is good. A computer
that really tests the parity isn't fooled and doesn't boot. I've heard the fake
parity called logic parity but to me it's just a scam because it costs the same as
parity and often the fake chip has markings of a ram chip. I get burned by that all
the time. I ask for true parity and get that crap and have to send it back. They
then say it's good and I have to explain all this to them.

24 chips might be a form of ECC memory. I recall only 20 chips but there might be
more depending on what chips they use I guess. ECC will not work as parity but will
work as standard ram in most cases.

>
>
> - 8 1x4Mbit (or 4x1Mbit) chips on side A (4MB, no parity)
> - 8 1x4Mbit (or 4x1Mbit) chips on side B (4MB, no parity)
> - 4 1x1Mbit chips on side A (one-half of the 8Mbit (1MByte) for parity)
> - 4 1x1Mbit chips on side B (the other half of the 8Mbit (1MByte) for parity)
>
> So, if you look closely, you should see that four of the chips on each side
> actually have a different part number. This should still be just fine,
> as it works out to 8MB with parity.
>
> Moreover, some people said you needed ECC memory instead of parity. That's
> bogus. As far as I know, there is no such thing as ECC memory in 72pin
> format--use parity (36-bit memory as it's advertized), and let the Alpha
> use it as a 72 bit data path, generating 8 bits of ECC for each 64 bits
> of data.

There is ECC memory in 72 pin format. Some MicroVAX's use it. The Multia doesn't
use ECC though. Ask for true parity and make sure they know what you mean by that.
