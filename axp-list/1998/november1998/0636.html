On Thu, 26 Nov 1998, B. James Phillippe wrote:

> > This is a hardware problem rather than an X problem. I have an ZLXp-E2 as
> > well running on an LX164, under Digital Unix, though. The problem is that
> > only the R and B signals are terminated with 50 Ohms on the graphics card.
> > The G signal has high impedance! Just solder a 50 Ohm resistor (47 will do
> > as well) between the G analog output of the card and GND and the shadows
> > will disappear.
>
> Hey, that might be it. I'm sure hoping you're right, because that's an
> easy fix. :) Say, how did you confirm this, and where exactly did you
> solder a resister?

I did confirm this be doing it myself :-) I mean: I had the same problem.
I even posted it here and no one could help me. Then I took the card
and measured the resistance between R-GND, G-GND and B-GND and only
R-GND and B-GND had 50 Ohms as it should be. G-GND was high impedance.
You might measure the resistance as well before starting to solder
around on your card.

Alternatively if you have a metal SVGA connector which can be opened,
you could open the connector and try to solder the resistor in
there rather than on the card itself.

This is actually what Digital is apparently doing. They sell a
special cable for this board which apparently has the resistor built
in and "magically" makes the image look better!

> On the card itself, or between pins 2 and... wait,
> which ground? My pin assignment diagram shows three possibilities:
>
> 5 - ground
> 7 - green ground
> 10 - shield ground

Between G and "green ground" of course. Although all of these
grounds are the same on the card.

> Second question: there is "tga_sync_green" option in my SRM console. One
> of the trouble-shooting sections in the handbook says to use this option to
> solve wavery/unstable image problems "for three-wire monitors". What is a
> three-wire monitor? Perhaps I should try this option and see if it makes a
> difference?

A three wire monitor is a monitor with Sync-in-Green. These are usually
older workstation monitors (like the DEC VRT19-HA 19") which require
the H and V sync signals on the green video line.

Most modern SVGA monitors have separate sync signals. Some can even
work with both, separate sync and Sync in Green.

BTW: I was mistaken with the resistor value: I think it is 75 OHM and
not 50OHM as I said in my previous e-mail. 50 OHM is the Ethernet
cable impedance and 75OHM is the video cable impedance.

So try to measure R-GND and you could see 75 OHMs (with the monitor
disconnected, of course). Then solder 75 OHMs as well between G and GND.

Hope this helps // Tom
