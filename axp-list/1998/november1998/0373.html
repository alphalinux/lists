I once supported an application that told users to allocate 2 Gigabytes
of
swap on their Sun server - the server itself had 512 Megabytes of RAM.
Well, one day I got a call from a client that wanted to know why my
company
was telling him to allocate a 2 GB swap file? I went to the developers
and asked them why they needed that much swap, and the short answer was,
"the common rule of thumb for swap space is 2x actual RAM, 3x for busy
server applications, and since this was a *really* important application
they increased it to 4x actual RAM." Neither the client nor I liked
that
answer, and the client went with 1x actual RAM and never had a
problem...

The reason the client called was becuase the Sun Field Engineer that was
installing and configuring their RAID hardware couldn't understand why
any application needed 2 gigabytes of swap - in all his years with Sun
he
had never seen such a thing outside of incredible high-end
applications...
The developers, it seems, had a high opinion of their code...

What the developers never realized was that this "rule of thumb" was
invented when a big server had 128 Meg of RAM!

Thought this might be interesting to some...
