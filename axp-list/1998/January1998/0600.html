>At this time it
>looks at though the egcs movement might become something like XEmacs
>and Emacs, GNU won't accept them.

I'm not an expert on the XEmacs/Emacs issue, but from what I've
read and recall on the topic, I'd say it is characterized by a
notably greater "divide" between the FSF (the organization that
officially runs Project GNU) and the XEmacs people than is
characterized by the gcc/egcs situation.

There are some similarities in that the two pairs of project
share similarly differing priorities and models of development.

However, I believe XEmacs put certain things the FSF considered
crucial at way too low of a priority -- perhaps including
maintaining good tty (non-X) behavior and getting copyrights
of non-trivial submissions signed over to the FSF -- for the FSF
to feel comfortable trying to work very hard to prevent a long-term
split between the projects.

I'm not aware of any such "drops" of important priority in
the egcs project. Some changes in priority are evident, of course,
but the biggest differences I've seen are often in the
*execution* of various aspects of the project.

At this point I don't believe the egcs' project team
priorities are unacceptable to the FSF (e.g. I think they're
insisting on copyright signovers for the FSF as does gcc2),
though they might not entirely fit the FSF's ideas of
priorities. (That's okay; it's rare to find *any* GNU
project being undertaken using the exact same priority list
that the FSF wants, I would think. The FSF is not in a
position to insist on conformity, although it tries to
persuade its people using verbage that can seem as if it
is trying to require conformity.)

Nor do I believe the egcs' project team's clearly superior execution
in at least some areas of the development and release process
(e.g. creating and distributing integrating test suites for
various languages, making snapshots and CVS access publically
available, providing various forms of distribution tailored to
common user needs, rapidly responding to and integrating submitted
patches) is likely to provide the FSF with any reason to insist
on keeping its gcc distinct from egcs.

One thing that is unclear at this point is just how well gcc2 will
keep up with egcs in terms of incorporating patches from egcs.

The difference in patch-acceptance rates is fairly astonishing
to me, as a casual observer of egcs with a fair amount of
experience on gcc2. E.g. I could submit patches, or technical
questions, to gcc2 and wait what seemed like ages for any response
or actual incorporation of the patch (by which time I might be
no longer well-suited to respond to queries accompanying
rejections). On egcs, I've submitted basically no patches, and
yet had lots of patches I made to *g77* quickly and happily picked
up by the egcs maintainer. (This was during the initial phase
of egcs development, so it could have been a "honeymoon" period
in both technical and psychological ways, but the happy attitude
on the part of the egcs maintainer persists to this day.)

So gcc2 "merging" into the egcs development process (by picking up
egcs patches as they're deemed "proven" in the field) might be roughly
as challenging as a horse-drawn buggy trying to merge into
a German autobahn with lots of traffic going at least 150kph.

Another concern, more overtly suggested by the official "press
releases" by the gcc2 and egcs teams, is that egcs' rapid acceptance
of submitted patches and willingness to try out "experimental"
features will make it more buggy.

Only time, and a voracious appetite on the part of the gcc/egcs
user base, will tell for sure. Certainly if egcs has more bugs
per release, that might be a problem. However, if it has, on
average, 5 times as many bugs, but bug-fix patches are accepted
10 times as fast, on the whole, this might appear to users
(especially of more "stable" systems that are less affected
by the "experimental" features) as if egcs is less buggy overall.

(Note that some of these "experimental" features were years old
and already well-proven in the field, however. Most of what
is truly "experimental" about egcs is the development *process*
and packaging, I think, not the code and algorithms being accepted
for submission.)

It is certainly an interesting, and somewhat challenging, time
for gcc developers and users, but I do think there will be more
clarity and hope by mid-1998 than there was in mid-1997. That
is just a prediction on my part, however.
