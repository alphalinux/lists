Ding!

>INTEL DOES NOT OWN ALPHA!

Correct.

>They purchased the chip foundry (for a tidy sum) and licensed the right to
>use the technology.

Sort of. They essentially paid 'book value' for the facilities (the
disclosed $700M), and have signed some other complicated agreements
for licensing the technology, guaranteed chip supplies, tier 1 status,
and probably some other stuff. The details of the other parts of the
deal haven't been made public, and probably won't ever be.
My understanding of Intel's Alpha involvement is that they are required
to manufacture Alpha chips and sell them to Digital, and solely to Digital.
The deal covers things like the process roadmap, availability, etc.
Digital, however, has the right to purchase Alpha chips from anyone,
including Samsung and Mitsubishi.

>As far 8400 system in Texas with 8 of these beasties in it.
>Now if the part is being produced by Mitsubishi (the 'PC' chip),
>then they may have decided to not go into production with the chip.

Uh, 8400's use the 21164 chip, not the 21164PC chip. Mitsubishi
and Digital Semiconductor jointly developed the 21164PC, and Mitsubishi
has the rights to build and sell the 21164PC. Samsung might also,
but I'm not sure of that.

>It is sadly true, that Digital is out of the semiconductor manufacturing
>business, but not out of the semiconductor development business. With
>access to multiple foundries, Intel, Mitsubishi, Samsung etc., they will
>just have to farm out the production of their designs. It could work out
>much to their favor (and subsequently to ours).

Correct.
