> Next, we need to address one little detail:
> A 68 pin SCSI connector means a wide (16 bit) SCSI bus. Now, think of wide
> SCSI as simply 2 narrow(8-bit) buses in the same connection. The problem we
> have to deal with is to ensure that if you connect only narrow devices on
> this external connector you need to disable any termination on the low
> 8-bit portion, and make sure it stays enabled on the high 8-bit half.

Ah... I see. The ZIP drive would be the only external device, and the
only other external SCSI device I'd ever be likely to add is perhaps a
scanner, so I would need to pay attention to that.

> Attaching narrow devices to wide bus connections is not a problem, as long
> as you make sure the termination is deal with.

Yeah, the only reason I haven't so far is because I just haven't been able

While we're on the subject of SCSI, here's one for you:
My HDD is a Seagate ST34371W (3.5", 4371MB, Ultra Wide) disk. The card
is, again, based on NCR53C875.

The device driver for this beast on 2.0.35 seems to only run the card in
8bit (just "FAST-10", I guess?), i.e. 20MB/s, mode. (At least by default;
there may be an option to turn on FAST-20.) The 2.1.131 device driver,
however, runs the card in 16 bit FAST-20 mode.

However, `hdparm -tT /dev/sda` shows no substantial difference in reads
between the device drivers, and comes out SUBSTANTIALLY slower than my
friend's Ultra DMA EIDE drive on a Pentium II 450MHz box. (my ~9 MB/s
"buffered disk read" vs. his ~18MB/s.) I would expect the disk to show at
least a modest increase in speed in 2.1.131, and I would expect the
discrepancy between UW SCSI and Ultra DMA to be smaller.

I have no trouble believing that Ultra DMA is faster than UW SCSI, but
THAT much faster? Does hdparm just not understand SCSI drives properly?
Am I hitting a CPU or bus speed wall of the SX164? Is there another way
to check the speed of my disk, or is that value actually accurate?
