I got my 'free' Multia this week and got a copy of RH6.0 for alpha in an
attempt to get this, my first non-Intel system running. I have run into
some unexpected problems, and those may be because of my naivity or
ignorance of what I was getting myself into, but I am sure I am not the
only one and I hope this is the right place to ask questions about it.
I have just joined this list, but didn't see any mention of an FAQ, so
if there is any (apart from the normal HOWTOs) I would like to know.

I have been using Linux since the SLS (pre-Slackware) distribution and
some 0.99 kernel so I may be a newbie to Alpha, but not in general :-)

I got my UDB from eBiz (www.thelinuxstore.com) as you may have guessed
and I ordered it to come with 32MB RAM and an IDE HD, and I got the
FD from Starship Computer. As I have a PS/2 keyboard and mouse and a
SVGA capable monitor I thought everything would be fairly easy at least
with the hardware, but it doesn't seem to be.

1. I am yet not able to find an IDE cable to connect the HD (2.5" Seagate
   ST91685AG), which seems to be very special as it needs a 44pin
   female-female cable that also carries the power-supply to connect to
   the motherboard. Unfortunately, eBiz didn't have such a cable and I
   didn't know it would be such a pain to find. The HD doesn't have the
   normal power-supply connector which makes it impossible to fiddle with
   44pin -> 40pin -> 44pin adapters and such. I don't even know where to
   get the connectors as spareparts because all the shops I went to
   almost balked when I mentioned something that didn't include the two
   letters "P" and "C".

If you have a clue as to what to do here I would really appreciate it.

I was able to start up the boot process with the three floppy disks
described and I intend to install by NFS mounting the CDROM from one of
my other machines. If you know of any quirks or something I should be
careful about, please don't hesitate to tell me. Even RH installation
is a first as I have always used Slackware, and I can't imagine a
distribution easier and more flexible ;-)

Thank you very much,

Peter
