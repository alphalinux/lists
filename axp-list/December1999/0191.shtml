Tony,

> Speed (MHz) is not everything, Having a stable system that is fast is
> much more important to me. Originally the speed of the Alphas attracted
> me, but now it is much more the stability factor. This goes as much for
> servers that I install as my own workstations.

Now that's interesting. I thought the Intel platforms were stable.
That's why the NASA Beowulf clusters are on Intel-based machines and not
Alpha-based machines.

> If you want to be a sheep, go ahead, add to the un-informed masses.

HA HA HA!! :-) I'm just looking for information to support an Alpha over
Intel argument. I can't really think of one now that COTS (Common off the
shelf) Intel-based PCs are so cheap and stable. Have you seen the new HP
Brio? A Celeron 450 based system for $500.00. Not bad for a mass
produced, for the sheep, style machine, huh?

> Personally, I have no desire to have "inhell inside" stuck to anything I
> own. I will go out of my way to avoid the brain dead architecture that
> still has backward compatibility to the 4004.

Though I basically understand what you are saying . . . the Alpha is a
cleaner, faster design than any Intel based legacy system. However, the
herd is going cheap, Intel based hardware. You notice Linux is home-grown
on Intel. It must be ported to Alpha processors.

Don't get me wrong . . . I do not have any allegence to any single
processor or OS. However, I do like Open Source. I think this is the
way of the future in computer science. Thus, I like Linux and the xBSD's
as well as Alpha, Intel, PA-RISC, and UltraSPARC based hardware. Heck, I
even like the positively ancient PDP-11 systems just to tinker with.

So . . . why Alpha? What are your arguments for Alpha and/or against
Intel?

Paul
