On Thu, 16 Dec 1999, warp@xs4all.nl wrote:

> A better question would be 'what you want to do with it?'
> Linux, FreeBSD, NetBSD and OpenBSD are all fine, stable OSi.

To sum up, from a Slashdot post and personal experience:

Linux: jack of all trades. Supports a lot of hardware, does a lot of
things, many (but not all) of them well. The Leatherman of OS's.

NetBSD: runs on anything in the way of old, scary hardware. For that old
machine you always thought you could use, but didn't have an OS for.

FreeBSD: stable, fast, runs on i386 and ??? Other ports exist but I'm not
sure of their status.

OpenBSD: Paranoid about security or need a good firewall? Run OpenBSD.
You don't have to work hard to make it really secure.

>From my experience, the *BSD's have a smaller and tidier default install.
You can add more packages if you want, but you don't get everything and
the kitchen sink whether you like it or not. And if you need to hack
around under the surface (i.e., edit init scripts, etc.) it's pretty
straightforward and standard, both internally and between *BSD variants.
        OTOH, Linux distros have a lot more tools and goodies by default
in case you need to roll a solution to a special problem. And it's nice
sometimes to just select a high-level wad of packages and say "install",
rather than use /usr/ports on *BSD.

Of course, I haven't used all of these as much as some peope have, so jump
in if you have more/better info.

        -Joel
