> > Intel Celeron 400 - 822 seconds == 9 million cell updates/sec
> > Alpha 164LX 533 - 316 seconds == 25 million cell updates/sec
> > Alpha 264EV67 667 - 164 seconds == 46 million cell updates/sec
> >
> > Alphas are good, very very good.
>
> Let's go thru the math on this. If one can buy a 400mhz Celeron
> for 500 quid or less, and an XP1000 for 8000 quid, (which sounds high)
> then one gets at least 16 Celerons for that XP1000. 9x16=144 million
> cell updates per second vs 46M updates. Now, I'd be curious where one
> can purchase those 533LX's at 1100 quid. In the US, 533LX's go

We got ours from Compusys, ex VAT of course. The price will vary
depending on the spec of what you get of course, I recommend going for the
lowest possible and upgrading it yourself with off the shelf parts. For
instance, we put a 20GB IDE drive in each of our machines for 170 quid
each, could probably do better now of course.

> for around US$1995, approximately 1230 quid. Even so, I think one
> could put together a Celeron for much less than 500 quid. A 400mhz
> Celeron and motherboard go for $120. One can add a disk/case/memory
> and have a reasonable system for less than $500. So, given US prices,
> one can buy 4 Celerons for the price of a 533LX, 9*4=36M updates/sec
> vs 25M on a 21164. Worst case, using your 533LX price, you still
> might be able to get 4 Celerons, depending on memory/disk you buy,
> or maybe 3 which makes the 21164 a wash vs Celeron in price/performance.

Probably although you would then need to go to the effort of writing an
MPI version of the code which would likely lose you a little performance,
I doubt that a 4 celeron system would be much faster if at all then a
single 533LX in fact.
 
> There are reasons to use Alphas.

There sure are, for the same algorithm coded for the 64 bit architecture
we are now hitting 182 million on the 164LX compared with the 9 million
the celeron 400 gets. One alpha is a heck of a lot cheaper than 20
celeron boxes but that isn't really what I was saying, I wanted to show
that the same program on an Alpha gets you better performance clock for
clock for integer work which people seem to ignore. It is accepted that
for FP the alpha would be better, why not integer?

> However, they may be cost effective for codes that need VERY high
> memory bandwidth or for codes that can't easily be parallelized
> and you have the $$$$ to spend.

True. Of course, our code can be relatively easily parallelised in which
case the Alpha pulls ahead nicely as for every one we buy we would need 20
of the celeron boxes. Not a general case of course but for us they are a
boon. We can get the same performance from four 164 boxes that we used to
get with a $million supercomputer just four years ago.

Shane
