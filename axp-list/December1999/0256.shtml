> > application will be faster than the same application coded specifically

> Not even, I have a piece of junk code which I use for testing ideas out

> Smith and Waterman search of Swissprot 37 database using a 277 residue
> protein sequence.

> Intel Celeron 400 - 822 seconds == 9 million cell updates/sec
> Alpha 164LX 533 - 316 seconds == 25 million cell updates/sec
> Alpha 264EV67 667 - 164 seconds == 46 million cell updates/sec
>
> Looks like a pretty good win for the Alpha, even if you consider that the
> Intel cost < 500 quid and the Alpha 164 was 1100 it's still better value.
> The 264 is less easy to justify as those systems are priced much higher, I

> boxes would be a better bet for our work. Even assuming you could get an

> around 30% less than the 533 164 and rather shocking 60% down on a 667 264
> box despite the apparent higher clock speed.
>
> Alphas are good, very very good.

Let's go thru the math on this. If one can buy a 400mhz Celeron
for 500 quid or less, and an XP1000 for 8000 quid, (which sounds high)
then one gets at least 16 Celerons for that XP1000. 9x16=144 million
cell updates per second vs 46M updates. Now, I'd be curious where one
can purchase those 533LX's at 1100 quid. In the US, 533LX's go
for around US$1995, approximately 1230 quid. Even so, I think one
could put together a Celeron for much less than 500 quid. A 400mhz
Celeron and motherboard go for $120. One can add a disk/case/memory
and have a reasonable system for less than $500. So, given US prices,
one can buy 4 Celerons for the price of a 533LX, 9*4=36M updates/sec
vs 25M on a 21164. Worst case, using your 533LX price, you still
might be able to get 4 Celerons, depending on memory/disk you buy,
or maybe 3 which makes the 21164 a wash vs Celeron in price/performance.

There are reasons to use Alphas.

I use them because I can't stand the space constraints of using
that many Intels and, of course, Alphas are 64 bit and support
large files, a necessity in my line of work. And, looking at
bang for the buck, DS10's are the way to go, Alpha wise. When
the UP1000 shows up, it might give the DS10 a run for the money.
UP2000/DS20/XP1000 are not as cost effective for most things.
However, they may be cost effective for codes that need VERY high
memory bandwidth or for codes that can't easily be parallelized
and you have the $$$$ to spend.
