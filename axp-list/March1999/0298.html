>Intel showed a 1000 MHz chip, and it's so flustrating to see the superior
>alpha fall/lag behind. How long has the alpha been at 500 MHz and with
>chips that were produced with the .35 micron process???? (imagine them at
>.18?)

No offense young Jedi, but I may have to use some of the logic you are
promoting as an organic fertilizer supplement this summer.
There are numerous flaws in your statement, but I'll pick out the most obvious
(anyone who's had a systems architecture course probably will want to stop
 reading here :-)

1. Faster clock rate is not always better
        Sounding the death dirge for the Alpha architecture because Intel
finally matches the clock rate DEC had achieved two (?) years ago with an x86
is foolish at best.
Architectures are not all created equal. Recall the 50Mhz Motorola 68040
provided as an upgrade to the stock Amiga 4000, which at 50MHz had overall
performance equal to the Pentium at 100MHz or 120MHz. Same could be said
about the SuperSPARC line.
        On the other side of the coin, remember the Exponential (XP) processor
which promised to clean house in the consumer computer market? Their
design at 500Mhz was architecturally inferiour to the current x86 on the
market at the time on every operation (as I recall).
        Quoting clock speed is more a tool of the marketing department
than the engineering dept. As far as architectural simplicity and beauty,
the Alpha has got x86 beat by a long shot.
  
2. Systems are complex, one variable does not determine overall performance
        This is a bit of SGI philosophy I have adopted when evaluating a
system I've never touched before. You see, SGI recognized a long time back
that processor speed is not the limiting bottleneck for most applications.
This also is mentioned in Eric Raymond's now-outdated Linux hardware guide.
By speeding up things like the graphics subsystem, disk subsystem,
memory bus, etc. you are able to enhance overall usability and performance
by a wide margin.

3. An overclocked x86 with nitrogen cooling is still an x86
        Intel has this nasty little habit of darn little variety and
virtually no innovation to speak of. Oh, they add 'multimedia' instructions
once in a blue moon but those are mostly of no consequence to Un*x users
(well, mpg123 can use the MMX/3Dnow! insn set). Intel relies on the
gullibility of the general populus to purchase their processors solely
on the image presented on the tele. Their marketing team and
Hired Chimpanzees In Spacesuits (HCIS at Intel Corp.) are extremely
effective at selling the unsuspecting person a processor which
performs no more than 5% better than the last generation chip,
but retails at 300% the price of the prior product.
