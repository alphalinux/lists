I just ran a disk benchmark:
-------Sequential Output-------- ---Sequential Input-- --Random--
-Per Char- --Block--- -Rewrite-- -Per Char- --Block--- --Seeks---
Machine MB K/sec %CPU K/sec %CPU K/sec %CPU K/sec %CPU K/sec %CPU /sec %CPU
256 3478 29.4 3618 22.0 1678 45.8 2862 76.4 3565 35.0 60.7 0.7

-------Sequential Output-------- ---Sequential Input-- --Random--
-Per Char- --Block--- -Rewrite-- -Per Char- --Block--- --Seeks---
Machine MB K/sec %CPU K/sec %CPU K/sec %CPU K/sec %CPU K/sec %CPU /sec %CPU
256 3662 27.6 3617 22.4 1633 44.4 2913 76.3 3557 35.0 57.9 0.8

System is a microway-533 lx system, quantum fireball 6.4 gb disk.

Not state of the art performance, but not to bad i.e. 3.5 MB/sec read/write
with 22-35% cpu usage. I didn't tweak anything (redhat-4.2 as installed
by Microway).

So yes SCSI may get a good bit better but 3.5 MB/sec isn't to bad.

Here's a similiar number from a Dell Pentium-II 300 with a IBM ultrastar
8.4 gb eide disk:

-------Sequential Output-------- ---Sequential Input-- --Random--
-Per Char- --Block--- -Rewrite-- -Per Char- --Block--- --Seeks---
Machine MB K/sec %CPU K/sec %CPU K/sec %CPU K/sec %CPU K/sec %CPU /sec %CPU
256 5499 94.6 9556 10.1 3309 9.3 5399 88.4 9093 10.7 59.5 0.9

I don't have any way to compare the IBM vs Quantum performance directly though.
I've not seen better performance out of any SCSI single disk solution btw.

In summary yes the incentive for scsi on alpha is much higher then on
an X86 system. For normal desktop use 3.5 MB/sec isn't going to
be a major bottleneck. The more disk intensive (news servers, databases,
large checkpoint files for simulations etc) the more scsi will be worth
the extra cost on alpha.
