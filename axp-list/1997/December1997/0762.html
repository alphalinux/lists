Had a couple of interesting issues:

1. I noticed the system did not seem to be mounting my paging volume,
so I did mkswap /dev/sdb2. mkswap couldn't deduce the size of the
partition, so picked something over 5 Gig [for about a 120 Meg
partition on a 4 Gig drive]. I killed it, but eventually the root
file system evaporated [on /dev/sdb1] and I've done a reinstall.

2. After the reinstall, ypserv and a couple of rpc gobbled up about
90% of the CPU - split around 15-20% user and the rest system. Is
this a known problem? I `fixed' it by removing
/etc/rc.d/rc3.d/S??ypserv, so I'm more interested in this in the
longer term.

3. I use `aboot' to boot the system from the SRM console. It appears
that both the `fdisk partition table' and `bsd compatible disk label'
must be present for this to work. It also requires some aboot-hacking
to clean up the boot on sdb, because aboot only finds fd0, sda, and
scd. Is anyone maintaining `aboot' [grep /usr/src/linux/MAINTAINERS
yields nothing]?

System: Alphastation 255 - two scsi disks - DEC UNIX on sda, RH 4.2 on
sdb. Uses SRM console to boot.

4. The discussion of `speculative execution' was interesting but
vague. I haven't tried to track architecture anywhere near closely
for over 10 years now, so I'm kind of out of touch. I kind of
wandered away during fairly early, but successful RISC development and
very early parallelism. Anyway, microprogrammed cpu's on larger
systems have had parallel evaluation of jump targets for over 20
years, so that is nothing new - except possibly driving down into
smaller CPU's.

I guess my question is more: What does Digital mean by `speculative
execution'? and Is there a standard definition of `speculative
execution' which makes a distinction between precomputing jump targets
or evaluating all possible jump targets in parallel? [I thought
precomputing jump targets was pretty much standard in RISC processors
from way back and should be pretty much standard in everything now -
given that everything has at least some pipelining].

5. Is there any way to capture the stuff dumped to the screen when the
system panics other than by `writing it down'? And, are there any
other magic keys besides `shift scroll lock'?

thanks, in adv.
