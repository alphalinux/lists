> went to there site and read the fine print. I still feel it is the
> developers responsibility to create a viable product before asking for
> money. In other words, perform the work required and then ask for the
> dough. Maybe I came off as cranky or something in my other mail.
[...]
> misread me and thought I was hostile. I merely question anyone who
> expects to be paid before they do the work. That is my "starving student"
> mind-set. I re-iterate is anyone interested in writing a browser for the
> alpha?

Dan,

What the Opera developers have proposed is indeed very unconventional, but
there is nothing inherantly wrong with it. This is their method of
ensuring their success in the scary world of alternative platforms. This
is also the method employed by a lot of people outside the software
industry, such as general contractors and such, where a portion of the
money is fronted in good faith, and a portion of the work completed or all
work completed for final payment. The difference with software is that a
portion of the work is worth nothing, in general. While you may be
opposed to their method, it is (as myself and others would see it) a
invaluable opportunity for us as users to get a truly good product ported
to our operating system. There is always a risk involved in trying to
sell to a minority market, and I think that if the Opera people didn't get
these pledges beforehand, their product on that platform would never
surface. Why should they take that risk? In addition to the code, they
must also pay for maintenance, updates, and support. They obviously know
the Win32x model, but they'll have to learn the PM model, and the Mac
model, and the Motif model. And they're going to have to deal with
potentially quite irate users because their design philosophy is not
seamless with the users' OS. All this costs money, and can be quite
expensive. Why would they take that venture if they can't prove that it
is going to be cost effective to do so? They way I see it, this is the
industry looking to the users and asking, "Hey, what do you want? We're
here _for_ you," not, "We are Microsoft. You will be paperclip Bob."
Again, while you may disagree with buying something that does not exist
yet (I sure hope you aren't as stubborn with other investments) I think it
was an invaluable opportunity for the users of those operating systems to
show that OS/2 and Macintosh have a viable user market, and should not be
abondoned for Windoze. (Not to be Anti-Linux, but that proof never
surfaced.) And isn't that what people complain about most with
'alternate' OSes? Lack of vendor support?

Regards,
Christopher
