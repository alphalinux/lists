>With regards to your message at 12:07 PM 12-06-97 -0500, Craig Burley.
>Where you stated:
[...]
>>I'm *much* happier that only Intel's *chip-making* expertise is
>>getting its hands on Digital's facilities, than if (as I gathered
>>was the claim of the article) Intel's *CPU-designing* expertise
>>got its hands on Digital's Alpha architecture.
>
>But they did. One of the biggest motivating factors for Intel to do the
>deal was to get out from the lawsuits filed against it for stealing Digital
>architecture for Pentium class CPUs. I suspect that some of the technology
>is going to make Merced a lot easier too.

Okay, I see what you're saying. I'm worried much less about
perception ("Intel won, Digital & Alpha lost") and about Intel
being able to use Digital technology than I was about *Intel*
being able to control present and future generations of the *Alpha*
architecture.

And that's what I *thought* the early press report(s) were saying --
that Digital was selling the Alpha *architecture*, not just
a few implementation techniques and some fab space, to Intel.
(At least that seemed to be the spin on the reports I saw here;
maybe that wasn't quite what the press reports were saying?)

In short, I see the current situation as being roughly similar
to the previous in the areas I care about: that there exists
real competition to Intel's Pentium/x86 architecture. I assume
Digital still has real incentive to make future Alphas run fast,
something Intel might not have had had they bought the rights
to the architecture.
