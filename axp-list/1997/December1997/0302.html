With regards to your message at 01:25 PM 12-08-97 -0500, Craig Burley.
Where you stated:
>Okay, I see what you're saying. I'm worried much less about
>perception ("Intel won, Digital & Alpha lost") and about Intel
>being able to use Digital technology than I was about *Intel*
>being able to control present and future generations of the *Alpha*
>architecture.
>
>And that's what I *thought* the early press report(s) were saying --
>that Digital was selling the Alpha *architecture*, not just
>a few implementation techniques and some fab space, to Intel.
>(At least that seemed to be the spin on the reports I saw here;
>maybe that wasn't quite what the press reports were saying?)
>
>In short, I see the current situation as being roughly similar
>to the previous in the areas I care about: that there exists
>real competition to Intel's Pentium/x86 architecture. I assume
>Digital still has real incentive to make future Alphas run fast,
>something Intel might not have had had they bought the rights
>to the architecture.
>--
Yup, but it was still disappointing to see Intel get off as lightly as it
did. Frankly the fact it stole Alpha design cosncepts din't even get
punished. They paid $700 million, but they got Hudson and StrongARM for
that too. Also, if Merced comes even _CLOSE_ to Alpha in performance at any
point, it's a no-brainer to see Digital drop Alpha development. Why bother,
as they have access to Merced under this deal.
HP is in on Merced too, so this leaves the high-performance CPU market
shrinking really quickly now. PA-RISC can't be getting much more from HP,
SPARC is still doing OK, but not in the class of the Alpha designs, and
SGI/MIPS is nearly historic now. The R10000 is not a bad chip, but not even
in the same class as the Alpha.
Motorola and the IBM PowerPC is slowly fading.
And what else? K6 is an x86 clone, and same goes for the Cyrix/National
Semi chips.

I am afraid we're into the "end game" now...
