On Thu, 18 Dec 1997, Dave Wreski wrote:

>
> 1. Using one box for outgoing, and one (or more) for incoming services
> (mail and IMAP, specifically)
>

This might not be a bad idea. A box that simply processes outgoing mail
need not know about mail spools housed on the machine that processes
incoming mail. Therefore, no NFS is required.

> Also, your machine stopping the delivery or processing of mail is due to a
> configuration of sendmail, not an inability of BSD, in case that wasn't
> clear.

Absolutely.

> Is the box currently I/O bound, memory bound, or CPU bound? I realize you
> said CPU bound, but how do you know? Do you have 'iostat' to play with on
> BSD? How about vmstat? I'm not sure that simply because 'top' reports a
> high load average means its strictly CPU bound.

We have sar running under BSD. And yes, we have vmstat and iostat as
well. The machine is certainly I/O bound at times -- the mail spool is on
a rather slow narrow disk. The machine is also frequently CPU bound,
(perl4 is a real hog). At least for the time being, its not memory bound.
With the exception of a few that run pine or mh, most POP their mail to
their desktops. For this reason, most inboxes are relatively small.
Those of us in Engineering fear Eudora and the new built-in IMAP support,
which will allow users to leave their mail on the mailhost, which will
most likely grow to astronomical sizes. ;)
