On Thu, 18 Dec 1997, Dave Wreski wrote:

> >> Memory i/o and raw processing power may be bigger on the alpha side, but
> >> as mail translates into many parallel low-bandwith processes that are
> >> independent of each other, you should get substantial performance from a
> >> dual-CPU system.
>
> Doesn't that depend on the applications he will be using? If I recall, mail
> was his primary application, and sendmail, nor imap are most likely
> configured to take advantage of dual-CPUs.

Hmm. I think it does: 300 users running IMAPd (of any kind) means 300
processes. That definitely makes use of dual cpu:es; as opposed to one big
app that's dependent on its own results from previous cycles. But, again,
i/o is usually the main prob on mail servers (unless procmail does A LOT
of pattern matching :->)

> Why not consider:
>
> 1. Using one box for outgoing, and one (or more) for incoming services
> (mail and IMAP, specifically)

That, OTOH, gives you extra hassle with NFS (or whatever)

> 2. Check out how the Sparc port is doing. I believe among the supported
> platformst, Sparc's will have the best I/O thruput..

Well, but how about the bang/buck? The used suns that are floating around
at reasonable prices are not in that kind of class, and expansion is
expensive, as the market is small and the harware is sun-specific.

BTW: Aaron never said he was determined to use RHL -- and 5.0 isn't out
for Sparc yet.

> Also, your machine stopping the delivery or processing of mail is due to a
> configuration of sendmail, not an inability of BSD, in case that wasn't
> clear.

Dunno about Aaron being clear on that, but if people use procmail etc.
this might be a good idea.
