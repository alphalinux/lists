At 06:22 hrs. 06-22-97 +0200, you wrote:
>In article <33AD59DF.8FC05590@net-arts.com> you write:
>>I own a 233mhz Multia I bought for about $800 new. I added 64 MB of RAM,
>>bringing the total to 88, added an external SCSI chasis with a 3GB drive
>>and 8X CDROM. Total cost including computer and RedHat disk: $1800. I
>>don't kow why this neat little box has been so maligned.
>
>You may try to overclock it with the three jumpers "cpu speed" on the
>motherboard. My 233 UDB runs at 300 MHz without a glitch. It seems that
>using the support to put it vertical helps in getting rid of the heat.
>
>These are truly good machines, but it is too bad the memory bus is only
>at 33 MHz; this is rather limitative with some applications (for instance
>Quake).
>
>I do not know why DEC stopped the production of such boxes.
The answer, I am sure, is that they couldn't make any money on them. Don't
forget that the lowly 166MHz UDB/Multia listed at over $4000 when in
production. They were blown out for much cheaper as an inventory reduction.
Also, the 21066 CPU's and many other components are long out of production.
The same has happened with many other models recently, but the UDB was by
far the biggest volume "blowout" model.
Look for another blowout on the PC164 boards in the next little while. The
LX boards are now out and the low-cost 21164PC's are in production, and if
DEC has a reasonably large inventory of PC164's they may become quite an
attractive bargain soon.
IMHO DEC has to get the higher clockrate Alphas rolling out in volume and
at a reasonable price soon. Intel, AMD and Cyrix are now shipping 233 and
266MHz CPUs this summer in volume, and for DEC to maintain it's claim to
faster CPUs it _HAS_ to get 566MHz Alphas shipping in large quantities at a
reasonable price.
