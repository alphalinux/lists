Ben Scott <bscott@leonardo.sr.unh.edu> wrote:
> People point to things like BogoMIPS and SPECint as benchmarks; I really
> don't put much stock in such things. The differences across hardware
> platforms and OSes are too great to make anything like SPECint be a valid
> benchmark. It is comparing apples and oranges.

Although all benchmarks are lies, SPEC benchmarks are actually amoung the
most useful of them, *if* they are quoted and interpreted correctly.

The SPEC benchmarks are benchmarks of a *system*. By themselves,
microprocessors do *not* have SPECmarks, although manufacturers sometimes
like to publish "predicted SPECmarks". But what happens is that the
press and some of the less scrupulous resellers will notice that some
super-expensive system using a given processor and clock rate achieves
72 SPECints, and they will advertise that their cheap system which has the
same processor but nowhere near the same cache size or memory bandwidth
has the same SPECint rating.

When a manufacturer publishes the SPEC ratings of a system, unless you are
going to buy that exact system, you should consider the rating to be an
upper bound on what a similarly configured system might possibly acchieve.

See the official SPEC page:
http://www.specbench.org/
