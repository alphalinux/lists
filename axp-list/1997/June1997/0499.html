> On a cold start (ie, turning the system on) or on a linux reboot I get the
> following errors. On the other hand, if I boot into NT first, and then
> reboot into linux, it all works. Given this, I doubt that I need to reset
> and IRQs, but I'm happy to be proved wrong. Comments, suggestions etc welcome.
>
> scsi_ncr53c7,8xx : at PCI bus 0, device 9, function 0
> : warning : revision of 17 is greater than 2.
> : NCR53c180 at memory 0x1800000,
> : at io 0xb000, irq 28
>
> scsi : burst length 0
> scsi : NCR code relocated to 0x40048694 (virt 0xfffffc0000048694)
> scsi : test 1 started
> scsi : driver test 1 timed out due to lost interrupt
> Please verify that the correct IRQ is being used for your board,
> and that the motherboard IRQ jumpering matches the PCI setup on PCI
> systems.
> If you are using a NCR53c810 board in a PCI system, you should
> also verify that the board is jumpered to use PCI INTA, since
> most PCI motherboards lack support for INTB, INTC and INTD
> scsi : DSP=0xfffffc0000048e8c (script at 0xfffffc0000048694, start at
> 0x40048e78)
> scsi : DSPS = 0x4000000.

I had a very similar problem on my Linux 2.0.30 i386 box at home. My
symptoms were very similar in the sensethat I was getting continuous
timeouts on the CCB's, etc. of my BusLogic BT948c.. Anyway, the machine
would boot fine if I was just coming off of NT... I tried everything
(including hacking past the IRQ checks, etc... just to get it to boot). It
would boot fine despite the warnings. Then one day I was browsing the
BusLogic site, and I saw some BIOS/Firmware updates. They were FAR newer
than what I had, so I decided, "what the hell, the card is FUBAR'd
anyway" ... After upgrading the BIOS and Firmware n' stuff, everything
worked flawlessly.
I do not understand why Windows NT would correct the SCSI Card's
behavior (except that the drivers were probably written by the
manufacturer). I don't know if this is a similar problem with your card,
but I hope this info helps :)
