>
> I do not know what you mean by booting 'single'.

Booting 'single' means booting in a single-user mode, as opposed to a
normal multiple-user operation. You do not run 'login' program then and
only limited initializations. Various file systems may also be mounted
in read-only mode, or not mounted at all, and you will have to remount
them if you need a write access. Once you typed 'exit' a boot process
will continue to a normal multi-user mode.

Add 'single' to your boot command (or 'init=/bin/sh' ; this will bypass
'init' program totally and it will run your basic shell instead so you
have to, and can, do everything "by hand" and from that you have to
effectively reboot).
