> Also, I realize a lot of people care more for an optimized
> gcc than g77.

This is what amazes me most in this whole discussion. Burkhard
Neidecker-Lutz <neideck@kar.dec.com> posted a gcc/g77 based run of
SPEC95 on one of the current Alpha's to comp.arch half a year ago
and he came to the conclusion that for this benchmark, gcc was about
3 % slower than DEC's cc and g77 about 35 % slower than DEC's
Fortran compiler. Now surely 35 % is a gap that's worth to bridge,
but if I were a vendor of a highly portable, retargetable C compiler
that got within 3 % of a vendor's specific C compiler on a
well-regarded industry benchmark, I'd probably not spent any effort
on the optimization passes of said compiler.

> Besides, since g77 uses the same backend as gcc, it's
> quite likely that some of his work will positively affect
> the gcc backend as well (and that's where all the
> Alpha-specific optimizations happen).

Although in principle you're right, C is basically, just because it
is so close to the machine, unoptimizable. I could point you to
your own Linux Expo talk (e.g., did you ever wonder why gcc _itself_
didn't reduce all the induction variables in you matrix multiply
example ?). Besides, Craig pointed out that there are optimizations
necessary for Fortran that cannot be applied to intermediate code
generated from C because of semantic differences between C and
Fortran.

Hope this helps; more info on request and remember: free advice is
worth every cent you pay for it (thanks to Steve Lionel).
