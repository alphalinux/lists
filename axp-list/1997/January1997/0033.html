Can someone comment on the speed (anyone running gaussian or some other
fp intensive task and/or code development) of 21164 based systems. My
research advisor has asked for some input on hardware/OS solutions and
I'm itching to go with Linux, but if Digital UNIX is a superior OS for
software development I'll go with that.

Also, if you people could share your experiences with Alpha Linux as a
development platform (productivity/stability of the OS) I'd be stoked.
I'm trying to convince our research group to go with UNIX (either Linux
or Digital UNIX) instead of VMS and nobody here wants to monkey with the
operating system, they just want to code.

If your choice is VMS vs. Unix, you are probably surrounded by old VMS
hands, and it is almost impossible to have a rational discussion :^).
There is no doubt that VMS is poorly suited for general scientific
computing (lack of good tools for data massaging/evaluation),
but try telling that to a VMS fan.

I must say that for scientific coding, Linux is still an adventure, for the
following reasons:

- Linux math libraries are still unoptimized (Basic Linear Algebra routines
especially, which happens to be important for Gaussian)

- gcc compiles slowly (say half of DEC cc's speed) and generates slightly
slower code (say 10% slower)

- the compiling environment is still under development: no profiling,
minor problems with the debugger

On the other hand, the economics are in favor of Linux: you can buy a
500 MHz Linux machine for $6000, probably less than you might pay for
a 300 MHz DEC workstation with DEC Unix, which is the easiest way of
getting a fully DEC machine (one could also buy separately the $6000
machine and DEC Unix licence (which seems to cost around $700---correct
me please if I am wrong, e.g. if this price is only for .edu people).

In short, if one wants to avoid learning the intimacies of Alpha
booting and kernel compilation, and money is no object, one would buy
a 'DEC workstation package'. If one is not afraid to tinker a little,
and familiar with Unix and Linux in general, one could afford a little
inefficiency here and there; the faster hardware makes up for slower
software.

All in all, my advice is to 'ride with Linux', but YMMV.
