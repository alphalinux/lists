>
> http://www.photocool.com/tecool.htm gives a pretty good overview. The
> specifics have to do with cooling photomultipliers, so they may not
> neccessarily apply to cooling CPUs. However the main thing to
> remember is even with a cooling fan and a heat sink, there is a limit
> to how much temperature differential they can create. Theoretically
> in the laboratory, Peltiers can cause a 70 deg. Temperature
> differential. Realistically, under these conditions I would expect
> no more than 20 deg.
>
Peltier-Elements are not useful for cooling chips, cause they are chips themselves. You may run a processor up to about 75 degree centigrade chip(!) temperature. That is about the same temperature the Peltier elements are allowed to run.
Peltiers are only useful if you want to cool sth below ambient temperature (Photomultipliers). The second point is that iif you need to cool away 25 W from the Processor you will need a (very large)peltier-Element consuming 250 W (!). Additionally you need to cool the peltier-hot-end down to 125 degree centigrade, which is very challenging if it's heated by 250W...
If the hot end of the peltier gets more than 125 degree centigrade it will die like every other piece of semiconductor, ( as you mentioned peltiers are semiconductors (!) )

So it's VERY unlikely that you may use a Peltier to cool a Microprocessor.
Use a large heat sink together with a strong fan, so you can easily cool away several hundred W.
