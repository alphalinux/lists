>At times when it gets warm the machines get flakey and I need to restart
>them, but they won't even get to the point of loading ARC. What is wierd
>is that they won't restart at times even with the cover off and fan
>blowing directly on the entire box. The CPU chip fins are just barely
>warm to the touch, and it still won't restart, which makes me wonder if it
>is a heat related problem or something else.

I don't know. "Hot to the touch" can be fairly relative, I would think;
if you're in an 80-degree room, perhaps 110 degrees wouldn't seem as warm
as it would to me in my office (which is currently 68.4 :). Further, the
CPU chip might have gotten lots of heat attention in the engineering,
but it might be *other* components that are failing due to their
overheating. Finally, *where* the heat is building up might matter more
than where you can feel it. For example, cars build up fairly intense
heat right in their engines' pistons and stuff; the heat is moved away
from these via the various cooling mechanisms (forced air, water, and
so on), and sometimes the thermometer, or where you might place your
hand, is too far away to properly assess the heat, especially right after
turning the thing off. That's why some cars have fans that stay on
after the engine goes off -- it's right at that moment that the engine
might have the most heat risk *internally*, so that's a bad time to
turn off all the cooling systems. Apparently, your car's engine could
be melting down a minute later due to not being able to get rid of
*internal* heat at the same precise moment that you're putting your
hand on top of the hood and saying "hey, this isn't so warm", because
the outside air has already cooled the *hood* (but not the sensitive
internals). You might be experiencing something similar with your
UDB, that is, incorrectly assessing the internal heat based on the
external temperature differential (versus ambient).

So, it could be your CPU chip is overheating, the fins register it,
but you don't consider them to be hot; or that some other component
is overheating, not the CPU chip; or the CPU is overheating inside
due to the fins not being able to draw enough heat away from the
inside (due in turn to too-high ambient temperature) but, again, you
don't feel it, because the internal heat build-up doesn't always
translate into obviously high external (fin-surface) heat.

The most practical way, perhaps, to diagnose whether you have a heat
problem is to run the machine similarly for long periods of time in
a well-AC'ed place, say, Northgate Mall in Dallas (most other malls
will do -- IIRC they AC malls down to 63F in the South ;-). If it
fails similarly, you have another problem (maybe still heat-induced,
but not high-ambient-temperature induced, e.g. a broken cooling
fan or intermittently failing chip). Otherwise, you've diagnosed
your problem.

As usual, this is all guesswork. I am *not* an expert, or even
all that knowledgable about these things.
