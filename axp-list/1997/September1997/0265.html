At 10:20 AM 9/13/97 -0400, you wrote:
>I am curious as to how u know of this? at 500mhz ( 433 mhz ) u need
>awfully specialised equip to see this happen.

First off, it's not the 500MHz clock, its the 1024 ticks/sec RTC.

What the additional code does, is check that there's really an interrupt
posted in the RTC status register; if not, it ignores it. Previously, the
register was simply read (which clears the interrupt) but not checked,
believing that the interrupt would come through only when there *was one
posted.

I think the problem occurs in that the core logic chipset on the MIATA/LX164,
known as PYXIS, seems to not have its interrupt cleared fast enough when the
RTC register is read; the interrupt code may exit and re-enable interrupts
when the interrupt bit in PYXIS has not been cleared yet, thus re-entering
interrupt level right away, falsely.

The above info was related to me by the person who did/does the SRM console
on MIATA, because they saw the same problem (clock too fast) and had the
necessary equipment to track it down, or intelligence to figure it out... :-)

>Doesn't dec have a cure where they will distribute their PAL code. This
>does seem to be a serious bug that might warrent a PAL upgrade ( FREE i
>hope to those that have the prob ).

If we were using the *official* PALcode for MIATA, we wouldn't see this, as
its been fixed. However, MILO's PALcode is *not* the official one.

And yes, we *do* distribute the PALcode, but its the sample PALcode that
Digital Semiconductor releases for their boards, of which MIATA is not one.
So, we have been using the EB164 sample PALcode, which uses CIA and not
PYXIS, and thus doesn't have this problem.

And of course, the solution, when available, will be FREE as MILO always
has been... :-)
