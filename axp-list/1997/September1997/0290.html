At 06:48 AM 9/15/97 -0800, you wrote:
>On Mon, 15 Sep 1997, Rob Zook wrote:
>
>> The only thing that troubles me about using a Peltier(?) junction
>> is power. They run on 12VDC, but according to the amp vs heat transfer
>> diagram I got from the manufacturer, you have to run at least 1 amp
>> thru it before it pays any attention to you.
>
> Many packaged cooling units take 12VDC, however, the
>voltage/amperage requirments are different for each unit, as well as max
>temp differential, wast heat, and heat transfered. You see, you don't
>need just a peltier wafer, you need a regulator of some type so as to
>prevend condensation(sp?), etc...
>
>
>> Now most PCs have 200 to 300W power supplies, but I get the impression
>> most of that goes to the disk devices. Running at 1 amp the Peltier
>> will dissipate about 12W. Does anyone know if this would make up
>> a significant load on a PC's power supply along with everything else
>> drawing power?
>
> Between the device and the regulator it could be as high as 5
>times that amount (depending on device size, and transfer requirments).
>
>> Which means you should probably put some kind of power regulation circuit
>> between the Peltier junction and the PC's power supply. That way
>> you can adjust the current and get just the right cooling effect.
>
> This is required for safe operation.
>
>> Also you must use a heat sink on the hot side of the junction or you
>> can damage it. So a good physical arrangement would probably be to
>> use some heat sink compound on put the Peltier junction between the
>> CPU and the heat sink.
>
> This is an absolute! You must use a 25% - 50% oversize
>(heat transfer wise, not size), the peltier unit will move heat from one
>side to the other, BUT all that power you feed it comes out as heat on the
>hot side.
>
> The main problem with the use of a peltier wafer as a CPU cooler
>is this. While you can easily cool your proccesor below ambient during
>normal operation, should the device fail you are worse off than having no
>heatsink at all. You see, without power, or when damaged (easy to do, any
>twisting/bending/shock can cause failure) a peltier device becomes a
>wonderful insulator.
>
>>
>> http://www.photocool.com/tecool.htm gives a pretty good overview. The
>> specifics have to do with cooling photomultipliers, so they may not
>> neccessarily apply to cooling CPUs. However the main thing to
>> remember is even with a cooling fan and a heat sink, there is a limit
>> to how much temperature differential they can create. Theoretically
>> in the laboratory, Peltiers can cause a 70 deg. Temperature
>> differential. Realistically, under these conditions I would expect
>> no more than 20 deg.
>
> Depending on the device, 50 deg is easily had. Also the devices
>are stackable (within the max/min temp range of the units). I had much
>fun with salvaged units. I pulled 18 large wafers used for force cooling
>power transistors in some microwave transmitters, and cooled some pentiums
>with em. With 8 stacked, a 6"x4"x3" (l/w/h) heatsink, and a large fan
>-100F was achievable for a short time (really pushing it) and -75F could
>be kept for some time. Overclocking was easy, but after a while the frost
>of the CPU would cause major problems.
>
> Also, I ended up toasting one CPU, a wafer near the top failed, so
>the 6 lower units were all dumping heat from the power they drew, but with
>1 wafer playi8ng insulator... :( The problem went away when they melted
>the wires off but by then the CPU was cooked good, along with the socked
>on the MB.
>
>
> Anyway, if absolutely needed I use em, but beware, most of the
>PC CPU peltier coolers you see are not that great. Cheap regulators,
>faulty failure alarms etc... A quality device is great, the prob is
>finding one (instead of building a regulator for one).
