Tom Linden <tom@thirdmillenium.com> wrote:
> (Of course, Intel architecture is a giant leap backwards. Why they chose
> an accumulator architecture with the 8086, I can only assume that they had
> read nothing of the literature)

You can only assume that if you know none of the history of it.

The 8086 was only intended to be an improved replacement for the 8085,
continuing the chain from the 8080 and 8008 (but not from the 4040 and 4004,
despite claims of people who've never so much as looked at the data sheets).

Back in 1972, when the 8008 was being designed, there wasn't nearly as much
literature on the subject. Also, they were only trying to design an
embedded controller, not a general-purpose computer.

At about the same time that they were designing the 8086, they also had
an advanced development project for a 32-bit processor that *was* intended
for general purpose computing. Originally known as the 8800, it was
renamed the iAPX 432 prior to release. It was what might be called a VCISC,
and was not successful in the marketplace, largely owing to poor performance.

Since the 432 failed, they had to push the 8086 family (80286, 80386, etc.),
while they developed a new high-end architecture (which eventually became
the i960). However, in the mean time the IBM clone market grew so rapidly
that x86 compatability became more important than raw performance. Hence
the i960 has only been successful in the embedded market.

Many people make the mistake of thinking that Intel *likes* the x86
architecture, but my own occasional discussions with Intel engineers reveal
that they are as disgusted with it as anyone.

Back in 1990 I went to see the film "The Hunt for Red October" on opening
night with a coworker from Apple. We got in line very early, and were
conversing about Macintosh software issues. After a while, someone nearby
asked us if we worked at Apple. He then asked, "when are you guys going to
build a computer around an Intel processor?" While I was trying to think of a
tactful reply, my coworker blurted out, "We wouldn't put an Intel
piece-of-junk in *our* computers!" As I expected, the guy turned out to be an
Intel employee, and in fact, the the designer of the i486 FPU. He didn't take
offense, and we had a nice chat. He was understandably proud of his work, but
was unhappy that they had so much baggage from the earlier processors that
had to be carried forward.

Intel didn't choose for the x86 to be their premier architecture, it just
happened. Of course, unless you are a complete idiot you don't refuse to sell
the customer what he wants to buy and try to steer him to something else
instead. That approach drives the customers into the hands of the competitor,
as happened in 1986 when DEC pulled the plug on the DECsystem-10 and
DECSYSTEM-20 and tried to force customers to migrate to the VAX. (Many of the
customers went to Sun and other Unix based systems; this was before DEC
embraced Unix.)

Anyhow, the amazing thing is that Intel has been able to take such a feeble
base architecture (the 8086), and due to customer demand turn it into one
of the highest-performance microprocessors on the planet (the Pentium II).
(Yes, I know that the Alpha is faster. That's not the point.)

I'm certainly no fan of the x86, but I've got to respect Intel's engineering
accomplishments. I'll have to admit that I'm somewhat skeptical about their
new IA64 'EPIC' architecture. However, Intel and HP may eventually succeed at
something that we've only dreamed about DEC doing with Alpha: migrating the
entire PC market onto a non-x86 architecture. Yes, it is true that the initial
implementations of IA64 will include x86 compatability, but after people have
been running Windows NT/IA64 for a few years they will be able to start
phasing out IA32. (IA32 will probably live on forever in simulation and/or
binary translation.)

Even though Alpha is my favorite architecture today, realisticly I can't
believe that it will ever succeed in the general PC market, whereas IA64
has a good chance.

Cheers,
