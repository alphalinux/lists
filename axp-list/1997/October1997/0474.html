greetings folks,

i know a lot of you may be shocked/disappointed/(fill in the blank) at
the recent news about the alliance, but take a moment and consider my
observation.

digital gave us an opportunity to learn about 64bit architecture. (imo)
their engineers are some of the best on the planet. the 'buck' doesn't
stop with 64 bits. there will be greater architecture and i'd put my
money on digital to hold up the forefront of this kind of development
and (smile) look forward to working with 128/256/512-bit linux.

if you want to talk about disappointment, i can ramble for hours, but
(smile) will spare you by giving one example: the day 'big blue' took
out 'cp/m' (chuckle - please, no religious wars).

in most cases, i have shrugged off my disappointment and moved on.

although, perhaps unrelated, i want to share a note i sent as a reply to
some questions raised, on this list, by alvin@iplink.net, 18 sep 1997,
and try to swing this thread back to linux.

--
hi alvin,


> 1) Linux is a fringe OS.


yup, it sure is, and i believe it is called "forefront"/"cutting edge"! 
(chuckle) most folks don't have a clue as to how advanced linux (in its
infancy) is, what posix is, how standards could have built a better 
cyberspace, or care. all they want is their application to work with
ease and without having to read a manual; (snicker) but then again, i'm 
not sure my thoughts would count, just by the fact i participate on the 
axp-list and we are (smile) certainly not like the majority, (chuckle) 
yet. 


> 2) Linux is not used for real appilcatioins.


(smile) it depends on who is defining real.  you and i know real things
can be done under/with linux using all those great gnu tools.  i believe
the folks who would say #2) would change their mind _if_ a number of 
major companies would port some of their software to linux/alpha _and_ 
be able to show a $_profit_$.  (imo) people get confused with FREE and 
"you get what you pay for".


> 3) Linux users/developers will not pay money for 3rd party products.


this is a tough one.  i purchased quake and just found another company
(linux/alpha based) who has a product or two i want.  (chuckle) do my 
15+/- linux cdroms count?  i'm willing to purchase products that make my 
life easier and save time.


> Is there any way to raise the visibility of Linux to the people at DEC


i, for one, believe dec has a visible eye on linux and will profit from
it, in the long run.  when dec gave linus an alpha and later placed the 
multia/udbs' on the "mature" list a lot of folks grabbed them up because 
they ran linux.  the bonus was learning 64bit architecture in advance of 
the rest of the computing world at a very meager $ entry. when the intel
"merced" finally makes it to the surface, the rest of the 32bit world 
(although great for secure/firewall/crypto `frontends' (smile)) will go 
away like the s-100 bus and "here" will be a "pool" of 64bit hackers 
with 4 +/- years of experience - or more, if you were lucky enough to 
have-access-to a osf/du license.  as others have stated, the future will 
probably have nt and unix and (imo) the folks weaned on emacs, minix, 
bsd, and linux will help perserve unix as an os (smile) [commercial
unices elided due to lengthy trademark ackowledgement].


one way to raise visibility would be to develop a native alpha (`fill in 
this space') `game' that would make oem (`fill in this space') `gamers' 
drool! ...remember, there will probably be 64bit linux/intel (smile) to 
make the short story long.


the only thing i have asked dec to consider is provide a hobby class
license for du (home use - no commercial application) like they have for
vms or provide some libraries for our linux allies at dec to use without
restriction.

--


best2u all,
