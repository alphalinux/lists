John Ross Hunt wrote:

> Here's an easier way. Assuming the second disk is partitioned and formatted,
> create a mount point and execute a shell script like this. All you need to do
> is substitute your device name and make similar corrections in /etc/fstab and
> you're set.
>
> #! /bin/sh
>
> cd /
> mount -t ext2 /dev/sdb3 /mnt/disk2
> find / -depth -xdev -print | cpio -padvVmB /mnt/disk2
>
> -John Ross Hunt

> >
> > I have got a new external 2 GB SCSI disk and would like to attach it to
> > my UDB. So far, my Linux was on the internal 518 MB hard disk, and now
> > I would like to move it to the external one.
> > My question is whether it is
> > enough just to partition the new disk and copy the whole linux filesystem
> > onto it,
>
> It will work just dandy. Use simply 'cp -a' but remember to skip /proc
> and a mount directory for your new disk in the process. 'cp -a' will
> handle /dev correctly (other methods of copying use 'tar' or 'cpio' or
> something like that, on stdout piped to another process reading stdin, to
> copy things around but 'cp -a' is the simplest). Create 'proc' directory
> on a new disk to provide a mount point , adjust 'fstab' and you may ask
> milo to boot from your new disk without blowing anything on your old one.
> This is a good check that you have everything in order. Actually simpler
> than on Intel. :-)
>
> > What I would like to avoid is to have to install Linux again
> > on the external disk.
>
> No need. On one machine I have actually a cron process which
> updates periodically a mirror system on a "spare" disk. In
> a case of a disaster the machine can be just rebooted from this
> "spare" (not remotely, someobody has to type on a console).
>
> Actually the simplest way to get an answer to your question is just
> to try. Not very much involved in that. :-)
