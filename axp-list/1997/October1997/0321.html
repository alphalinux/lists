> This is indeed the crucial issue; the fact that there are SCSI/IDE
HDDs/CDs
> listed is just a matter of completeness; when they say those have been
> verified, it doesn't necessarily mean that others have failed the tests.
> And in the case of the video cards, it's more of a list of which vendors
of
> expensive/high-performance cards have drivers that will run on the
Alphas,
> and which may benefit the most from the additional Alpha horsepower.
>
> But back to the main issue...
>
> Both video and SCSI controllers have BIOS on them, and the latest
> revisions of AlphaBIOS attempt to init the cards using an x86 emulator to
> run that BIOS code. In fact, its the *same* emulator that is part of MILO
> that we use to init the VGAs via their BIOS code.
>
> Now, the BIOS emulation is *NOT* just a matter of handling the x86
> instructions, which is really the easy part. The emulator must also be
able
> to handle bus memory and IO space accesses; in the case of PCI bus, this
> also includes Config space. Now, on the Alphas, access to the latter is
done
> in a completely different way than on INTEL-based PCs, so the emulator
must
> detect what's being attempted by the INTEL code, and perform the
appropriate
> equivalent on the Alpha.
>
> It's complicated by the fact that there are *two* hardware "mechanisms"
for
> PCI Config space access defined in the spec, whose sequences of INTEL
code
> are sufficiently different to require different detection methods.
>
> In the case of the Matrox Millennium, the BIOS code actually uses *BOTH*
> mechanisms; the BIOS emulator only supports *ONE*. Fortunately, in this
case,
> none of the unsupported mechanism operations seem to be critical, and, in
most
> instances, don't cause additional problems (but I have stories... ).
>
> The above is just one example, but I think you can see what can happen.
> Be glad that there *is* such a list; it gives those people who don't wish
> to "push the envelope" a sense of security that what they buy will work.
