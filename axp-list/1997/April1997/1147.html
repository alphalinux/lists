On Mon, 28 Apr 1997, Bill Bradford wrote:

> On Sun, Apr 27, 1997 at 12:51:27PM -0700, Terry Carroll wrote:
> > That's true, but in the U.S., ownership of the media gives you the right
> > to run the software without the license.
>
> Since WHEN? I've never heard of this as being true.

Since December 12, 1980, when section 117 of the Copyright Act was
enacted.

> In order to legally
> run and use several operating systems (VMS, SunOS, Solaris, etc), you must
> have an RTU (Right-To-Use) license along with the software media.

Unless you actually own (as opposed to merely possess) the media.

> > In order to limit you solely to
> > the terms of the license, the licensor must transfer physical possession
> > of the media to the recipient and still retain ownership of the media.
>
> Read a license closely sometime - at least with my Sun stuff, it says
> that Sun still owns the media, you are just paying a fee to borrow it,
> or something tot hat effect.

That's exactly my point. As long as the software manufacturer retains
ownership, you can't take advantage of section 117. But in the case of a
true sale, including a transfer of ownership, you can.
