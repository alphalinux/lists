>I, to have been considering my "next machine" and comparing the
>alternatives of (a) Dual P-II or 500 axp. There are various
>considerations to make:
>
>1: If your primary use is (as you said) running non-multithreaded
>applicationns, you might find yourself having a difficult time utilizing
>the benifits of a dual-processor system. Few people (unless running
>heavy server-apps or specialized multithreading apps) can squeeze much
>performance benifit from a dual-chip system.
>
>2: If cost is an issue, you may want to get a P-II with a dual-chip
>motherboard, but wait on the second processor until you are convinced
>you need/want it. SAG and Dell are two companies (off the top of my
>head) which make such systems.
>
>3: If you have not used an Alpha system before, make sure you realize
>all the extra work that is required. I bought a little axp computer for
>experimentation and there are definitiely large learning-curve and
>implementation overheads involved. Much software is not written
>properly for a true 64-bit OS, even with the wonderful x86 emulator, it
>is difficult to get much software running. I haven't heard from many
>people who've had any luck getting Netscape up and running. In shoft,
>an Alpha is a fun experimental system if you also have an Intel system
>to fall back on. Otherwise there are a lot of difficulties incurred.
>
>4: You may have an easier time with sound software/drivers on an x86
>system.
>
>Go with the Alpha if you (a) enjoy tinkering A LOT and (b) really need
>RAW PROCESSING POWER.
>
>That said, have fun.
