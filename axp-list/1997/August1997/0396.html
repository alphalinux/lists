>I've tried manipulating a large library (^17MB) on an old 60MHz Pentium,
>working on a SCSI-2 Jaz drive (so the disk is not too slow). A make,
>doing lots of ranlib's, takes nearly 30 minutes even if there are no
>changes; on an IBM rs/6000 43P (not a particularly fast workstation), it
>takes just a few seconds!

I'm no expert on this, but why "doing lots of ranlib's"? I believe the
only purpose to ranlib, on systems where it has any purpose at all,
is to construct indexes for a library so `ld' can read it efficiently.

So, if you aren't loading from the library between running `ranlib' on
it, eliminate all but the final `ranlib'.

In particular, I am guessing that what you might be doing is something
akin to `ar rc libfoo.a foo1; ranlib libfoo.a; ar rc libfoo.a foo2;
ranlib libfoo.a; ar rc libfoo.a foo3; ranlib libfoo.a; ...', in which
case I think you'd be much better off eliminating all but the final
`ranlib'.

(Perhaps on the IBM system `ranlib' is smart enough to do nothing
if no updating is needed?)

Sometimes modifying `makefile' rules to do this is a bit of a pain,
however...especially when one tries to target a wide variety of
old, buggy, or incomplete versions of `make'. But it is doable,
worth carefully reading various chunks of documentation to figure
it out.
