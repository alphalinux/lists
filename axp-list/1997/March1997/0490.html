There are two major types of ways of handling "parallel" or "simultaneous"
instructions that I can think of off the top of my head, and I've never
heard the terms used in any way but interchangeably.

The two are superscalar and VLIW. Just about every processor you would
buy today is superscalar, which means that instruction parallelism is
handled at run time by the processor doing dependency checks between
two (or more) instructions and determining whether or not they can be
executed at the same time, and whether there are sufficient resources
available (ie, execution units) to do it. In some processors, like the
Pentium (R) Processor, the execution units are not equal, so two
instructions without dependencies may not be able to be executed
simultaneously (or in parallel). In out of order processors like the
R10000 and the Pentium (R) Pro Processor, instructions without
dependencies can pass other instructions if there are free execution
units.

In VLIW, ILP (instructin level parallelism) is determined at compile
time, and "opcodes" are actually two opcodes concatenated together.
This increases the complexity of the compiler, but removes all of the
dependency checking from the processor. I don't know of any current
commercial processors using this off of the top of my head, but
companies like Multiflow tried really hard at this a while back.

The EV4 and EV45 (to my knowledge) are superscalar dual issue
processors, and the EV5 is quad issue, meaning two or four instructions
can be sent to execution if they are independent. The Pentium Processor
would be dual issue, and the Pentium Pro can be up to 3 issue
(sorta 4, its doing conversion to microops, and the number of microops
per instruction varies by quite a bit).

At least, to the best of my knowledge. More information on the Alpha
is available at: http://www.azstarnet.com/~axplinux/alpha-sys-3.html
