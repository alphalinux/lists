Are you talking about a regular boot or an initial installation?

For the first set, like docs say, CLOCKMODE to ARC in
/etc/sysconfig/clock. Installation menus also give you this option.
Depending on a relase you may, or may not, need '-u' option to 'clock'
when it runs from startup files and that is it. For more details
do first 'man 8 clock' and look later into scripts in /etc/rc.d
directory.

If you are talking about the fact that an initial installation
leaves you with a pile of files dated far into the future, which
may later be a big nuisance (when you do incremental backups, for
example), then after you set your date properly, with 'clock -A ...',
you can do the following:
touch /tmp/now
find /bin /sbin /lib /home /root /usr /v* -newer /tmp/now | xargs touch
(add, delete, to the list of directories above as needed; this is
an example, from memory).

The trick with 'touch' is not as good as a correct time from the
beginning, but better then something totally out of whack.
There is some chance that one day Red Hat will help you also with
the second problem. For now you have to live with it the best
you can.
