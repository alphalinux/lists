Jay,

Are there any plans to add these patches for the 2.0.31 kernel? The best
luck I had was when using glibc2 with a kernel compiled under glibc2.
The kernel was completely stock, and I didn't see much in the way of
unaligned trap problems. The new milo works great, but as with anything
else its hard to tell what is helping what.

Currently I see the best option as using the stock 2.0.30 tree with the 2
genie patches, after upgrading to glibc2 (or just starting with the new
redhat beta to simplify things). This is based partly on personal
experience, and partly on what I've extrapolated from Harvey Stein's
*excellent* kernel/patches notes.

Another option may be to use the stock 2.0.31 kernel, which may fix the
trap bug already - is there any word on what alpha specific fixes were
merged into the 2.0.31 kernel? I'm not particularly concerned with em86
support, more with the overall stability and performance of the machine.
i.e. - if there is a bug in the kernel that will kill my machine, I want
to get rid of it. It seems the usual advice is to try things out and see
how they work, but I'd be willing to bet that people who are experiencing
problems have one of the few variables which can crash a machine in
common.

I have a stress test program which was originally given to me to stress
linux/intel machines, but also works on the alpha. By the middle of next
week I should have some time to test out a few different configs, but if
anyone wants to give it a shot on there own, it might be interesting to
map out what combinations of milo/kernel/patches are more stable than
others. My particular interest is from people who don't have any
problems at all, i.e. - I'd be interested to see if it is because the
machines aren't heavily loaded, or because they have a good config.

At any rate, if anyone has any interest let me know, and I'll post the
stress program on our ftp site.
