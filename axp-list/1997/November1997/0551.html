On Thu, 20 Nov 1997, Mike Howard wrote:

> which does not. The community really doesn't want too much:
> - acknowledgement
> - source access
> - pretty minimal support
> - to see the product of their labor put to use
> - and the opportunity to do some small amount of business in order to
> support their programming addiction.
>
> Does any of this stuff make it to RH management or are we just farting
> in the wind when we talk about this kind of thing?

It definitely makes it through. Press releases are not an appropriate place
for such acknowledgements as they must be short and are read mostly
by corporate types.

Let me tell you some of the ways we're addressing all of your points though.

- We send out hundreds of free copies of Red Hat
- We give significant amounts of money to Linux International, the LDP,
and the FSF (we're one of the FSF's top American sponsors)
- We sponsor shows like Linux Expo, which flies in developers, literally,
from all over the world
- We mention the Linux community in press releases, our manual, and on
our web site
- We are *committed* to including source, GPLing our products, and
making all of it on the web site.
- Our new support program (www.redhat.com) is designed to help the
Linux community do more high-end consulting by giving them a framework
and credibility.

This is just a small list, and by no means complete.

Red Hat considers itself part of the free software community. Many of Red
Hat's employees (including myself, Michael K. Johnson, and Christian Gafton)
made real contributions to that community before we were hired, and
have continued to do so. Red Hat's management and investors believe that
Linux's primary strength is it's development model and will not do anything
to disrupt that. Believe it or not, our marketing folks and primary
investors are as fanatical about free software our developers are.
