On 16 Nov 1997, Christoph Zens wrote:

> >> generally it can be said that cisc code is faster to comile than risc code
> >> (which is an *advantage* of the risc architecture, not a drawback!).
>
> >Why? Being slower to compile surely seems like a drawback to me. Usually
> >it doesn't matter but why should it be an advantage?
>
> The more you cut down the code into little pieces (load/store/add...) before
> running it, the less time is needed by the CPU to interpret the commands at
> runtime.

That sounds like a gross oversimplification. I only have a 21164 but if
you're right byte-loads will be slower on the 21164a, because they are no
longer split into several instructions? :-)

> And, yes, CICS-CPUs are heavily micro-coded (not hard-wired), and
> thus behave like an interpreter.

AFAIK current CISC-CPUs do not use microcode for most heavily-used
instructions - and RISC basically has only those instructions anyway.

> Having to perform only some basic operations, the RISC can take much more
> advantage out of syncronous pipes. Ever heard of a CISC-CPU with a 17-stage
> pipeline?

Of course RISC has advantages. Being able to make them faster and/or
cheaper is the single important advantage of RISC. This may imply longer
compilation-time and people are willing to accept that drawback to gain
the other advantage. Nevertheless longer compilation-times are still a
drawback (and I'm not saying that RISC necessarily implies longer
compilation-times).

> You also would not want to say:
> "BASIC is the best coice, because it doesn't need *any* compile-time..."

I do say: "An interpreted language usually has the advantage of much
shorter turn-around-times."
