Christopher Curtis <ccurtis@ee.fit.edu> wrote:
> lca: machine check (la=0xfffffc0000208490,pc=fffffc00003ffacc)
> Reason: correctable ECC error (short frame, retryable, dc_stat=0):pn
> Correctable read error to memory occured at address 1418b50
> To fix: ...?

It's a hardware problem. You either have flaky SIMMs, or they are not making
good contact. In the latter case, sometimes reseating them fixes the problem.
Also, cleaing the contacts can be helpful. A pencil eraser works well for
that; just don't scrub too hard, and be careful not to leave any residue.
It is possible, but less likely, that you have a bad motherboard.

Note that correctable errors are in fact exactly that. The system is just
reporting that an error happened but was corrected. If you get uncorrectable
errors, that's another story. Also, correctable errors should only happen
infrequently. If they happen a lot, it would be worth studying the
reported address to see whether they only happen in one area, or are evenly
distributed throughout memory. I think the physical address of the error is
the "la=" number, though I could be mistaken.

Most recent PC motherboards with Intel chip sets do support parity, but
hardly anyone ever installs the right memory to take advantage of it. It's
pretty scary when you think about it; the machines are getting occasional
memory errors that aren't even detected, much less corrected. Random errors
sometimes hit memory that is not in use, and sometimes they hit things that
cause out-and-out crashes, but the really terrifying part is that it may hit
important data, get saved to disc, and you not find out until much later.

For example, a single-bit error in the exponent of a floating point number
might cause an error by a factor of eight in a strength of materials
calculation for a bridge, resulting in a collapse.

For a little more background on ECC, see:
http://www.brouhaha.com/~eric/computers/ecc.html

> These lca: ECC errors occur even though the machine sits idle. They did
> not occur before I recompiled the kernel (was using the stock kernel).

When you rebuild the kernel, it can cause the memory usage to change
slightly. If you just have a few bad bits in the memory, you might hit
them more or less frequently.
