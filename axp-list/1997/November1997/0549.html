Now, I do think they are smoking dope if they think they will sell many
RHmember subscriptions at $299.00, that smacks of the Caldera reality
distortion field. We, my company paid only about $50 more for a full one
year support contract awhile back. Add it up, Redhat has only had 3 or
fewer revisions in a given year in the past. Even if you paid full price
for 3 boxed sets + shipping, AND bought a ball cap, AND figured in a 10%
discount on the typical amount of Redhat products you bought in a year,
you would still fall almost $100 short of the $299 membership fee. What
kind of "deal" is that? Thats alot of money to pay for the convenience of
getting the dists automatically. Yes, I know there are other unnamed
perks. But as you can see this is not gonna be for the 'typical' RedHat
user.
Don't forget the `membership card'. That's got to be worth a lot.

SOAPBOX++
On a more serious note, UNIX vendors have always pretended that they
created the products that they are selling by omitting credits to the
original authors. RH's 5.0 press release on their web site reads like
they invented the idea of community development and the nifty new
technology: Glibc.

This has a far too familiar ring to it: back when I was fairly new on
UNIX systems, we purchased SCO's TCP/IP - from Lachman. The
documentation was not very good and included several rewrites of Eric
Allman's papers on Sendmail. The kick was that nobody was listed as
original authors and there were no references to the BSD doc - which
was much better than the `edited' version. Similarly, there are
never any credits in published man pages unless the stuff has been
copyrighted with a specific clause requiring credits: witness the
implicit claims of ownership of uuencode/uudecode, PPP, SLIP, etc.

SOABOX++

The UNIX programming community is the primary source of innovation in
S/W and has been for the last 20 years or so. It just `ain't right'
that commercialization of its technical successes is achieved by overt
or implicit claims of ownership. If it weren't for the work RMS has
done on the copyleft, there wouldn't be Linux and it would be just too
tempting for RH and others to just grab all the S/W, make a few
inconsequential changes for the sake of incompatibility with other
distributions, call the result proprietary S/W, and sell it with their
name and no other. SCO built a pretty large operation that way - for
a while. Bill Gates has made this into a fine art. They all do it,
given the opportunity.

SOAPBOX--

Regarding liking RH. I'm of a mixed mind. It depends on how they
grow the company, i.e. how greedy they get. Historically, companies
begin by leveraging off of the community and then trash out the
developers. This is justified by the 80/20 rule: they discover that
80% of their profits come from 20% of their customers and so
`naturally' concentrate on the most effective means of generating
profit. This reasoning neglects the fact that 80% of the new product
and development comes from that 20% profit base.

I don't know, but suspect that a company which maintains really good
faith with the community will do much better in the long run than one
which does not. The community really doesn't want too much:
- acknowledgement
- source access
- pretty minimal support
- to see the product of their labor put to use
- and the opportunity to do some small amount of business in order to
support their programming addiction.

Does any of this stuff make it to RH management or are we just farting
in the wind when we talk about this kind of thing?
