>All of a sudden I'm getting flashbacks of the Linux world in '94.
>Remember when Doom for Linux came out? I think that proved that Linux
>was worthwhile more than anything else. Is Quake doing the same thing
>for the Linux on the alpha?

Yes, it is. I have taken Quake to several trade shows (FED UNIX/Open
Systems World in Washington, D.C. for example, and Internet World in
New York City). People would walk right past the Alpha Linux machine
(particularly since Red Hat V4.0 made the standard interface look like
windows)

UNTIL I STARTED UP QUAKE

Then people came *literally* running across the floor to look at it.
Full-screen, no pixelization. QUAKE as it should be.

At FED UNIX I had one guy who kept coming around, and stopped past
several times just to look. He was in uniform, and had more brass on
his chest than I have in my favorite grandfather clock. You could see
from his face that he knew what that type of performance meant to his
application (which is probably flying around in an airplane five miles
up).

I was also asked by the host and producer of PCTV to have Alpha Quake
on his show about high-end 3D games. It opened the show, and the awe
in his voice as he talked about it was good news to me.

Yup, QUAKE is a great demo, particularly when you can turn the volume
up loud enough to compete with the other booths.

It helps to sell vertical and turnkey applications.

As to the other applications, they are coming. Remember how long it
took to get them on Intel Linux. You will start seeing them, "soon".
