Regarding all the Applix/AXP stuff. I think that the best summation for this
type of issue is that historically an operating system's success is directly
related to its application support (How many applications it can run). Now,
after many years Linux/X86 is beginning to see real applications support
(Caldera/Applix/Xinside/ID/etc.) and we are beginning to witness the true
blooming of Linux into a real, serious contender in the professional market.

This, it could be said is true of ALL commercial operating systems. But, we
all know that Linux isn't commercial (Thanks Linus) so what has made it a
success and how can that apply to the AXP/SUN platforms?

Linux has survived and prospered because of us. Geeks that didn't want to
blow the cash (Real Cash!) on a weak PC unix. We wanted to hack, we wanted to
play, we wanted to help. In this, Linux began to see quite a bit of
non-commercial application support almost everything you and I use everyday is
non-commercial, and quite good. Linux X86 has prospered because those of us
that have always supported it have pushed and positioned it within our own
environments until the idea finally started to catch on (We're still having to
blow the fire though, we're not there yet).

AXP/SUN Linux do have a couple of advantages over the infant Linux. One, the
ability to support native OS applications (ie. Solaris, and DU). This right
there greatly enhances the usability of the machine (Frankly, I wrote the
Idiot's guide, cause I was the idiot that wouldn't run a box without netscape
;-) Also, we have all of the other X86 sources that we can compile and run to
give our new toys the old toy look and feel. (Seems self defeating doesn't it?
:-D) This is creating the sparc. . .spark that may one day lead to the
acceptance of AXP and SUN linux.

Gasp. . .So what I'm saying is that yes, Applix and other applications should
look at getting ported to the box. It is a feedback loop that will continue
to grow and grow and will return much more than is put in, in my opinion. On
the other hand (being an old corporate systems vet.) it is quite expensive to
port these programs and it is, in the end, a business decision. Linux
(AXP/SUN) will survive, improve, and prosper until such time that it is a cost
effective business decision to port applications.

Did I say short? I hope not. [Gerald: Steps slowly off soapbox]
