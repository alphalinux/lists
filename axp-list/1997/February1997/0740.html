> a) It doesn't provide the degree of logging *and control* of package components
> that newer packaging formats (like RedHat/Debians') provide.
> b) It does not provide a dependancy-checking tool for determining what
> packages require other packages.

these two can speak in favour of slackware as well. giving more control
to the machine is only good so long as it makes all the right assumptions.
and to be honest, redhat does not come close. i have been running an
intel system that started as slackware 3 years ago, and everything is
still up to date because i maintain it. with my alpha, otoh, i more often
find myself arguing with rpm, pam, and other things that redhat puts there
"for your convenience" then with the real linux issues. it often amazes
me the things that redhat chooses to include or leave out ...

> c) There are components that are rather old and buggy. Slackware is the
> only distribution of TeX materials that I know of that still includes the
> fonts that Don Knuth specifically requested *not* be distributed about
> 2 years ago. It's a couple releases out of date on Ghostscript, using
> 2.6.1, which is no longer supported at all by the nice folks at Aladdin
> Software. The list probably goes on.

sure it does. slackware is not something that you can just install and
let be. but then, it is much simpler to install if you know what you're
doing, since you dont have to argue with the installer program.

redhat is reminding me more and more of the things that piss me off about
windoze, etc.

sorry about being long-winded.
in short: yes, there are those of us that would welcome slackware for
alpha!

thanks ...

-ross
