On Fri, 9 May 1997, CHIH-HSIANG HENRY YAO wrote:
> I need to know about how much more bigger are the Linux/Alpha codes compared
> to similar Linux/Intel to decide on the size of my partition.

Well, FWIW, on an Alpha, opcodes are all 4-bytes long, with no extra
"operands" following the opcode. But that really doesn't mean much, because
the processor architecture are very different. Perhaps of more use would be
this output from "df" on our AlphaStation running Linux:

Filesystem 1024-blocks Used Available Capacity Mounted on
/dev/sdb1 44615 22321 17686 56% /
/dev/sdb3 95183 14246 76022 16% /var
/dev/sdb4 718030 392209 303568 56% /usr
/dev/sda1 1978342 656278 1281163 34% /home

As you can see, a 45 meg root partition is about half full. That's with a
couple extra kernels in /boot but nothing in /lib/modules. /tmp is a symlink
to /var/tmp, so /tmp is not counted against root space. Make /var as big as
you want it to be, but 50 meg is a good minimum. How much swap to allocate
pretty much boils down to, "Allocate as much as you'll need." There are a few
different rules of thumb, but some conflict, and which one is "right" is a
matter of debate.

As for /usr: Many people (myself included) recommend putting /usr/local and
/usr separately. The theory is, if you put everything you do in /usr/local,
then you can nuke /usr without worrying about your own stuff (thus, you can
totally reinstall a new distribution if you need to). The RedHat 4.1
distribution uses about 410MB in /usr, plus you'll need about 30MB for
building a kernel, plus at least 50MB for breathing room.

As always, YMMV, and fine tune for your own needs. :-)
