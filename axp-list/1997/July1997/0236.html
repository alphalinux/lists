This is not a question of an underlying processor, but of a controller.
Different disk controllers may see the same physical media in a different
way. You have a fighting chance that something like the above will really
work if you use in both machines at least a close model of a controller
if not the same one. Normally a disk with a "foreign" partitioning will
be at least readable, but fdisk will whine about partition boundaries and
writing to such disk may have rather undesirable effects (like scrambling
a file system :-). As for heads/sectors/cylinders - you may ask fdisk to
write there really what you want. This was rehashed on this list many,
many times.

With the same controller a hard disk is completely interchangable
between Intel and Alpha machines. A friend has Alpha at work, an
old Intel at home and one disk in a "pluggable" enclosure with sockets
on both boxes. Both Linux (ext2fs) and FAT partitions are fully
readable and writable on both ends.

> and I had no trouble partitioning it.
> When I brought it back to the alpha, unfortuantely,
> it is unable to reliably write to it for very long, i.e. if I mke2fs
> on a big partition, it will freeze somewhere in the middle.

The most likely reason for that, if there are no troubles with
partitioning and a controller, that you have problems with
cables and/or termination. Alphas seem to be more sensitive in
this area than Intel boxes. This is just an observation and I do
not have a technical explanation (pushing buses harder with smaller
tolerances?). Active terminators are usually recommended.
