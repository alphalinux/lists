Couple of corrections. Uploading due to X2 code limitations is 28.8 NOT 33.6 (which requires an adjustment to the X2 code sometime in the future)

56k is possible but SPEC's from the FCC limit it to 53k... i have seen several cases of 56+ throughput on ALL fiber telco networks within the same switch! So its possible.

The real hinderance to X2 performance is transitions in the local telco cloud where a digital t-1 is passing thru multiple racks of switches where it goes copper instead of fiber. This causes an D/A & A/D conversion to take place
causing Signal to Noise ratio's to increase.

According to one Network pioneer who reconfigured a test rack (USR Total Control) for software compression and MultilinkPPP he got 2 X2's to get HIGH throughput on high quality lines in the local switch. Somewhere around the 128k mark and higher at times..!

At 12:13 PM 7/10/97 -0400, you wrote:
>> Am considering moving up from my 14400 modem. Any
>>suggestions on the fastest reliable modem?
>
>I've had tremendous luck with my Multi-Tech MT-2834BA, its only drawback
>perhaps being that it is a bit pricier than most. The fax implementation
>in particular is very good, and it will do UUCP spoofing for less cost
>than a Trailblazer.
>
>If you are considering any of the "56K" units out there you should be
>aware that they can attain speeds in excess if 33.6 only under special
>conditions and only in one direction.
>
>Basically you have to be connecting (usually a large ISP) whose units
>connect to the telco in the digital domain. This eliminates on
>analog-digital transition and can thus allow faster speed (although NEVER
>as fast as 56K, even with a perfect phone line). The speed advantage is
>only in one direction (provider to you) and you are still limited to a
>(theoretical) max of 33.6 from you to the provider.
>
>"Concentrators" used by a lot of local telcos also have an adverse effect
>on the performance of these units, and there is virtually no way to know
>if your home phone is going through one of these unless you are friendly
>with your local phone company techs.
>
>These units are essentially designed for somebody who wants to download
>quickly from a large provider like AOL, providing, of course that you
>have chosen the same flavor of 56K unit that your provider has. You can
>NEVER get more than 33.6 with two of these units talking to each other,
>since one end has to be the "digital" unit on a T1 line or similar.
>
>The above limitations, plus the lack of a standard at this time, would
>lead me to suggest that you stick with one of the established-brand 33.6
>units at this time.
>
