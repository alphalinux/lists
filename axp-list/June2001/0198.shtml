>I've always disagreed with forking the kernel, personally, but NOT forking

Okay, brace for this armchair quarterback's brain dump:

Compared to many other arches, alpha is actually quite in-synch. I used
to flame linuxppc-dev and debian-powerpc for the persistent policy of
keeping (multiple!) separate trees for PPC kernels, pointing to the
shining example of alpha as how a port should be run:

   1. Present and discuss patches against stock on a list like this
      one. (I agree with Greg that this has been the de-facto gathering
      place of alpha folk from many arches.)
   2. Send the resulting patches to Linus and/or Alan.

Because alpha has done this pretty well, 2.2.x stock came with excellent
alpha support, from 2.2.0 through 2.2.19, while PPC required patches
through 2.2.18, and ARM has never been in synch! However, it took a
very substantial amount of work to make that happen.

Unfortunately, with massive filesystem additions that were known to only
work at all on i386 (and then with some bugs), interface changes that
were only tested on i386, and entire subsystem rewrites, all happening
between 2.4.x subversions, this has been impossible to keep up with
2.4.x. Maybe we do need to fork after all, and leave out the
last-minute junk that doesn't work yet...

The way PPC works is that Cort, Paul, and Ben H (Ani Joshi too?) each
have their own rsync/Bitkeeper archives for stable and unstable, one is
usually better for stable and the other for unstable. Daily snapshots
are built both of the whole tarball, and also a patch vs. Linus' latest
-test release, with a goal of keeping that patch as small as possible
while still making progress on the arch. So new features are brought in
when they work, and the patches split up and submitted to Linus/Alan on
a somewhat regular basis, which has been a lot more frequent in the last
6-8 months than before that.

As noted, I used to hate that system, but with the 2.4 madness, must
admit to some pretty big advantages for at least one forked tree, over
swimming against the rapids to stay in synch.

>Prior summaries of what I really think would help the situation are
>correct: I advocate annointing someone or a group of someones to act as
>"port maintainers", which would review architectural changes and would
>follow the kernel development more closely than your average "I only work
>on my driver" kernel hacker.
>
This is already somewhat the case, see the MAINTAINERS file, which has
entries for ARM, PPC, PMAC (though Paul is taking over all of PPC from
Cort now), M68K, MIPS, S390, SPARC and the SGI VWS subarch. But no
alpha (and I imagine we'd have quite a contest for such a post :-).
 Again, all of the other arch maintainers are keepers of that arch's
fork, which we haven't needed for alpha since Jay's 2.0.x patches.

> They could be the ones that understand the
>implications of changes made to the arch-independent portions of the
>kernel and review patches in that context (with the ultimate goal being
>stability and consistency across architectures...or at least as much
>consistency as is possible). The way it is now, only a small number of
>people review and commit ALL changes to the kernel. As any management
>training will preach, it's just not possible for a group of less than 10
>people to effectively manage the amount of people who submit patches to
>the kernel.
>
Oh my, I'm not sure we should even have that many reviewing all of the
changes. How about a 64-bit person (alpha, IA-64, SPARC64, S390,
MIPS64, soon PPC64), a big-endian person (SPARC, MIPS, HPPA, PPC, M68K),
and an unsigned-char person (ARM, PPC), with at least one NUMA guy with
broad arch experience? I don't know about the other arches (SuperH,
etc.), but that would cut down your team of ~13 to three or four and
should make everything work everywhere.

Not that such a "best situation or compromise" is going to happen
though.:-)

But if what comes out of all this is an alpha maintainer who coordinates
this arch's patches and mailbombs them to Linus and Alan with some
chance of success, and perhaps maybe possibly a CVS, Bitkeeper or
Subversion archive of a "working" alpha tree to the extent this is
absolutely necessary (as they have made it necessary with 2.4's
wrenching changes), then that would represent progress. Do you agree
with that much, Greg?
