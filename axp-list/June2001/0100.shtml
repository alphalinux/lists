Maurice Hilarius (maurice@harddata.com) wrote:

> I concur.
> People often forget it IS Linux, and that distributions are just
> "snapshots" of what a company decided to package at some given point in
> time. As progress continues, and nothing stops when a distro is made, it is
> best to consider these as simply "starting points".
> Naturally if you are a fan of "Distribution X", stick to that, as it is
> going to give you the shortest learning curve to get comfortable.

I wouldn't say that all distros are simply "snapshots." Linux is just a
kernel, period. It is true that SOME distributions are not much more
then collections of other code put together in a fancy package. Then
there are others, Debian for example (just for example), who try and
work closely with the upstream authors (and in some cases they ARE the
author). Even bugs from the bug tracking system (BTS) is often sent to
the original authors. Not all distros are commercial, and not all
distros are based on a commercial one. Debian (again just for example)
is NOT based on another distro and is not-for-profit. IIRC Debian was
first founded in 1993, but work started in mid to late 1992. Packages in
Debian do NOT come from other distros, they come from the original
authors. If anything, OTHER distros are based on it.

As several people have said in the past: stick with what you know.

> We do our own distro, called Northern Linux, which is based on Red Hat
> We feel that more timely updates are needed for our customers who buy
> machines and support from us, so we add to, and update the Red Hat version.

As does Debian. That's why there is security and proposed-updates
sections. Those are updated often.

> I CAN say that Red Hat has the most history in the Alpha Linux market. That
> is not an endorsement that they are best, however, it is what we have
> chosen to start with for several years so far..

I agree. Red Hat was first, but that doesn't make them best. I moved
away from Red Hat several years ago because their support of Alpha has
been weak.
