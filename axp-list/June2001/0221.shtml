On Thu, 21 Jun 2001, Greg Lindahl wrote:

> Look, I'm just pointing out that it's a stable situation. If you ask
> inflammatory questions, what good do you think it will come out of it?

I don't think anything will come of it, frankly. Apparently, the only
people who "matter" like the status quo, so why even entertain my rant?
For the record, "stable" doesn't always equal "best situation or
compromise".

> Because that's a stupid idea? History has shown us that we are often
> totally in synch.

I've always disagreed with forking the kernel, personally, but NOT forking
the kernel requires more patience and knowing everyone's little quirks,
which gets downright irritating at times. I feel like I'm in high
school: gotta be in the right "crowd" to get anything done.

What history shows that we're often "in synch"? Take a look back at the
state of Alpha even as far back as 2.0.x. What history shows is that it
frequently takes more than two minor revisions of the kernel to get Alpha
"in sync" with the changes made to the rest of the code.

Apparently, though, you either misunderstand my overall point with what I
wrote. I was always taught that the only dumb questions were the ones not
asked, so I think that asking repeatedly is sometimes worthwhile since it
just might change a not-so-good situation. Obviously, if I didn't care
what happened on Alpha, then I wouldn't bother writing as much as I
have. In short, this discussion may be a "kernel politics" hot-plate, but
I don't think that taking a "let's not rock the boat or upset
anyone" approach is working in the best interest of the development of the
Alpha portions of the kernel. Fact is, I get more questions about why
some kernel version or another doesn't compile on Alpha and when I think
the changes will be integrated than ANY other questions (including "will
Linux run on my machine?"). If even one third of the kernel versions
within a RELEASED kernel major version (2.1.x and 2.3.x don't count since
they're development version cycles), it's just not acceptable to me
(nor anyone else who's ever done any kind of QA testing...one third is a
pretty substantial failure rate for a released product).

Prior summaries of what I really think would help the situation are
correct: I advocate annointing someone or a group of someones to act as
"port maintainers", which would review architectural changes and would
follow the kernel development more closely than your average "I only work
on my driver" kernel hacker. They could be the ones that understand the
implications of changes made to the arch-independent portions of the
kernel and review patches in that context (with the ultimate goal being
stability and consistency across architectures...or at least as much
consistency as is possible). The way it is now, only a small number of
people review and commit ALL changes to the kernel. As any management
training will preach, it's just not possible for a group of less than 10
people to effectively manage the amount of people who submit patches to
the kernel. (Yes, I was the unwilling participant in the Fed. Govt's
Incident Command training :-P).

Of course, you're welcome to disagree with me as vehemently as you'd like
:-)
