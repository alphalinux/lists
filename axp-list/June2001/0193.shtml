On Wed, 20 Jun 2001, Michal Jaegermann wrote:

> Regardless of a platform you have to be a bit persistent with your
> proposed patches. Dropping then on linux-kernel list is also usually
> not good enough. Better send them _also_ to Alan and/or Linus.
> This does not guarantee anything and if a patch is dropped, for
> whatever reasons, you are not likely to see any comments. A workload
> for these people is really high. Reasons quite often can be that
> a timing is bad. Other possibility is that they do not conform
> to guidelines from Documentation/CodingStyle.

I can definitely say that the latter case is just not true for my patches
(I strive for 100% compliance with the coding style...which is more than I
can say for most driver submissions that I've seen get accepted and
reformatted after the fact). As for persistence, been there...done
that. I resubmitted one of my documentation patches THREE times on
different occasions, only to see them totally ignored. The excuses that I
frequently got from the few folks that do pass Alan's and Linus' filters
were that i386 was most important and that my patches would need to be
reviewed to make sure that they didn't interfere with normal operation on
i386 (which made ZERO sense considering the fact that they were
DOCUMENTATION patches!). This is also true for other patches as well
(those to bring Alpha up-to-date with various changes made to the kernel
source...read: include/linux header changes...for the primary benefit of
i386). Patches containing the SAME EXACT CODE were accepted from other
people at a much later date, but mine were completely ignored.

> Yes, some proposed changes vanish without any apparent good explanation.

The best explanation that I've found so far is that most of us 'unwashed
masses' aren't important enough for Alan or Linus to even glance at our
emails. Hell, in two instances that I can remember, they BOTH read and
responded to threads that surrounded my submission time-wise, but never
saw fit to review my patches.
