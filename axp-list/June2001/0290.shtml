Mark brings some good points, I have my own in this
regards having met some of the developers internal to
the Alphaserver group.

> 1) Compaq is selling all of their Alpha tech to
> Intel and then promising to use Intel's future 64bit
> designs (whatever that may
> be) for Compaq hardware.

Does Compaq have any clue how long it would take to
get all the Alpha software ported to IA64
(Itanium/McKinnley, etc)? The SRM/PALcode dependency
alone is a show stopper. A new emulation/recompiler
would have to be built to facilitate Alpha over IA64
emulation, and that will further handicap the IA64
platform. Given that Alpha just got the crown for HPC
and a large following of OpenVMS/Alpha users in the
finacial and health segments, what on earth are they
doing?

> My problems with this are:
> Is the govt going to allow this?

GOOD POINT!! Digital and Intel went to court over
patent infrigements (Intel grabbed some Alpha bits to
improve Pentium illegally) and the DOJ stepped in to
make SURE Intel didn't outright buy Alpha off the
cash-hemorhagging Digital. Does the Compaq Intel deal
have any loopholes that would prevent the DOJ from
doing the same thing again?
 
> What happens to purely Compaq products? (SRM,
> OpenVMS, True64, Compaq's
> compilers, etc)

I would suppose they would be emulated, migrated and
then dissipated as Compaq Alpha engineers become Intel
engineers to leave to pursue other careers. I thought
the Compaq buyout was bad, this is far worse for
sustaining Compaq/Digital platforms, notably all
things Alpha (Tru64, OpenVMS, Non-Stop Clusters,
Advanced Server, compilers, SRM, board designs,
chipset designs, CPU designs, patents, etc).

> How freaking stupid is Compaq's management? Intel
> can't even get a first generation ia-64 product out
> the door. Does anyone really think they will
> have a next generation processor available by 2004.
> Talk about being sold on marketing.

Intel did one hell of a sales job on Compaq for Compaq
to risk losing its most loyal following in some very
high profit segments on the "promise" that IA64 will
rule the world. Trading a proven platform for an
unproven platform after publicly denouncing the IA64
design in favor of Alpha and then dumping the port
effort of Tru64 UNIX to IA64 shortly after.

This is a 180 degree reversal of both the dismissal of
IA64 and the current lack of a "Alpha peer" in either
performance or robustness. Compilers for IA64 still
have a lot of growing up to do, as do the platforms
that run under them. Alpha was introduced slowly with
VAX forming the underpinnings. This move looks more
like cannibalization.

> 2) How much of future Alpha design is inside of API
> now?

Samsung, through API, owns design patents on Alpha and
indeed enjoy a high degree of autonomy. What I don't
know is if the patents include the all important
patents for SRM/PALcode and the obvious need to share
some of this information with Compaq to consolidate
certain platform plans. API has it's own chipset in
th works to replace the aging Tsunami which differed
from Compaq's slated Titan II. Presumably, API could
survive by using it's "Linux in ROM" and using legacy
PALcode, at least for a time.

> API seems to have been forced to be an all Linux
> hardware company with this announcement. Which
> makes their work for gcc 3.0
> improvements very
> important. Personally I'm only buying Linux stuff,
> so Compaq leaving isn't
> that big of a deal. UNLESS this really going to
> affect future design
> improvements by API.

They were officially Linux anyway. It just so happened
some of their systems were Tru64 compliant, though
Compaq was eliminating support for newer support
hardware (storage, network and video controllers).

Comments anyone? I feel like going home to stare at my
three Alpha's for a time, sobbing.

--Kirk
