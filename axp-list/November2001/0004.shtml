deleted about "irony"....

        http://www.theinquirer.net/01110105.htm

Most of the 24x7x365 Industrial and Financial heart and soul of America runs
on Alpha either as VMS or Tru64 (and to a certain extent on Tandem, but
they aren't Alphas). Aside from a 390 there is nothing out there that
matches them for either performance or reliability -- never has been.

Two plus years ago, Project Bravo ported Tru64 Unix to Merced -- using the
IA64 emulator running on top of an EV5/6 machine running Tru64 version 4.
(Might have been an EV6, not certain.) The project was abandoned largely
because Tru64 ran faster on that emulator than it ran on the IA64 Silicon
when it was finally received from Intel. [And the fact that the Enterprise
division (Tru64 and VMS) was not allowed to compete with the Industry
Standard Server Division... even though the reverse prohibition was (and
is) not true.]

Back in those days -- nominally 1999 -- Today's shipping and soon to be
shipping Alphas (EV6/7 and EV7) were known to be much better performers
than anything Intel had in its pipeline through 2004. McKinley was
predicted to be as fast as an EV6/7 machine, but since it doesn't exist
yet, so nobody yet knows if will be even close. [As for the Alpha
acquisition, except for the initial press relase, Intel has failed to
acknowledge the deal -- never even mentiond the deal/technology in the
September Intel Developer's keynote.]

The sad thing is that the Alpha was killed for purely marketing reasons.
$280 million R&D is a big number when you are loosing your shirt. "Inferior
Technology" had nothing to do with it, despite the spin that Q has tried to
put on the decision. Compaq is a PC company, despite all their claims to
the contrary. They simply were never able to market Alpha even though the
technology was far superior to anything on the market. And as of
SuperComputing 99, IBM had nothing that could compete with EV8 (or EV7 for
that matter) ... which was supposed to be shipping now, not EV7!
EV7 was delayed more than a year because of <pick your favorite
non-technology-related reason>.

So yeah, Alpha was an expensive deal -- but that's because Compaq told the
world that Alpha was a proprietary, non-standard environment... what else
would any right thinking person conclude when confronted with a corporate
division which sells "Industry Standard Computers" -- but doesn't sell
Alphas?

Once again, Superior technology looses out because of inferior marketing.
Superior marketing always wins, no matter how inferior the technology.

..."deja-vous" all over again...?

9 months after RCA sold their "money loosing" computer division to Univac,
their internal accountants "adjusted" their numbers and discovered that not
only was the computer division NOT loosing money, but had actually been
making money for the proceeding several periods....it was expenses from the
rest of the company [read, marketing expenses], unjustifiably charged to the
computing division which caused it to look as if were loosing money.

Rumor has it that the only thing keeping Q more-or-less afloat this year
has been Alpha sales in the HPC (High Performance Computing) arena, mostly
Tru64, but a sizeable percentage also Alpha Linux.
