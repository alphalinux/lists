Thanks to all who took the time to reply the first time.. Let me give a more
practical explanation of why I was asking.

Two weeks ago I was in Santa Cruz for Cisco PIX Firewall training. Many of
the people in my class live around the area and work for consulting
companies who service the .COM industry. One of the guys was explaining to
me that he is seeing a lot of Red Hat/Apache servers popping up all over the
place. It's still typical to see Sun E10k servers on the back end, but
typically your front end is either Linux (typically Red Hat) running Apache,
or NT and IIS.

When I said "cluster" I really meant Web Farm. It seems there are two
mission critical areas for Systems. The front end and the back end. I
suppose the most common approach on the front end is to setup your servers
and use Round Robin DNS. Let's say you have twenty servers on the front.
If you're serving mostly static content (rootshell.com, gnome.org) I don't
think Alpha would give a noticable gain over a Dual PII/450. But if you're
running some sort of dynamic web site (Google, Amazon, Lycos, mp3.com)
wouldn't you benefit from a machine that has more cache, better FPU, and
64-bit virtual memory space? On the front end it's probably better to throw
down twenty DS10 boxes than than twenty Dual PII/450 for a small to medium
web site, isn't it? I was at Spec.org and the specs on DS10 and DS20 crush
any of Intel's technology right now.

The back end is where "clustering" comes in.. Running applications like
Oracle and Sybase. Is it just me or is this where Alpha really shines over
Intel? Of course, many are still buying Sun Enterprise 10000 clusters
(except for ebay) - but when the time is ripe, would Alpha Linux be the
preferred choice over x86 Linux?
