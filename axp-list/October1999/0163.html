At 03:10 PM 10/9/99 -0700, you wrote:
>Hi Kirk,
> I have seen the writing on the wall and it says "Telecom". I moved to
>a little company in the guts of the telecommunications protocol business
>called Adax Inc (Berkeley California, where I live) I moved them to
>StrongArm
>while I was in the OEM Division and they decided I knew something. I saw an
>opportunity to learn the structure of the backbone of international
>telecommunications.
>I began last Monday. (and went for discussions at the worlds largest telecom
>equipment manufacturer on Tuesday) My book is still open because of some
>issues which one often encounters in intimate little companies, but,
>with a degree in Mathematics, I like the protocol business (Frame relay,
>ATM, GSM, 3G, ...). The future for telecom is fascinating.

Telecom will be the single most important aspect of computing in the next
10 years. Backbones will jump to 50 Gbit and fiber, microwave and
sattelite will have infastructure all over the planet (and probably a few
around the dark side of the moon and around Mars, don't laugh!).

> The company we supported with their OpenBoot environment is Network
>Appliance
>a group of Sun expatriates, and very capable ones.

I had a feeling, NetApp Filers are excellent machines, the WAFL filesystem
is a very smart bit of engineering in its own right, kicked the pants off
my test Alpha/NT and Alpha/OSF machine with its specialized embedded OS and
optimized drivers. It had the same "fuzziness factor" of third generation
Amigas with embedded OS on a ROM and the nice firmware of the Sun machines
(which I still like alot for obvious reasons).

They developed their own
>kernel for their Alpha platforms. I did my best to encourage Digital, then
>Compaq to acquire NetApp.

This would have been a great buy, though the way Digital and Compaq were
going it would probably have been sold off like most of the other bits were
over the years and that would have helped no one. StrongARM, the PCI
bridge chipsets, Ethernet controllers were sold to Intel during the big
lawsuit, Cabletron got the switches, hubs and routers, the trend in the
last 5 years has been to sell, not buy, so I guess it made some sense from
where they were headed at the time (though I'm still not at all sure where
that is under Compaq, more consumer "slick" products to compete with Dell?
Less emphasis on the server division? What!)

>Dell now re-sells Alpha-based Netapp servers. Netapp developers (core
developers of NSF) really wring the >performance from Alpha processors.

Yeah, most of the Dell guys didn't even know they were indirectly promoting
and profiting of Alpha, isn't it a strange world? I have a lot of respect
for Sun developers in general since they do a good job of optimizing code
for UltraSPARC (well, usually). However, UltraSPARC is a lightweight 64
bit RISC compared with Alpha, the late model 64 bit MIPS (notably the
R10000 and up) and the HP PA RISC 8500. The old saying goes "if you want to
learn to start a good fire with two sticks, learn with the green wood."
Those NetApp guys got ahold of some dry cedar and started a blaze.

Network Appliance has not only shown that it is possible
>to make lots of money being on the "bleeding edge", but that it is also
>possible
>to build robust platforms. Netapp filers back up companies like Yahoo and
>Amazon.

I love the idea of the platform and have implemented their products in my
proposals to clients and in the book I'm working on. Its not a classical
OS/hardware platform combination but rather as the name implies an
appliance using an embedded OS. Like your telecom experience has probably
shown, embedded OS/apps are the way to go for support, development and
stability reasons. Plus, you can use the new large capacity FeRAMs to
store upwards of 1GB of embedded OS, apps and data files at near memory
speeds (under 150 ns) when properly implemented.

> The company formed by the OpenBoot developers (out of Sun) is called
>"Firmworks" , www.firmworks.com . I suspect Firmworks has rights to the
>original Alpha port, though the guy who did it moved to Network Appliance
>during the process.

This is important, I shall evangelize them and make some connections
between API and these people. If nothing else, it should "make the
girlfriend jelous" so to speak and get Compaq thinking about what dragging
their feet on a common ALpha firmware will produce in the ALpha clone
markets if all of the sudden there is a choice.

> Please don't put Network Appliance name on the "mailing list", just out

>but Firmworks is fair game. I am on good terms with Compaq (I think)
>so anyone could contact me. Aaron Bauch is till with Compaq, though
>in a different group, and was the liaison to "Hudson" during the porting
>effort. Ed Freedman ran (may still run), the group which produces the
>EBSDK (Eval Board Software Dev Kit).

Aaron Bauch was involved? Wonder why he hasn't spoken up, maybe I should
check on the ALpha/NT list to which he is definitely subscribed.

> The product Netapp used for their first Alpha platform was

>platform with a 500 MHz 164 processor. Netapp fortunately choose
>to develop their own 264 boards, much as I tried to drag our group
>into the "real world". and have us build the board Netapp wanted.

I really like the DMCC stuff, by the way, could you explain to me about the
use of the "Interrupt Acceleration" mode (I know there were 4 channels and
that these were used to attach addtional modules, not for acceleration I/O
devices). How many PCI devices could the PCI based (not PICMG stuff) 164'
machines in terms of concurrent busmasters, slots and available hardware IRQs?

> Our division was sold to Smart Modular Computers
>in July, which was in-turn bought by Solectron about 3 weeks ago. I
>choose to bail even though Smart Modular is in my neighborhood.
>I didn't have confidence that Smart Modular could be successful
>in the CompactPCI business. In retrospect I could have made
>a little pile when the Smart Modular stock jumped before the
>purchase.by Solectron (which also just bought Force).
> Ironically, I am now surrounded by CompactPCI development
>as the telecom industry moves to CompactPCI
>en-mass.

Yeah, I know all about that. The OEM division was my favorite part of
Digital (outside of the AlphaServer and StorageWorks divisions). I tend to
follow what happens with those engineers and the technology they created
pretty carefully.

> What is your involvement with Alpha?

I'm a long term supporter of the Alpha platform and most things (but
obviously not all) Digital innovation created. My primary interests in
Alpha came from OpenVMS and later Windows NT, I was one of those hit hard
by the Alpha/NT support loss since it constituted most of my old business
(I've since moved alot to Sun UltraSPARC/Solaris, Alpha/Linux and am
working on getting my certs for Alpha/OpenVMS). I'm currently writing a
book called " The Alpha Platform Handbook" and am constantly looking for
resources, especially now that I've had to carve about 300 pages that were
already finished and approved by IDG Books (mostly Alpha/Windows 2000
content) and reallocate chapters and info to Linux and OpenVMS (low and
high end, respectively).

I've contributed to the creation (both directly and indirectly) of quite a
few Alpha utils and applications that may not have otherwise existed. The
NT Recovery tools from System Internals (headed up by Mark Russinovich) was
one in particular that I felt proud to make the "match" between BEI's Paul
Bunn (UltraBac, a fully Alpha and Intel NT backup and image backup software
vendor). There were several less significant tools for making life on
Alpha easier, particularly under Windows NT. I've been working with Aaron
Sakovich (runs the Alpha/NT webiste) to create FAQs and HOWTOs as well as
collaboration on some NT Magazine articles. The news took my breath away
having invested 5 years into technical writing about, building, selling and
support Alpha systems running WIndows NT, Linux and BSD UNIX. I lost a lot
of clients when the news came, many felt betrayed by COmpaq and mislead by
me or in any case infurianted that they had no warning as to the fate of
their multi-thousand dollar computing investments.

I'm busilly trying to simultaneously find information to rebuilt my book
project as well as create migration paths and tools for those running
Alpha/NT. My search led me back to OpenVMS at the high end and Linux at
the low end (BSD UNIX has become a pretty minor player with all the dev
going to Linux). I'm also trying to make time in my growing family for
certification on Open VMS 7.2 Galaxy and Tru 64 UNIX with Tru Cluster.
I'll probably take on Tandem certification when Tandem goes Alpha in the
EV7 timeline assuming that OS remains seperate and distinct from Tru 64
"Non Stop" which is becoming increasingly less likely.

I'd like to have some of your insights into this project as it continues to
development. My "modified" release date has now been pushed back to
February (compensating for many, many wasted months on now useless Alpha/NT
and Alpha/Windows 2000 information). If you can give me insights into the
path of Alpha in the industrial and telecom computing markets (especially
running VxWorks) I'd been overwhelmingly appreciative. Not too many of the
contacts I have know the application of Alpha in the telecom and OEM
markets anymore (for obvious reasons).

If you would prefer phone conversations as opposed to lengthy emails, I can
be reached at 503 605 5376 (yes, I'm living right on the doorstep of the
evil empire of Intel in Beaverton, OR).

Hope to talk with you soon,

--Kirk Erichsen
