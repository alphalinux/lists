>Where are you getting this info that Compaq co-developed Slot-B, Because
>to the extent of my knowlegde they did not (and I work for Alpha Processor
>Inc)?

Actually, my understanding is that Digital had the original plans and
Samsung (pre API creation) liked the idea enough to run with it. Samsung
and AMD finally agreed upon the specs and created some initial testbeds
including the Slot A and Slot B concepts.

This apparently isn't all that important anymore since AMD has publiclly
backed away from the Slot B concept that would have made a generic, low
cost (many Taiwanese vendors had signed on) motherboard designed for it
capable of accepting either a K7 Anthon or a EV6x Alpha. In theory, all
you would need to do is flash the EEPROM with a new firmware and get either
an Athon PC BIOS or an Alpha firmware console. As far as volume Alpha sales
through Slot B is concerned, AMD has just scuttled it, any new players in
the AMD K7 market are going to build for the electrically identical but
physically dissimilar Slot A format and another slot as yet unreleased.
API is now the sole builder for Slot B systems with apparently no takers on
building Slot B boards for the comparitively low volume Alpha business.
Strike two for our beloved platform.

>>If Compaq
>> doesn't adopt Slot B in ANY of its product offerings, what kind of signal
>> does that send? It would be like Mercedes Benze and Bosch co-developing
>> Disc brakes only to continue to use less efficient drum brakes (which
>> didn't happen). Why develop something for someone else to use and gain
>> nothing from it directly? As for SRM licensing going around several times,
>> someone needs to stop and consider the ramifications of NOT licensing SRM:

STRIKE above (I just found out an hour ago), now there isn't much point.

>Well I'm afraid there's little I can do there other than continue voicing
>my opinions (to anyone that will listen).

And voice them loudly sir, maybe something amazing will happen and this
won't be a total disaster after all.

>> 1) AlphaBIOS died with NT, no more AlphaBIOS
>Ummm...we'll see.

I just spoke with AlphaBIOS development team leaders at Compaq's DecWest,
AlphaBIOS and the code for Windows 2000 Alpha is frozen. The last version
of AlphaBIOS is 5.7, end of story. About 2 engineers are left to fix
problems that might plague the development teams working on 64 bit Windows
for Microsoft. Other than that, it is finished. If API wants to keep it,
it had better buy it now to keep it up to date. You could rip out the NT
boot code and make it more suitable for Linux or BSD UNIX (who have no
equiv of MILO).

>> 2) Continuing to ship AlphaBIOS on systems means only Linux can be used
>> (with MILO) even though it is inferior to SRM (designed to address UNIX and
>> VMS)
>Not true. Somebody could write a different loader to replace MILO.
Possible, but in about 6 months AlphaBIOS is going to be pretty dated
without modification which is going to hold up new board products unless
API either buys the AlphaBIOS code and modifies it as they see fit or go
with a completely different firmware entirely (homegrown if necessary).

>> 3) ARCSBIOS was owned by Deskstation, Deskstation is dead and it was NT
>> firmware anyway.
>
>Yup, not much future for ARCS BIOS.

Deskstation may be in the market to sell of ARCSBIOS if Compaq doesn't play
ball. With the money pains they are in, they may sell for a song. That at
least would be homegrown in a sense since Samsung co-wrote that firmware.
I still say an SRM or even a stripped down, graphical SRM would be better
for the long term. Long term thinking is desperately need right now, we
are flying blind on this platform with no one providing answers on changing
of the guards or migraiton paths (for those invested in NT, finding an OS
with comparable tools, applications and services is a tall order, VMS is
the obvious one at the high end, Linux at the low). Its a sorry position to
be in, but its all up to you (API) to get the ball rolling and Alpha's into
the hands of cosumers.

>> 4) SRM is a superior firmware (with the only aspect being the lack of
>> filesystem support at the firmware level ala ARC/AlphaBIOS) and would make
>> a nice standard.

>That's a tough call to make. While I agree that SRM is technically
>superior (I like it myself), people want Alpha to be a volume player and
>they want it to be easier to use (and install Linux on) and AlphaBIOS has
>a much friendlier interface than SRM, especially for people that are new
>to Alpha.

Alpha is hardly in a position to be a volume player now that AMD has
welched on their end of the deal. The Slot B cartridge concept would have
created a huge slew of boards that were "Alpha ready" even if they shipped
with an Anthon and a PC BIOS flashed onto their EEPROMs. This promise is
gone, its up to API to figure out something else that can get volume up
which leads us (at least in part) back to the firmware crisis: No new
AlphaBIOS and API's currently limited access to the SRM console (which is
licensed code that you have to pay for, like a chunk of OS code). Along
with this comes the PALcode issue which is a similar stinker on Slot B
boards to support the OS. The PALcode in the existing MILO is based on the
oldest samples of PALcode that come from Digital UNIX 3.2 and are samples
that come with the EBSDK. Clearly, this is not a long term solution.
Either Compaq cuts a deal on SRM and allows API to modify it as they see
fit for their Slot B designs or sell AlphaBIOS for same, either way its up
to Compaq and they have not been showing much interest in workstations or
the clone market in general. It may be months before they even figure out
they've orphaned you.

>> 5) Not licensing SRM to Slot B vendors KILLS Alpha because the clone market
>> will never be able to grow without Compaq support and SRM code.

>I can't agree with that. SRM is one (of many) firmwares out there for the
>Alpha and I feel Alpha can succeed with or without SRM. Now, when you say
>'licensing' do you mean for a fee or 'here you can have this and
>redistibute it for no cost'?

I count only 4 firmware, and 3 of them are for NT (ARC firmware, the
oldest, AlphaBIOS the newer window based firmware and ARCSBIOS which was
the Deskstation/Samsung orphan found only on the 164UX and 164BX). SRM is
the only logical choice for the reasons I have already given. I just hope
that Compaq realizes how important it is for them to license their overly
protected firmware and PALcode implementations.

>> 6) Changing over to OpenBoot firmware will be lenghty, expensive and
>> troublesome, the only reason to do this would be to work AROUND Compaq
>> mangement, but this is nearly impossible. No Alpha firmware for the Slot B
>> market means, once again NO Alpha. Where are these commitments we're
>> hearing about, are we the only ones to see the glaring holes?

I was grasping at straws with this one. It may still be necessary for API
to go OpenBoot if Compaq doesn't play ball and hold out on the firmware
code. You would still need access to their PALcode however, so you're
still stuck with Compaq's current management issues for better or worse.
The investment will be significant since there will need to be a staff of
full time programmers and testing/QC people all the time to keep things
updated. Makes you want to tear hair out and I was just starting to get
positive about my favorite platform (and bread and butter) again.

>Sorry, System Refernece Manual....it's been a long day.

I'll buy that, but here is a definition guaranteed to confuse anyone who's
trying to get Compaq's take on the Linux OS on their hardware:

http://www.digital.com/alphaserver/linux/install_guide.html

Choose the install PDF file for the System Resource Monitor reference
(practically every page).

Aaron Sakovich and Terry Shannon's (regular posters on the Alph/NT list)
take on this is similar to my own that something major has to be done by
the customer base of the Alpha platform to finally get some attention and
get things done. I've sent many senior techs questions on how they intend
to grow the clone market with API and on its own with no real answers yet.
The walls are shaking at Compaq and I don't expect any responses for some
time (if at all) on these issues.

If I can render any assistance to API via the work I'm doing now on a
handbook, I shall provide it. There are ways of getting what you want,
public awareness being one of the more powerful.

Lets make some noise, I've lost my shirt already, I'm not losing the
trousers too.
