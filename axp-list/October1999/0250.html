On Tue, 12 Oct 1999, Mike Stanczyk wrote:
> I'm contemplating getting a cable modem from the local provider. (AT&T formerly
> TCI) However, there's one catch, they want to install it on a Windows 9x/NT
> platform. I really don't want them screwing with my other Windows machines.
> So the idea is: Since I have a Multia dual booting Linux and WinNT, let the
> cable people install on the WinNT partition, then setup Linux at my own
> pace.

That worked for me... most cablemodems are really ethernet devices, so
they can run on just about any hardware.

I booted my SX into NT before the installers came out, and didn't mention
a word about Linux. Interestingly, the sales rep at Comcast knew
perfectly well what an Alpha is (he's a former DEC employee, go figure)
but the service order clearly said "Pentium". I doubt the installers ever
even heard of Alpha. Once they saw the familiar WinNT desktop they knew
what to do, though I had disabled FX!32 so their CD-ROM wouldn't run...
they just shook their heads and went to the Network control panel applet
to finish the install.

@HOME issues a static address to each customer, for now, even though they
configure each box for DHCP. (When I later called tech support, one of
their first suggestions was to disable DHCP.) The "media kit" was shipped
to me in advance, along with a printout of my host parameters, including
host/domain name, IP address, subnet mask, name/mail/proxy servers, i.e.
everything I need for Linux ;)

Linux worked just fine, of course... in fact when I had problems later due
to a bad gateway, I used Linux to run all diagnostics (arp, traceroute,
etc.) while I was on the phone with tech support (_they_ knew what Linux
is, and seemed almost glad I was using it).

The only obstacle was convincing the installers that my tulip was "good
enough" and they didn't need to rip apart my machine and install one of
their generic PCI cards (Linksys, I think).
