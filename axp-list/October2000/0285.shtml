The SunPCI card is quite amusing actually. All it is is an AMD processor,
RAM, Video, sound, etc. that is all integrated to one PCI card. The only
parts of the sun machine that it actually uses are the hard drive (your
'virtual' drive is a file on the hdd [kinda like VMware]), the CDRom (which
doesn't always apear correctly for all things you try to run) and the
floppy. It can interface through Sun's video adapter or you can run it
through it's own on board video adapter. but again, the only processing that
the Sun CPU is actually doing is just the interface (which runs under
Xwindows) to the card which actually runs all the x86 apps. If I remember
right, for the price of the SunPCI card, you could buy a modest desktop and
monitor and run just as fast without having to deal w/ compatibility issues.

I agree with you on the pricing. Alpha based computers are a lot more
expensive than Intels. One of the biggest things that I've heard is 'if only
they cost less I'd buy one'. Sun Microsystems is actually more expensive
typically than even Alpha though. Their advantage is that they have a HUGE
application base to keep their OS/ arch going, where AlphaLinux just doesn't
have the application broadness that both Intel (winblows) and Sun
Microsystems (solaris) have built for themselves.

I wouldn't expect to see someone with a Sun machine as their home computer
either though. There's no way to justify the cost / learning curve
associated with them. Home computer users typically want something that they
can turn on, surf the web with, do some word processing with, and maybe play
some games. Then you turn to the gamers, which are probably one of the
bigger drives in the computer industry right now for more horsepower and
graphics. Sun hasn't gotten all that much faster until just recently with
their UltraSParc-III's because they haven't had the need to. All of their
applications typically will run on anything from UltraSparc on up (even
HyperSparc depending on the App). They don't run games in solaris. Even as
compute servers, you can always just throw more CPUs at the problem and fork
the jobs.

Sun, SGI, and DEC never designed their products to be on the desk of the
home PC user. They were designed as workstations for business and or servers
for business where they were suited to doing one type of work. I used to
work at one place where the only thing they used Alphas for was for GIS
(Geographical Information Systems). There was one group of 10 or so people
that used them in the 700+ employee company. Most of the desktops were
AS200's with an AS2100 as the database/file server.

The older technologies are being retired because current 32bit processors
are now able to equal or better the performance of them.

Kurt Ludwig

Subject: Positioning the Alpha processor

A few small segments of the marketplace have been into 64-bit computing for
a
while now--mostly for large-scale, highly-specialized applications. Over
the
next couple of years, however, we are likely to see the mainstream begin to
move to 64-bits as well (with the introduction of ia64)--that is, an ISA
fully
compatible with the majority of current applications but transitioning to a
new paradigm of computing. My view is that competing 64-bit technologies
(alpha, sun, sgi, etc.) already have the edge on any upcoming technology,
but
they are not positioned to take advantage of the mainstream. That is, these

next several years will provide a unique opportunity for already established

64-bit technologies to branch out and take advantage of the rookie ia64
bids.
How is Alpha prepared to address the mainstream in the next several years?
If
people are going to upgrade their computers, how can we make it more
advantageous to get an Alpha than an itanium?
Even the low-cost variants, like UPxx00, are still very expensive for the
majority of computer owners. In fact, the majority of people don't even buy

the fastest computer available; there's an entire market segment devoted to
the "entry-level" machine. In order to address current 64-bit computing
technologies' lack of volume, don't we have to appeal to the majority?
Maybe
we are retiring good technologies (LX,SX,etc.), in favor of the latest and
greatest, too fast to be able to appeal to the majority. Or are we content
to
just appeal to the high end?
It seems to me that we need to provide solutions for more than just the high

end. The business sector is seeking to meet a specific set of requirements
for general office computing; the home computing market is seeking to meet
yet
another set of requirements. No current 64-bit technology seems to address
all of these, and none are in the same price range as x86 variants.
In addition to competitive pricing, there should be other incentives to
switching platforms. As an example, Sun has the SunPCi add-on which
"seamlessly" (according to them) integrates Windows programs into the
Solaris
operating environment. We don't have anything like that for Alpha, do we?
I brought up these points in an attempt to foster good discussion on salient

issues. These are the questions that have been running around my head for a

while now. Comments are welcome.
-Chaz
