Positioning the Alpha as a Market Leader

The Alpha traditionally has an image of being a beast: ferocious performance
with a price that will mangle a budget. Obviously that's not entirely the
case, but there is no marketing campaign to change the mainstream views about
it. If the ultimate goal of any product is to make that product accessible
and appealing to the mainstream, then it's the mainstream user that must be
targeted. Kurt mentioned the gamers as being a major driving force in desktop
computing today. What gamer wouldn't jump at the chance to "upgrade" to a
more powerful platform, as long as that platform provided the same
functionality as the current one and gave better performance at a reasonable
price? This would lead to a conclusion that the people responsible for
promoting Alpha (Compaq, API, the user community, etc.) should be concerned
about (a) investing in adding the functionality (to the Alpha application
base) that already exists on x86, (b) investing in the technology that would
pull mainstream users away from their x86s and toward the Alpha, and (c)
advertising Alpha as a viable alternative, overcoming the beast mentality,
etc. To some extent this is happening, but on a very small scale; a scale
that strikes me as small compared to the size of Compaq and its competitors.
It has been a long time since I've seen a mainstream advertising campaign for
the Alpha--not even any ads in PC Magazine! Where is the media buzz about
Alpha these days?

> I say forget the desktop market. It will always be driven by commodity,
> low-margin parts. Build good servers instead, and let market forces decide.
Shouldn't we try to address the desktop market, if that's where the real
volume of sales is? Maybe that's a longer-term goal.
Perhaps not the home desktop market, but what about the business desktop?
This discussion is about positioning the Alpha to be a market leader as the
x86 era slowly comes to a draw. Something will fill that void, but what will
it be.

> The metrics people seem to use are price/performance and availability of
> software.
So these are the things we need to work on, then, to make Alpha a viable
mainstream option for businesses. This is something we could work on now to
position the Alpha properly for the near-term and mid-term.
So let's take software availability. Yes, we have a jump on infant
technologies, but not *that* much of a jump. How could Compaq create a "huge
application base" like Kurt mentioned Sun has? Yes, highly specialized
applications are available and the Linux community is working diligently to
provide things that have traditionally been lacking.

> The older technologies are being retired because current 32bit processors
> are now able to equal or better the performance of them.
So perhaps even Alpha's incredible performance is not enough of a draw for the
average power-hungry consumer? What sort of a roadmap do we see for Alpha
software development as 32bit processors are retired because of new
OS/application requirements? This seems like something we should be eminently
concerned with.

I am very happy with Compaq's commitment to giving us high-performance
compilers, jumpstart CDs, and the like. However, in order to develop a large
application base, additional investment in areas that Windows is "good" at:
like support for multimedia hardware and software, comprehensive office
productivity suites, etc. (things I'm sure most of us Alpha users have thought
"I sure wish I could do ..." about).
