"Charles L. Chandler" wrote:
> How is Alpha prepared to address the mainstream in the next several years? If
> people are going to upgrade their computers, how can we make it more
> advantageous to get an Alpha than an itanium?

The metrics people seem to use are price/performance and availability of
software.

Alpha has a jump on IA-64 for software, certainly. We don't know yet how
quickly IA-64 will actually penetrate the market.

Fast emulation of x86 would certainly help Alpha. Something like FX!32 running
on Linux could be a powerful incentive. Of course, the availability of good
emulation is also a disincentive for ISV's to actually port software, which was
a big problem for Alpha/NT and will affect IA-64 as well.

> Even the low-cost variants, like UPxx00, are still very expensive for the
> majority of computer owners. In fact, the majority of people don't even buy
> the fastest computer available; there's an entire market segment devoted to
> the "entry-level" machine.

IA-64 won't come cheap either. The mainstream will continue to run x86 for some
time.

UPxx00 boxes are actually quite reasonable, especially the new UP1100 mb's.

> In order to address current 64-bit computing
> technologies' lack of volume, don't we have to appeal to the majority? Maybe
> we are retiring good technologies (LX,SX,etc.), in favor of the latest and
> greatest, too fast to be able to appeal to the majority. Or are we content to
> just appeal to the high end?

I say forget the desktop market. It will always be driven by commodity,
low-margin parts. Build good servers instead, and let market forces decide.
