OK, so one fundamental misunderstanding was that a $500 chip estimate got
translated into a $500 motherboard+chip estimate. That's a pretty big
difference since it ignores the cost of nine 4 ns SRAMs (assuming a 4 MB
cache), 7 high pin count custom ASICs, the integrated controllers for audio,
Ethernet, & UW SCSI, etc, etc.

Of course, the key thing missing in your trusted friend's cost estimate is
quantity. There's no fab in the world that would quote without an
associated quantity, because that determines how they amortize their fixed
costs (the $1B+ capital investment in the fab, design costs, etc).

If anyone wants to look at pure marginal manufacturing cost without respect
to any of these real world economics, you can use the figures published by
MicroDesign Resources, the publishers of Microprocessor Report. They
consider nothing but die size, process, and their guess as to yields. More
importantly, they publish these figures in their newsletter and are willing
to have them attributed to them (i.e. they're not anonymous).

Here are some of MDR's estimates for a few of the more popular
microprocessors:

        21264A $160
        PIII Xeon $40
        Athlon $105 (inc. 512KB L2 cache)
        Power3 $320
        G4 $35
        R12K $140
        PA8500 $330

As you can see, your friend's estimate is three times what the "pros"
estimate. Of course these numbers are mostly of academic interest, because
in the real world you DO have to amortize all your fixed costs and provide a
return to the investors who put up the billions of dollars of capital to
build your fabs and hire your top flight engineers.

You were to look purely at the manufacturing cost, without considering any
of the other costs, one would think that $100 Coppermines or $250 Athlons
would be a no brainer - after all, that's 2.5X markup which ought to be
plenty for any reasonable company. How soon do folks think we'll see <$100
Pentium IIIs?

Now, I'm hardly one to be defending Compaq, but there are plenty of things
that they can be blamed for without inventing new ones.
