        Two or three years ago, I would have agreed with you outright
regarding the need for serious marketing consultation. However, Linux is
now trendy and a marketable commodity among corporate big-wigs. With an
estimated installed base of 10-15 million users (and keep in mind that
probably only 50% of Linux /actually/ participate in those polls), Compaq
would have been foolish not to throw their support behind Linux.
        AlphaLinux is a different story, though. Whereas Linux is now a
household name thanks to media exposure, AlphaLinux is more obscure due to
the lack of good marketing decisions by DEC when Alpha technology first
emerged. DEC has always been funny that way, more reserved in their
marketing efforts. Why? Because Alpha users typically chose DEC
technology on the basis of technical merits rather than a marketeer's
smooth tounge. AlphaLinux follows suit similarly, attracting users who
are not impressed by dancing Intel engineers in colored spacesuits.
In other words, AlphaLinux does not need the marketing expertise of Compaq
to continue as an excellent platform to run Linux on. What AlphaLinux
does need, however, is the /technical/ support of Compaq in the arena of
optmized compilers at reasonable price, availability of technical
documents, pre-release information for Linux developers, etc.
