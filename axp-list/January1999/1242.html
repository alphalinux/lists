> I've been salivating over getting an alpha workstation for some years
> now.
        Ditto that. The stability of Linux/axp was a prohibitive factor
for me a couple years ago when considering a system from Aspen. To the
credit of Linux/axp stability, Linus is running an Alpha (600/xxx) at home
alongside his other toys. In general, software will be of higher quality
if the developers have to rely on it's proper operation to get work done.
> My work is frequently heavily math intensive, especially with
> floating point. Now I'm very seriously considering making the switch, and
        Sounds like my work as well. My platform of choice for
fp-intensive things is SGI (8 processor Origin2000, 4 processor Onyx2) or
a cluster of 40 or so Suns simply because that's what we have available at
work.
> have been investigating prices and options from various Linux-friendly
> vendors. Looking at the prices, I'm starting to wonder if it is really
> "worth it". I could build a 400 mhz K6-based system w/ SCSI and lots of
> RAM for probably $1000. The basic Alpha system I'm looking at (533 mhz
        Not sure what PC vendor you are referring to, but the lowest price
I could come up with for a K6/400 system with 256M/9G UW-SCSI disk
subsystem lay around $1600 without monitor. Guess I just choose not to
skimp when it comes to designing a personal system.
> the Intel Pentium III) in the office for about 6 months now, and they
> are a joke. It's time for x86 to leave my desktop.
        My chips lay with the AMD K7 if/when it is released. One thing
that really sickens me is that from an architectural perspective, x86 has
not changed much at all in the two decades since the 8086. They refine
the fab process, crank it up to higher speeds, add marketing buzzword this
and that functionality but in reality, nothing new is to be found under
the sun.
> I've been following the threads about people "reventing" back to
> x86, and it sounds like there's argument either way. What I was hoping
> for it to get those same people to give some specifics as to why
> one or the other is better.
        The Linux/x86 user community is many times larger than the
Linux/axp community. For that reason, the x86 people get the latest
greatest candy first in the Linux world. No bones about it, x86 is where
you want to be if you cannot do without someone to hold your hand and walk
you through problems.
> 3D acceleartion will finally be a reality for the Alpha. (Currently I do all
> my stuff on the 3dfx series of 3d accelerators, but from the looks of it
> Glide is not going to make it to the Alpha anytime soon. A true shame.)
        Metrolink's Extreme3D product will be released for Linux/axp
probably within one month. Until that time, I'll gladly use their MetroX
server (what is it, $30 now?).
> So - is it worth it? Will I see enough of a speedup from a (say) PII-266
> to a 21164-533? What about from a PII-450 to a 21164-533? Better yet,
> what's going to make the difference in speed - would it be better to go
> up in mhz (say, to 600 or more) or to get a motherboard with more cache?
> I'm already planning on UW-SCSI and at least 256 megs of RAM, of course.
        You are comparing apples and oranges. Read my last post regarding
benchmarking. It really, really (really, folks!) depends on what your
application is. For floating-point intensive code, the Alpha will win
hands down when pitted against a P2/266 - especially on large matrix
manipulations or bitonic searches. But compared to a P2/450, I can't
really say. Don't have one running Linux to compare it to.
        For other things, the x86 may win over the Alpha. The best thing
to do is find a friend with an x86 system like the one you might build
and one with an Alpha like you might purchase. Bring along your code,
compile on both, and compare side-by-side. Spend a couple hours in front
of each one. Contrary to what the marketing droids want you to believe,
system performance comparison is a highly technical art and not one which
can be summarized by spec95foobar and specbob this and that.
        Here's my over-generalized concluding comparison. If you want
compatibility with the rest of the Linux/GNU world 100% of the time, play
a lot of games, and cannot do without DOS or Windows applications, x86 is
the only way to go. At least at the present time. If you code in your
sleep, can stand portability problems and fix them, and your fp-intensive
code runs faster on the Alpha - go with the Alpha.
