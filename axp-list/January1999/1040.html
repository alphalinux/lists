I have to say, I almost totally agree with you. The part about risk
assessment (just rephrasing what you seem to be saying), and compromise
between services and security... well said. Also, the part about
physical security.... very true too.

However, as anyone working in the security field would know, these days,
the philosophy of "security thru obscurity" simply carries too high of a
risk, even if you're protecting yourself from so called "kiddie
scripts." (i've personally never heard of that phrase) With all the
information available on the internet, and the ease of access these
days, the kid in the garage cracker can easily go beyond using a few
well known hacks. It's easy enough to find step-by-step instructions on
building a bomb on the internet, is it that much harder to go beyond
some simple scripted attacks?

Also, even though I have no statistics to prove anything (nor do
statistics necessarily prove anything)... but the increase number of
attacks in the past few years (mostly proportional to the increase
population on the net)... should also raise a few concerns. You now have
a greater population of crackers, this could mean many things. First,
you should suspect that you have a greater variety of cracker
personalities (if you're profiling your attacker). This could mean very
persistent and intelligent kids. Second, collaborative cracking
attacks... this we've seen quite a bit last year already. With such
collaborative efforts, do you think they will all simply use scripted
attacks? With the exchange of ideas and methods, I would think they
could easily go beyond your simple scripted attacks.

Also, some of the recent attacks I'm aware of involve sniffers. So even
if you're on an Alpha, if you run something like telnet, or
non-anonymous ftp with a user account over a public network, you're
screwed.

About a year ago, I had just gotten a cable modem service, and was
running a windows 95 box on it. I had ftp running so that I could
transfer files from work with an non-anonymous account, with user and
password different from my higher security accounts. I just wanted to
keep warez people from uploading to every anonymous ftp site, but still
be able to ftp myself a few files from work (low security docs).

Well one day, someone somehow was able to execute a change in the win95
registry remotely, subsequently gaining anonymous ftp access. They then
uploaded an executable containing a virus. And that was it... clearly
the work of some kid screwing around. Fortunately, I never executed the
virus, and during a standard virus scan, it was found and deleted. I
later checked my logs and via some social engineering with the ISP,
found out it was some college kid living in southern california. I
contacted the kid to tell him I knew it was him, he got a little scared,
and was surprised that anyone could track him down. He apologized,
almost sounding like he pissed in his pants... so I figure it was enough
and since no real damage was done, nor did i have anything important on
my win95 box, I let him go... actually, he and I keep in touch and are
more or less buddies. Anyways, the point is, your typical college kid
found some way to change the win95 registry remotely... that to me is a
pretty sofisticated attack... well, at least it still beats me as to how
he did that. I've never seen this attack in the standard security
bulletins... maybe I missed something, but at least to me, this is
beyond running a few standard scripts.

Ok.. this is getting way off topic... anyone wanting to discuss security
with me, just email me directly,... I think I've had enough of this
discussion on the list.

D.

>>Why Alpha? A couple of my development projects need 64bit support.
>>Most of the rest is security thru obscurity -- few to none of the
>>script kiddie hacks work on Alphas (they rely on one or more of x86,
>>little-endian, 32bit). This lets me be a little more sloppy in my
>>security maintenance and still keep a working system on the public
>>Internet.
>
>There has been a little debate on the list lately becuse of this
>statement. I'll defend it too :)
>
>It is sort of true that using an Alpha over Intel is security through
>obscurity.
>
>However, I'll tell you what .. if I wanted root on most anyone's
machine
>bad enough, I could probably get it.. even people that follow CERT
>religiously and who patch every known hole in the software and OS.
>
>How? The hardware is probably insecure. I walk into your house, sit at
>the console and boot to a floppy. I have root. I mount your partition,
>change /etc/shadow, and reboot. You are hosed. If you have a BIOS
password
>it'll take me a few more minutes to pull the CMOS RAM battery .. but
>if I have the console, I basically have root. Most people have little,
if
>any, home security.
>
>Now, I don't want to get off on a security tangent here.. but this is a
>parallel to Intel/Alpha security issues.
>
>Most attackers who will break into your home don't give a crap about
root.
>They'll take the machine itself, pawn it off and they don't need root.
>Most attackers who want root, won't break into your home.
>
>Therefore to keep someone from getting root on your box, you don't need
to
>live on a military base and keep your machine in a bank vault. Physical
>compromise isn't a likely attack scenario for most people.
>
>Similarly, for many servers, scripted attacks are the only ones they
have
>to worry about. For the guys that can get into any box because they
write
>the scripts to begin with, they will have more interesting targets.
They
>aren't in the attack scenario.. script kiddies running around with the
>new 0-day DoS attack are the ones that will likely hit your machine.
>
>Using an Alpha stops them.
>
>If you're protecting nuclear launch codes, you're working in a
different
>model .. if you're just trying to run a box to keep a smooth internet
>connection going, and don't necessarily have secrets to hide,
protecting
>from script kiddies is fine, so long as you don't make for yourself
some
>intelligent enemies and change your threat model all around again.
>
>Myself, I have known vunerabilities in software that I use. I accept
these
>vunerabilities because the only two users of the machine are myself and
>a trusted friend (who has root via sudo anyhow .. so if he really wants
>to get flashy and get a root shell through a 'sploit instead .. who
>cares?). So, I don't worry about local attacks .. instead I keep remote
>access tied down. SSH happens to be the only way in from unknown
networks,
>and telnet w/ OPIE is as good as it gets for trusted networks.. all
other
>daemons are firewalled. And, really, OPIE would be good enough for me
..
>but I like playing with crypto so I set SSH up just to do it. A
different
>threat model than if I had a hundred and twenty users to worry about.
>
>Real security is about balancing use vs. security.. because, if you
pursue
>"security" (paranoia is a better word) fully, you'll bury your box
under
>30 feet of concrete and steel with no network connection and no users
(not
>even yourself). Because as long as there _is_ a way in, there is a
>possibility for unauthorized access.
>
>It's all about threats and acceptable models for an application,
folks..
>not some imaginary end of "absolute security" .. it doesn't exist.. for
>reasons closely tied to stuff like Godel's Incompleteness Theorem or
>Turing's Halting Problem.. its just something you have to live with.
>
>Now, I better cover this now, because someone invariably says it .. the
>OTP is an absolutably secure cryptosystem, yes. But an OTP doesn't help
>you when an attacker takes some .. compromising pictures .. of your
>secretary or attorney, or .... and gets her to steal the pad. Its only
>absolutably secure in a given model, namely in a model where
eavesdropping
>on the ciphertext and even some (or all) knowledge of the plaintext is
>the only attacks. An OTP gives no message authentication .. if you're
>worried about that sort of thing, you need to use a MAC too.. it
doesn't
>help against bribery or torture.
>
>You have to pick your likely threats.
>
