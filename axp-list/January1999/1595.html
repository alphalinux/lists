> Dan Morrill wrote:
> >
> >
> > I have read that the "UNIX" clock's mechanism of counting the seconds
> > since January 1, 1970 will exceed the range of the 32 bit field used to
> > store it on January 19, 2038 at 3:14:07.
>
> Watch what you read. If you would read this list then you would
> notice something I posted a few days ago on this list.
>
> $ perl -e 'print scalar gmtime 2**31, "\n"'
> Tue Jan 19 03:14:08 2038
> $ perl -e 'print scalar gmtime 2**32, "\n"'
> Sun Feb 7 06:28:16 2106
> $ perl -e 'print scalar gmtime 2**55, "\n"'
> Sun Jun 13 06:26:08 1141709097
>
> Besides this is a clear application and library issue and has nothing
> to do with "UNIX". Did you look, by any chance, on a range of dates
> in Oracle on 32-bit machines? Even with all deficiencies of that
> date-stamp? Just an example.

Here's an interesting one for you:

Why does this happen? ;)

> perl -e 'print scalar gmtime 0xf0c29d868bfd7f, "\n"'
Tue Dec 31 23:59:59 2147483647
> perl -e 'print scalar gmtime 0xf0c29d868bfd80, "\n"'
Wed Jan 1 00:00:00 -2147483648

Note that the only difference between those numbers is the least-significant
bit (1 year). This is about 9 bits short of using the full 64 bits.

Yes, I know the answer. But interesting to think of all the unsuspecting
people (Will there be people) 2,147,483,647 years from now, suddenly living
in negative times!
