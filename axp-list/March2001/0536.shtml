Aaron,

>I'd thought I'd share some comments, mostly in regards to the first system
>you set out as I think this is the most viable option.

Thanks for your comments. I'll respond inline.

>I think it would be important to make the Board 100% standard ATX
>complient so that any ATX case can be used. Make the board available to
>system resellers! This will allow whoever produces the board to have a
>higher production run because the same board would be used in internally
>produced machines as well as sold to vendors.

With solder-on and conventional sockets, you do sometimes require a larger
board to accommodate the CPU/cache, the system chipset, and any add-on
STP/QFP ASICs for such things as the Southbridge functions (usually the PCI
to ISA bridge, I/O controller for UARTs, parallel, USB, ATA and sometimes
audio), network, SCSI, etc. I've seen some fairly massive ATX 2.0 compliant
motherboards that still manage to make room for a massive CPU/cooling
assembly/power management-VRM and still have room for 6 PCI slots, 1 ISA
slot and have all the bells and whistles of audio, network, SCSI, etc. all
built onto the board. It can be done, its a question of how much time would
be spent on a low-cost volume part, especially if it was just a testbed and
not specifically targeting any one market or purpose. I'm not entirely
convinced that the UP1500 isn't more of a political move by Samsung than a
real product per se. API should be making motherboards, not Samsung.

>Perhaps a smaller power supply requirement? 250w? I'd like to see future
>boards not quite so power thirsty. Just a thought :)

Power demands are going up, not down. You can implement power-save modes,
but you need soft-power support in the OS. One of the hardest things Tadpole
ever did was get rudimentary power management support working in the
Alphabook 1 running OpenVMS 7.1. For safety reasons, you are always better
off with a higher rated power supply, 300 watt should be considered the
minimum for an Alpha system (the 164LX with a 600MHz CPU and 256MB of RAM
uses about 110 watts, all by itself. Add the load of a couple of 10,000
SCSI drives and you end up with a minimal requirement of about 150-180
watts, and that's without a high-power video controller that may need 15-50
watts itself.

>VMS compat. isn't as critical in this product IMHO. If some savings can be
had while by sacrificing VMS support that may be >ok. I'd say the next step
up in price VMS becomes more important.......just a thought. Although if
VMS support can be kept >then "hurray!"

>>From a practical perspective, the issues may be too complex and involve too
many legal issues to be practical. There is also the issue of modularity;
Compaq wants to sell you a "solution" when they hear you asking for an
Alpha-something-or-other. Compaq is tightening the screws on vendors who can
build OpenVMS and Tru64 compatible hardware, it wants to sell all of it
without having to share the pie. If the only OS that need be supported is
Linux, then you can even forgo SRM and boostrap directly into Linux from
SROM and eliminate another license or development expense. I suggested a
config for OpenVMS and Tru64 support solely because shops still need some
small test boxes and training on big iron is not practical. Perhaps that is
another battle for the DECUS groups to fight, fair enough.

Sun understands the need for lower-end machines and IBM is also coming to
its senses. Compaq is moving in the opposite direction and trying to make
Alpha a key differentiator with the traditional profit margins for a
proprietary RISC design with three (Tandem's Non-Stop is going Alpha native
within 24 months) proprietary OSes for it. We'll probably never see a cheap
Alpha come out of Compaq because unlike Sun (who have one in-house hardware
platform, SPARC and SPARC64) Compaq has x86 boxes at the low end. They
don't want to compete with their own Intel based products, leaving them no
room to build low tier Alpha machines. API is the only available option and
unlike Compaq, API builds systems, motherboards and provides a whole host of
services, they don't just sell "solutions," leaving room for the ISV/VAR.
        
>The important thing is to use memory that is pretty standard and cheap.
>With ALPHA's RISC design memory is probably more important than clock
>speed for many applications. The ability to expand memory cheaply
>gives these MB's a chance to live a LONG life. This is attractive for
>educational buyers who generally have a longer product replacement cycle
>than many corporations (5-7 years opposed to 3-5)

2GB of system memory probably isn't enough headroom for 5-7 years, but
memory technology turns over faster than chip generations (about every 9
months), so determining how much memory should be supported is tricky. 4GB
seems and ideal, but there is a tradeoff; supporting 2GB and larger memory
arrays almost always involves the use of registered/buffered DIMMS as
electrical load for large memory arrays goes well above the practical limits
imposed by unbuffered parts (1-2GB, I believe). I don't think either AMD's
761 or the VIA KT133A support registered memory types, though AMD's 761 does
support ECC (KT133A does not). To support 4GB now would mean having to use
more expensive "server" targeted memory, which may be rarer and even more
expensive than it is now in 36 months as stock dwindles. This brings us back
to a 2GB practical limit, assuming a useful working life of 48-56 months.

>I'd substitute "Any GOOD 3d AGP chipset that has at least 24mb of on board
>video ram and is currently supported by true64 and Linux"

Probably a good point. GeForce may be a good choice, but not to the
exclusion of all others. As far as I know, neither the ATI Radeon nor the
GeForce have bootable support thanks to their 32 bit extended VGA BIOS
routines and lack of support in most OSes that Alpha supports. For a
platform that used to be sold on the virtue of its graphics capabilities,
things have been in a pretty sorry state on the 3D graphics front for
awhile. Lack of AGP support in most Alpha platforms is only the most
obvious issue, with Compaq controlling SRM with a stranglehold, there is no
way to add 32 bit BIOS routines to support newer video controllers.

>Again substitute "Any on board 10/100 ethernet but it MUST be supported by
>True 64 and Linux"

Intel 82559 is now the "hier-apparent" to the 2114x Tulip, after all, Intel
owns BOTH. 21x4x Ethernet controllers, the 21x5x PCI to PCI bridge chips,
StrongARM and a few others all were snapped up during the Digital/Intel
sale/cross licensing. Intel 82559 is now supported In Tru64 and OpenVMS (as
of 7.2, I believe, 7.3 has been out for months).

>To this I would add a single 32 bit PCI slot also (maybe 2) Just so there
>is SOME room for expansion in the future. ATA 100 support is important,
>and a ATA chipset whose BIOS can be upgraded later (ATA 133?) on might
>also be useful (we are all dreaming here, so cut me some slack on this one
:)

To make it cheap, you'll probably want to build as many components in as
possible. Firewire will become more important than ATA (except maybe for
serial ATA, but that's based on a similar bus protocol to IEEE 1394, but
lacks industry wide support). Using two 64 bit PCI slots allows you to use
32 bit or 64 bit PCI controllers. Having 66 MHz supports means being able to
support newer 66 MHz 3.3 volt peripherals. To do this right, the PCI slot
capable of this should be on a separate bus from the internal bus. I'd say
a practical number of slots for a low price point is 2 slots, 2 PCI buses (2
slots on one, internal PCI bus on the other). A system chipset that could
support PCIx (133 MHz 64 bit PCI, each slot is its own bus, de-multiplexed
address and data, streaming modes, etc) could operate in legacy PCI 2.2/2.1
mode and support frequencies down to 25MHz, 32bit modes of operation and
regulate voltage for legacy devices from 5.0 volts to 2.9 volts (PCIx).
 
>Couldn't we use LINUX in flash the way API is now? This may/would save
>development time, and I'm quite sure you could teach linux to load Tru 64
>unix. Since this is aimed at the lower end keeping the development costs
>down is important. Also the ability to be able to correctly identify
>future hardware that is plugged into the thing should be easier. (Grab the
>source recompile linux firmware xxx and load it up) The ability to use a
>more "open sourced" firmware loader has been discussed here before. I
>think it may have a good fit in the praticulair application.

Absolutely, the only other alternative is to do an OpenBoot-like console
based on the free implementations, like FICL. Since Linux is the primary
business driver of Alpha at the low end to mid-tier, it makes sense to have
a Linux-centric system design (especially on a Linux list). The adapted
LinuxROM project that API is using is good evidence it is a working solution
with none of the licensing ubiquities of SRM, AlphaBIOS or OpenBoot. For
Linux use, its also cleaner as there is no need for maintaining intermediate
console firmware. This also opens up what consistitutes supported hardware
as that would now include just about anything their was a Linux native
driver for.

>Also DEC was pretty darn smart with the fail safe boot loader available on
>many Alpha motherboards. I would say with the market that this is aimed
>at (hobbiest and people that like to poke around) any motherboard produced
>should have this option.

Agreed, this is one of the better reasons to own an Alpha, even an old one.
You can easily rebuild a system that has been all but physically trashed.
Fail Safe Booter (FSB) and debug firmware (POST, environment checker, etc)
are excellent features and its my understanding that IA64 platforms will
offer something similar, probably as a convenience to early ISV/IHV
developers. FSB is not present on all Alpha systems though, even some of the
newer boards.

Thanks for commenting Aaron.
