With regards to your message at 05:08 PM 4/6/01, KirkE@paccessglobal.com.
Where you stated:
>Greg,
>
> >API already has the UP1100, which uses AMD's chipset. I haven't asked,
> >but one would presume that they will continue to have a low-end
> >machine using the least expensive motherboard possible.
>
>I have been informed that UP1100 is EOL. As is the older SlotB based
>UP1000. At this point, only the Samsung product (not an API product) with a
>name dangerously similar to those built and sold by APIis slated to have the
>AMD Iron Gate G4 (761), dubiously named the UP1500. Samsung has some info on
>their site about it, though it is still under "development." At first I
>thought that the UP1500 would be a fairly innovative offering to provide the
>Alpha platform community with an "enthusiast"/ entry level programmer
>workstation or web appliance. On closer inspection however, it looks like
>it has retained the rather unusual UP1100 non-standard ATX arrangement, a
>detail pointed out to me by a colleague. This isn't much of a problem in a
>desk side workstation I should think, but would probably pose a problem in
>sourcing rack mount chassis that would fit without obscuring slots, drives
>or memory banks.
Actually the case is NOT an issue.
Yes, the PCI slots are offset from the default ATX locations, but the cost
of 2U cases for this is not exorbitant. For example our company provides a
2U with 400W power, 3 slot PCI riser, full extension slides, hotswap fans,
2 x 5.25" bays, etc. for $325.
And VERY few Alphas have ever fit into a "standard" case, so this is
nothing new..
The real issue will be wether or not API will be able to deliver in N. America.
For example the UP1100 has been available here in only the 600MHz version,
whereas Samsung has also been selling the UP1100 in a 700MHz overseas.
Considering the memory bandwidth limitations of the 1100, the extra "oomph"
of the faster processor was/is important. With the new DDR based UP1500 I
think it will be a VERY nice machine!
The only deadlock we see is if there will be enough supply of DDR cache RAM
for these to be built.. Try buying a DDR Alpha CPU module nowadays..

>Sun has managed to produce some excellent low-cost systems that are selling
>like hotcakes. The added benefit is that exposure and access ultimately
>leads to increased acceptance and a larger user base, along with the obvious
>benefit of more software (and often better software). The Sun Blade 100, the
>Netra X1 AC200 and the new line of SPARCEngine motherboards including the
>AXi and the AXe are making it easier and cheaper to buy, build or OEM an
>Ultra SPARC II based system (and soon enough, Ultra III). I bought an
>SPARCEngine AXi with a 360MHz UIIi CPU and 256MB of RAM from NU Horizons for
>little more than $700.00. An Ultra 30 coming off of lease soon followed.
>It's now possible to obtain a 500 MHz IIe system like the Netra X1 or the
>Sun Blade 100 for less than $1,000, but there is absolutely nothing similar
>in the Alpha arsenal.
The only new and interesting lower end Sun board is the AXI1105 and the Blade.
And they are not without teething issues. I know, as I have an 1105 here.
It is a nice design, but it is still not "ready for prime time". For
example the keyboard and mouse ports do not work yet! Sun assures me that a
new ROM BIOS revision will fix that "real soon now". It also requires a
"special" version of Solaris 8. And Linux does not run on it yet.

>Having broached the question more than once for a quote from most of the
>vendors still selling Alpha solutions, obtaining a 4.5 year old 164LX
>motherboard with a 533 or 600MHz EV56 CPU is usually about a $900 to $1200
>proposition, and that's before integrating an actual system together.
>Having gone this route in the past, I'm inclined to suggest to obtaining
>systems that are already fully integrated and guaranteed fully functional.
>I obtained my last Alpha through Ebay auctions and scored a PWS 600au with
>512MB memory and an 18GB drive for under $1,000 (not the going rate, even a
>year later).
>
>At current, the only practical, low cost option for Alpha platform
>enthusiasts (ranging from the stalwart OpenVMS crowd/DECUS groups, the BSD
>camp, the living-dead of the Alpha/NT camp and the Alpha/Linux camp),
>students or programmers is for obtaining out of date hardware through the
>auction sites or calling up leasing companies that occasionally give up
>Digital branded workstations and servers for a song. It could be said there
>is no Alpha workstation market anymore, but what are all those clustered
>Alpha systems that are filling the computing media being programmed on? How
>many people on this list would be willing to part with $1,500 or so to be a
>fully integrated "Sun Blade" style Alpha, just to keep their stake in the
>platform, write some code and keep their platform specific skills up? If it
>could run OpenVMS and Tru64, there would be an automatic market in the DECUS
>crowd and plenty of shops with an OpenVMS and Tru64 investment would snap
>them up. Linux users would love them as they would be price competitive with
>Athlon Thunderbirds on the X86 front. Whatever replaces the UP1x00 needs to
>address these issues, its been so long since a cost-effective solution worth
>buying has come out, they'd be all the rage. There would be dozens of ways
>to promote such a system. Sun is proof enough it can be done (remember that
>an old Ultra II based system like the Ultra Enterprise 450 workgroup
>server/workstation still fetches some $20,000 for the base unit and another
>$2800 per 450MHz CPU).

A good bet for low cost is the Compaq XP1000 units. The older 500MHz
version is available for under $6000. Good memory bandwidth, nice design.
At $13000+ the 667 they still sell is vastly overpriced. But the clear-out
500's are a good deal.

I agree that there is some level of "ball dropping" in the Alpha low cost
camp, and we need to look no further than the fact that it WILL get better.
Now is just a lousy time if you want both the latest and a cheap price..
