Greetings all,

I mentioned what I think is the collective desire for an affordable Alpha
system in a previous post. Lets go one step further with this by actually
coming up with a list of system requirements, capabilities and ultimately
cost for such a unit. You can't have it all, so the completed system will
have to be an artful compromise. A low cost Alpha is not in-and-of-itself a
real product category, it must be more clearly defined as being an entry
point to a larger class of systems with similar architecture, console and
operating system support. The ultimate goal of this task should be to
produce a working set of qualities that will make it suitable for use in
software development, testing, hardware/OS training and quite hopefully,
mind share outside the Alpha enthusiast ranks. The form factor, level of
integration and overall system cost should be similar to that of offerings
by other vendors at a similar performance level (Sun Blade, Apple iMac,
etc). The fact that many users of this list, as well as many other Alpha
lists as well, own and operate such venerable systems as the Multia (AKA
UDB) are evidence of the enduring success of such designs.

It's also important to mention that rather than suggesting merely a single
platform design, what is needed is an initiative with an ongoing commitment
to provide low cost entry points into the Alpha platform that allow system
integrators, administrators, developers and students access and experience
necessary to sustain the platform and grow the market. Building one
motherboard or system and calling it quits benefits no one. Rather than
pointing fingers or leveling blame, I believe there is a real opportunity
here to increase "mindshare" and buoy up the platform against such
successful competitors at Sun.

The suggested configuration here is just that, a general guideline of
capabilities to match the needs and expectations for a larger audience at an
under $2,000 price point (with OVMS and Tru64, OS not included. Hobbyists
will only need to pay for the media kit in a non-commercial environment).
With the case of OpenVMS, finding a working configuration can be tough given
its small list of supported video and audio devices. My suggestion is for a
system with a lower degree of integration in order to support VMS and Tru64
and using plug-in boards for video/network.

Enthusiast/Developer/Student (emphasis on Linux):

Small footprint ATX deskside chassis
        space for one internal 3.5" 80GB 7200 RPM ATA-100 drive, 1 combo MKE
DVD-RAM/DVD-ROM/CD-ROM. Optional support could be provided for a
standard floppy or an MKE LS-120 ATA-33 "floptical" drive in a
vertical position adjacent to the CD/DVD drive. 300 watt ATX power
supply
        
ATX compliant motherboard
        700 MHz EV68 or EV69 CPU/4MB DDR HSTL cache in either
non-upgradeable "soldered on" or upgradeable socketted configuration
        AMD IG-4 761 chipset (VIA chipset may become a viable option also)
        256MB 184 pin PC266 DDR SDRAM, 4 sockets (2GB max using 512MB
unbuffered ECC modules)
        Nvidia GeForce 3 MPU, AGP 4x (integrated as a soldered on AGP
device, no AGP slot is provided) with 32MB DDR
        Single port Intel 82559 Fast Ethernet
        ALi Southbridge I/O chip
        ATA-100 support (via ALi southbridge, treated as a SCSI controller
by the SRM console)
        Integrated USB, IrDA wireless, parallel, dual 9 pin serial, PS/2
keyboard and mouse ports (via ALi southbridge)
        Integrated PC97 accelerated audio (also via ALi southbridge) for 16
bit, 48KHz stereo audio
        Integrated dual port TI IEEE 1394c "Firewire" for external devices
          1 64 bit PCI slot supporting 66MHz 3.3 volt operation, isolated from
internal PCI bus (where the Firewire, ATA-100 and Fast Ethernet stem).

        Variant 1: Appliance server (Netra X1 - Alpha style)

        Space for second QFP to support Intel 82559 Fast Ethernet could be
included for using the system as a server appliance in a 2U chassis.
Omitted on the board will be the AGP video, system will be managed
"headless" through serial ports or provide video via the PCI slot. AGP
extensions disabled in the SROM/console. System could be bundled with only
the single internal ATA-100 drive and the MKE LS-120 drive but maintain
the Firewire and USB support. Implement additional system management
functions, including watchdog timer and thermal controls. Out of Band
management and remote reboot facilitated through off-the-shelf ASIC. Cost
should be only slightly higher than the X1.

Target end-user street price for system of around $$1500-$1700 for 256MB
configuration

DECUS OpenVMS/Tru64/xBSD Enthusiast/Developer/Student (Variant 2):

Small footprint ATX deskside chassis
        space for one internal 3.5" 80GB 7200 RPM ATA-100 drive, 1 combo MKE
DVD-RAM/DVD-ROM/CD-ROM. Optional support could be provided for a
standard floppy or an MKE LS-120 ATA-33 "floptical" drive in a
vertical position adjacent to the CD/DVD drive. 300 watt ATX power
supply

ATX compliant motherboard
        700 MHz EV68 or EV69 CPU/4MB DDR HSTL cache in either
non-upgradeable "soldered on" or upgradeable socketted configuration
        AMD IG-4 761 chipset
        256MB 184 pin PC266 DDR SDRAM, 4 sockets (2GB max using 512MB
unbuffered ECC modules)
        ALi Southbridge I/O chip
        ATA-100 support (via ALi southbridge, treated as a SCSI controller
by the SRM console)
        Parallel, dual 9 pin serial, PS/2 keyboard and mouse ports (via ALi
southbridge)
        Integrated PC97 accelerated audio (also via ALi southbridge) for 16
bit, 48KHz stereo audio
        Integrated dual port TI IEEE 1394c "Firewire" for external devices
(treated as an ordinary SCSI controller)
          1 64 bit PCI slot supporting 66MHz 3.3 volt operation, isolated from
internal PCI bus (where the Firewire, ATA-100 and Fast Ethernet stem).
PCI slot occupied by IntraServer ITI-6011E (combo 21143 Fast Ethernet and
Permedia 3 video/4MB).

        In this configuration, the same planar board/etch as used in
originally proposed system. Omitted are the QFP/STP for integrated
components such as Intel 82559 Fast Ethernet (on integrated PCI bus), Nvidia
GeForce 3 video (on integrated AGP, support disabled in SROM/console), USB
(header not implemented), IrDA (header not implemented) and the PCI slot
will have to be used by the combo Intraserver video/network controller.

Target end-user street price of system for around $1900 for 256MB
configuration (assumes higher cost of implementing support for OpenVMS in
console and driver development). While this isn't exactly a dream machine
(particularly the video), it beats the Compaq XP900 by a mile.

There are of course several technical issues to overcome: Firmware console,
SROM and operating system/SRM drivers would have to be written more or less
from scratch, to name just a few items. The system described here does bear
some (deliberate) resemblance to the UP1500 currently under development at
Samsung. While it won't change the world, if there is enough interest in a
similar "low-end workstation" configuration perhaps their is a chance the
design will be as successful as Sun Blade has been for Sun. Who knows,
produced in sufficient volume, there may be systems like these running GPL
code at college campuses for years, giving potential future customers a
chance to use an Alpha for the first time. Every college campus I ever
attended had a SPARC lab and often enough an IBM RS/6000 lab as well. There
are still fully functional NEXT labs with ugly NEXT cubes in all directions.
When was the last time you've seen an Alpha lab? Low cost systems will have
a more profound effect on sales in the long run than a system priced above
where most schools are willing to pay. Its hard for companies (particularly
here in the US and Europe) to make plans that entail 5 years goals when only
the next quarter really matters. Built rather inexpensively, a further
stripped down unit based on a variant of this proposed configuration could
be built specifically for universities and practically GIVEN away. Make a
very public game of it and let a few dozen people "win" one. Media events
like this turned Apple around with the often trivialized iMac.

Comments anyone?
