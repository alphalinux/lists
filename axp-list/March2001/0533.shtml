Alvin,

>I think there are a couple of problems that we are looking at here.

Yes, not least of which is a lack of low cost entry points into the Alpha
platform, regardless of intended purpose. From a traditional workstation
perspective, the UP2000+ with 1 or 2 667MHz CPUs creates a misleading
picture (though I have heard it used as a comparison) as you will still have
to build a complete system around it. There are many vendors on this list
that may be able to produce a UP2000+ based system with fairly excellent
"duration of life" support for the box, but $9,000-$12,000 does not qualify
as an entry point/low cost system. Compared to other RISC platforms that are
starting to offer a more reasonable interpretation of just what "low cost"
means, we are at a loss where it comes to newer (EV6x) hardware. Even old
XP1000 with the 500MHz 21264 (EV6 .35) are quite costly. As such, the only
option for an EV6x machine is a good deal on an auction site or the rare
system coming off of lease.

>Only Compaq and API are making Alpha mother boards. There are other
>manufactures of special products like Force for special applications but
>these are low volume and high price.(I could be wrong here but I have
>looked and had little luck so I am sure that this is the case)

Compaq sold off the OEM division to SMART who then sold it off to Force
Computers (an industrial computer vendor). Unless you are interested in
backplane based industrial computer with one or two PICMG Single Board
Computer (SBC) CPU boards, they are not in the business of making Alpha
"motherboards" though they have the templates and licenses for both. Force
offers two single board computers based on Digital's original DMCC (DEC
Modular Computer Components) architecture. The EV56 and EV6 designs are both
of the 1998 vintage, topping out at 600/2MB and 500 MHz/2MB respectively.
You'll have to get the SRM console updates (5.6 is the latest) from
Digital's old FTP site, these ship with AlphaBIOS only.

>It would be nice to be able to buy an alpha workstation directly off the
Compaq web site.

You can buy some Alpha gear through Compaq's refurb site (Compaqworks), but
that's it, and they don't keep anything in stock.

>The UP1100 is now end of life and although the 1500 is in the works there
is
>no assurances from API that they will be selling the 1500 or any single
>processor board in the near future.

No, you're right about that. UP1500 may never see the light of day,
particularly if Samsung (not an API product) sees how dismal the sales
figures for the UP1100 were (or so I've been told). I'm not sure Samsung
wants to take up the mantle to "create" the low cost workstation market. I
don't think they would know how to market it properly if they made one. If
a large number of them don't end up in universities, colleges and perhaps
even high schools, whatever is sold at profit will constitute very little
and the market will not grow. As I've said before, SPARC and RS/6000 labs
are ubiquitous in colleges, not so for the Alpha, or any operating system it
supports.

>At one time API announced that a deal that would the AMD processor using
the same motherboard interface as the Alpha. I had >hoped that this would
mean that there would be a reason for companies to build motherboards that
would support both the K7
>and the Alpha. To date I think only API has a mother board that will do
this(UP2000). The CPU's alone for this
>board run over $2000 each.

I'm familiar with the press releases (dating back to 1998) you are referring
to. Some tech magazines wrote about this with excitement as the EV6 bus was
given center stage with the impressive performance of the new AMD Athlon
processors. What was not known at that time was that while electrically
very similar, AMD chose to use the slot-1-like Slot A while API needed slot
B. Slot B has many more pins to support such things as power and ground and
can operate at a higher frequency. No "Slot B" Athlons were ever produced
and similarly no "Slot A" Alpha processors were ever produced. As a result,
the hopes for mass produced Alpha compatible motherboards disappeared. I'm
not sure that with the differences in requirements at a boot level if it
would have ever been practical to believe that each cheap PC-like
motherboards made on a sub 5% margin would get an SROM and SRM firmware
console (Compaq controlled code), the Fail Safe Booter or the firmware
debugger. It would probably have been an incomplete "customer supported"
SRM console install with questionable Q&C put behind it. With the fairly
major differences between PC motherboards manufactured by one company vs.
another and the 3+ chipsets that were being used in Slot A motherboards, it
would have been a very difficult process at best. Remember also that what
was driving the market at the time was the left-overs from Digital's push
for Alpha/NT acceptance in 1997, NOT Linux. When AlphaBIOS fell out of use
with the death of Alpha/NT (also Compaq controlled code based on a MS
controlled ARC 1.2 spec), only the SRM console was left, but the licensing
fees, development environments (OpenVMS as opposed to AlphaBIOS's VS 6 on
WinNT), required OS support (including Tru64 and OVMS) and the lack of
familiarity PC vendors have with these environments would have effectively
stopped any ongoing efforts.

>The problem as I see it is not that there are no inexpensive systems based
on the Alpha but no inexpensive motherboards
>based on the Alpha.

Its worse than that, there are no initiatives to have one, strategically or
otherwise. If API were to produce one and only one board, discontinue it
after a year and halt all support for it one year later, it would do neither
the VARs, resellers or end users any good. To make them cheaply, they must
be produced in volume and that means a long term strategy must be in place.
Compaq won't be building Alpha motherboards any time soon. API is all there
is, and their efforts have been received with luke-warm support, in part
because they were produced in such small quantities that they never really
achieved a low price point. Ironically, if the Samsung UP1500 were released,
it would probably reduce sales of the API UP2000+ as it would be around half
the cost, probably the chief reason it will never see the light of day. Why
Samsung is building Alpha motherboards in the first place is beyond me,
that's what they formed API for.

Thanks for your input Alvin. Increasingly, its becoming clear to me that
the issue of an inexpensive, mass produced, mass marketed Alpha is a
political red herring. Compaq dumped Alpha projects left and right because
Digital had tossed billions into it with only limited success. A few dozen
well placed Alphaserver installations in key universities and loaners to
major companies might have turned this around. I'd love to know if that was
ever even attempted.
