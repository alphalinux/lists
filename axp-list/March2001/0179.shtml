Alan Young wrote:
> One would guess that Linus' and/or DEC's original port may have attempted
> to use the PALcode in the ARC firmware.

I don't believe so. Linus had SRM because he did his early development
on OSF/1, before Alpha/Linux became self-hosting. As a nice
side-effect, Linux and OSF/1 (now Tru64) became compatible at the system
call layer.

> Since SRM was being sold
> seperately back then, MILO may have been a stopgap measure so people
> did not have to pay extra to run a (semi-)free OS.

Yes. MILO was created to offer a free bootloader for Linux. It was
also a bit smaller than SRM, which with typical memory sizes in 1995 was
a plus.

Incidentally, much of this history is documented in LJ's archives. For
example, see "The Miniloader" in
http://www2.linuxjournal.com/lj-issues/issue20/1178.html.

> Could also be ARC was centered around a certain 32-bit OS and did
> not "fully" support 64-bitness?

I have been told that ARC PALcode is completely unsuited to Linux, but
cannot substantiate that claim.

Putting PALcode in the kernel is an interesting idea, but kind of
defeats the purpose of a "generic" kernel. (Remember the days when RH
distributed a separate kernel image for each board?)
