Convergence Points
Itanium is late to liberate

By Peter Coffee
September 11, 2000 12:00 AM ET

Get ready for a remake of the pivotal scene in "Patton," where British
forces triumphantly "liberate" a city-only to find American tanks lined up
in the town square to greet them.

In the role of the embarrassed Field Marshal Montgomery will be Intel's
long-delayed Itanium; in the title role of Gen. Patton will be Compaq
systems powered by Alpha CPUs, already freeing our toughest jobs from their
computational limits.

Alpha arrays are dominating rivals in high-performance computing. In
mid-August, the U.S. Department of Energy announced that Compaq will build a
12,000-Alpha system with clock rates exceeding 1,250MHz; in other wins this
year, U.S. and French sites have chosen Compaq/Alpha for tasks from drug
design to climate modeling.

Like Gen. Patton, the Alpha chip has had its ups and downs. At the end of
1998, Microsoft promised Alpha- targeting versions of Windows tools; in
April 1999, Microsoft demonstrated an Alpha-hosted 64-bit Windows. But that
August, Redmond pulled the plug on Windows for non-Intel chips. Sic transit
gloria Alpha? Wait for the second reel.

This January, Red Hat released its Linux 6.1 Deluxe for Alpha at $79.95;
April saw SuSE's Linux 6.4 for Alpha at $49.95; and July brought
TurboLinux's Alpha Linux at the same price with 60 days' free support.

If you don't want to take a chance on open-source systems, you can run your
Alpha on Compaq's Tru64 Unix; if that combo is good enough for Oracle's ASP
partner, Nupremis, it can probably handle your workload as well (while
733MHz Itaniums wait for Windows 2001).

The Intel/Microsoft axis will make a noisy entry this fall-but like
Montgomery's bagpipes, they'll find themselves facing the massive brass band
of the Alphas already in town.
