Luigi Cavallo wrote:
>
> Dear Rich,
>
> thanx for the very instructive, at least for me, lesson.
> However, from SPECfp benchmarks, it seems that an AMD 600 MHz should be
> more or less 3 times slower than a 21264 666 MHz.
>
> But the alpha is roughly 6 times more expensive...

If you go with a UP1000 700, and assemble it yourself, it
would be more like 4x, last I looked at prices. Depends on
which Athlon too. The faster they are, the higher the cost
per Mhz.

> If you have a nice parallelized code, as in my case, maybe better to buy a
> small 6 AMD cluster instead of a single alpha.
>

Don't know about your situation, but there is usually serial code
hanging around used to handle the "simple" stuff and that code will
run faster on Alphas.

>
> Now, I have a small budget (academy usally has not lot of money) and I
> really don't know whether to go for 2 UP1000 or 12 AMD 600.
>

Consider your parts list. 12 power supplies to go bad,
12 disks, 12 floppies, 12 network cards, 12 video cards,
12 motherboards, at least 12 DIMMs, etc. Your odds of
having a failure are worse due to higher HW counts.
The other issue is space. 2 UP2K's fit in 6U of rack space.
12 AMD's, using 2U cases, would be 24U. Even at 1U, which
requires special parts, you have 12U. Now when dual CPU
socket A motherboards appear, you're back down to equivalent
space use. Dual PIII motherboards in a 2U case would give
you less parts, less cost, and higher density.

I'd really like to see a dual cpu UP1100, or, a DS10L with
dual cpus. That would be awesome compute per rack unit.

Then there is that old 64bit vs 32bit problem. If you don't
need 64bit, you actually have a choice. If you must have 64bit,
then for now Athlon and Intel are out.
