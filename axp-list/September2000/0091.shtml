Steve Rogers wrote:

> unfortunately the Athlon is shipping in 1.1 ghz. and AMD is releasing no
> less than 5 new iterations of the Athlon processor over the next 5 months,
> ranging in speeds from 1.5ghz. up to 2 ghz.
>

Yup, and it's still slower (at some things, _much_ slower) than the Alpha
even at a given clock speed. Tell me again why Alpha has higher SPECCPU
scores at 500MHz than Athlon at 1GHz? And has anyone noticed where the extra
32 bits on the Athlon chip went? Oh, that's right - it's only a 32 bit
chip! Doh! So much for my VLM capabilities without needing massive hacks...

C'mon, when EV6 first came out and didn't move immediately past 500MHz people
were decreeing the end of the Alpha. Then Intel and AMD announced 1GHz chips
and we weren't yet past 667MHz. But geez, the Alpha is still faster. When
the Alpha was screeming past the barely 200MHz Intel chips at around 500MHz
the Intel folks said "Hey, it ain't the clock speed, stupid" in an attempt to
diffuse their less than lackluster performance. But now all you hear is
"Look, we've cracked the 1GHz barrier and we'll keep going". You don't hear
anything about the barely marginal improvements (sometimes as low as
single-digit percentage improvements for much, much larger clock speed
increases) that the continued speed bumps are worth.

What does all that mean? It means that IA32 is, frankly, running out of
gas. It's more than just clock speed, it's more than just "Hey, look at the
new jazzy things we make you do just to use our chip!" (cf. the Pentium 4
requirements), it's more than just putting it on a cartridge instead of PGA.
It's the whole ball of wax - that means clock speed, instruction set and
general chip technology, bus bandwidth, cacheing subsystem (size, # of sets,
algorithms, etc.), memory topology, technology, bandwidth, and tools to make
it sizzle. Alpha has all of those (maybe slightly less so in clock speed
_today_), and it has them in spades. Intel, AMD? Well, they've got clock
speed and even some okay compilers. But the rest of it? Feh, give me my old
Commodore64. At least _it_ had excuses... (Registers, Intel don't need no
stinking registers...*blam*)

>
> Intel stock was just downgraded, and Athlon processors are rapidly
> replacing Alphas on a widespread basis in the production environment.
>

Um, and what production environment are you talking about? Or is this an
omnipotent statement meant to speak of a greater truth? Hrmpf...maybe our
sales figures are wrong, because it looks like Alpha is stronger than ever.
Hey - did someone accidentally lump Athlon sales under the "A is for Alpha"
category? Didn't think so. Face it - everyone is replacing everyone; Sun is
replacing Alpha, Alpha is replacing Sun; IBM is replacing Intel, Intel is
replacing IBM. And, to top it all off, there's this great new company called
Alpha Processor Inc. that is dedicated to the Alpha and the low- and
mid-range customer base. So...if our Alpha sales are doing so well, _and_
there is a company outside that is also selling them independently...hmmm...

Oh, and as for Intel. When was the last Alpha chip shipped that had to be
pulled en-masse because of a hardware bug that couldn't be worked around.
The word "marginal" just means "we didn't find the bug in our one-person
testing lab so it can't be that bad". And of course, ahem...wanna compare
errata sheets? I'll rent the forklift so you can bring in the errata for
IA32 chips. Frankly, pathetic - truly pathetic.

>
> It may be considered a return of bragging rights, but it is a hollow
> vicotry at best.
>

Okay, if this is a hollow victory, I guess I'll take it. That, and the best
damned chip money can buy. Oh, and I'll see you at EV7. And if you're not
an Alpha user and that doesn't scare you, it's only because you don't get
it. But if you seriously believe your email, that's a good indication that
you don't get it anyway... Go out, read the product briefs and check the
benchmark scores. Then join the believers.
