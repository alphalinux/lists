Steve Rogers wrote:

> Peter Rival wrote:
>
> > So here are the real questions:
> > - Is having 3D rendering programs on Alpha (let's say Alpha Linux for now) desirable
> > by you? (Wider audience - how many would pay for them, and at which premium - the
> > one for Windows or UNIX?)
>
> Fortunately, a number of mainstream programs are going Linux. Houdini was released
> already for Linux, Maya is being released soon etc. Fortunately, this brief flirtation
> with M$ NT crap is rapidly coming to an end.
>

So then the availability of software for Linux is not a hindrance, right? Just the fact that
it's only Intel Linux and not Alpha Linux. I want to make sure my messages are clear so I
don't bark up a tree and have a bee's nest dropped on my head ;)

>
> Certainly from a technical point the Alpha is preferable. Of course there are some
> financial considerations as well. When the Alpha was 7,000.00 box and was WAY out ahead
> of 4,000.00 PC box, it was one thing. However, when you can build very fast and very
> powerful systems for dirt cheap and the Alpha is pushing 10-15 grand for a box, the
> chioce gets much harder.
>

Okay, that's my next question. What I meant here was, say the software is available for
Alpha Linux - would you be willing to (all other things being equal) pay a premium for having
it there and if so in what kind of ballpark?

>
> > - What price point would you need a DS10 (DS10L now, I guess) to meet in order to be
> > more cost effective for your work than the Athlons currently available? For example:
> > say the Alpha is 2x as fast as the latest Athlon which costs $2k for a full system,
> > so the Alpha should cost no more than $4k.
>
> I find that completely logical. It is also a point I could sell to management. But when I
> tell them the Alpha is 2x as fast and cost 10k as opposed to 2k for the Athlon, they will
> tell me to go buy 5 Athlons. My average completion time is faster, and therefore, this is
> a better buy.
>

Sorry, that was supposed to be an example. I have no idea how much faster our Alpha Whizbang
2000 is than an Athlon - that's why I'm asking the question. Let me restate. Assuming an
Alpha is faster than an Athlon for these given jobs, at what price point does the performance
difference become worth less than the additional cost. Basically, how much extra would you
be willing to pay for an Alpha given that the software is available? (Which is the context
of my first question - circular, isn't it? ;)

>
> > If I can get some sort of a consensus I'll take whatever I get to Compaq management
> > and lobby for support (what form it takes to be decided). If Alpha is as dead or
> > dying in the rendering business as you say it is, maybe this will be its
> > resurrection. Whatever else I may be, I will do my damnedest to get this done if
> > people really want it.
>
> Last year at Siggraph, StationX Studios shared the booth with API, they were showing the
> prerelease version of their animation software called Messiah. (runs on Alpha and x86) I
> had voiced concerns over Alpha at the time and asked if both versions came on the same
> disc as many other apps did. I was told it did not, but was recommended that I get the
> Intel version if I had doubts since "people were dumping Alphas left and right"

Can I just ask the larger audience - if you hear a company say this type of thing let me
know. I'll send it along the line and I'm sure our version of Bruno The Enforcer will have a
talking-to with them. It seems we (Compaq, or at least me anyway) always hear these
statements well after it's too late to do something about it. I want to change that.

> At the
> time I doubted this, but since the Athlons have come out, numerous Lightwave effects
> houses have ditched most or all of their Alpha equipment. We dumped 20 systems, and I
> have helped others that were also transitioning to Athlon and selling Alpha equipment.
>
> >From numerous reliable sources and associates, Alphas are getting dumped faster than
> Amigas were.
>

Well, that I can't argue with. But I _am_ at least going to try to change it.

>
> However, price may not be the only deciding factor in Alpha getting back into the
> production market. Newtek holds a 51% marketshare in studios and production houses, with
> SoftImage, Maya and others splitting up the balance. Only a couple of these have
> announced Linux solutions, and even less Alpha Linux.
>
> IF Newtek could be persuaded to port Lightwave 6.x to Alpha Linux, that would be
> something. But Linux still has a long way to go in the way of support.
>

_This_ is something that I needed to know. You tell us what you need, and we'll see what we
can do about getting it there. That's our job, plain and simple. I'll bring that suggestion
forward. But I'll need one more detail to get anywhere - how many people would be willing to
at least contemplate continued/renewed/new use of Alpha as a platform? Or perhaps, would
this have a trickle-down effect of getting other shops to port to Alpha Linux? (Yes, I'm
looking for an angle to sell this with. :)

>
> I despised having to sell my Alpha systems, but when it got to the point that I could not
> use the latest tools, they became a hinderance that did nothing more than take up space.
>

I hear that loud and clear. Until I got a copy of Applix that worked reasonably well, I had
to bounce back and forth between my Alpha workstation and my PC. But hopefully that will
change soon enough as well...

To the drawing board....
