> I'm not sure I would like to see workstation-class hardware (read: SGI,
> DEC/Compaq, Sun) "going for a wider appeal."
> Workstation users, by and large, have money to spend -- and the
> performance and price tag of these machines reflect that.
>
> Expand into the corporate market, and you have to compete with
> IBM, Dell and the like. Doable.
>
> If you want to push further, perhaps into the home market, or
> especially into schools .. you have to start making boxes _cheap_ ..
> real cheap. Just like we have "generic" shit box Intel motherboards
> with buggy chipsets, we'd start seeing that sort of thing coming out of
> workstation makers.

Ah, so you are saying that they designed these chips to be "workstation"
class and thus not for mass market appeal? Hummmm . . . perhaps so. I
guess that's why they are always in need of corporate investment. Their
market isn't big enough to support their efforts?

> iMac's aren't aimed at companies, they are aimed at individuals. Every
> case that I own is either airbrushed with funky designs, or covered
> with stickers and logos; or both. Corporate beige sucks. And, who the
> hell wants a big beige box in their home. Home users need a machine
> that doesn't look garish in their living room -- now whether a
> tangerine iMac fits the bill or not is a matter of taste -- but I'm not
> in a position to judge; like I say stickers and airbrushed madness
> mark my machines.

I understand your response, however, I was simply making the point that
the real money is not with the individual but with the corporate sale.
Think about it. How many of the computers in your house are from your
company? Your laptop, your spouses laptop, perhaps even a machine or two
that you "borrowed" from the company to do "work at home". If you are not
a computer junkie then you most likely have only one personal machine that
does your bills and let's you cruise the net. The point is, in my
opinion, the average household contains more company hardware than
personal hardware. However, given the demographics of this list, the
results from the list members may show the reverse trend.

> Alone, the look of the machine won't sway me: that's where my
> girlfriend's airbrush and talent come in; but the look of a machine
> would play a roll if I was on the fence between one or more machines.

Girlfriend's airbrush? That's cheating. How can you refuse your girl?
Anyway, if all things were equal, price would be my deciding factor, not
color or design of the case.

> Everyone I know who isn't a computer geek who has a computer do one of
> two things with it: stick it in an out of the way room, or put it in
> one of those folding computer cabinets so you can't see it when not in
> use; because computers look stupid.

I'm sure that's true. Most don't like to be seen as the family "geek".
It's un-cool. However, that person usually makes the best bucks. Go
figure. Anyway, I display mine without issue. To me, they are machines
that "do work".

> Most people wouldn't leave a cardboard box filled with books sitting
> in their living room; even if they love the books -- put whatever you
> like inside the box, and when you look at it, you still want to get it
> out of the room! Computers are the same way, and they even LOOK like
> cardboard boxes... with a few notable exceptions; the iMac being one.

Yeah, but I guess I see machines differently. I don't care how they look
as look as they fill the requirements and are within my price range.

> Personally, I don't like the look of the iMac much -- but I'm still
> thinking of picking one up and throwing Yellow Dog Linux on it :)

You know . . . was thinking of picking one up too. However, mine would
be for accounting.

Anyway, this is one man's point of view . . . .

Paul
