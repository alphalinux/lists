The way to sell business on a platform (any platform) is by having:

    High profile placements ("eBay adopts Alpha over Sun
    Enterprise 10000 servers")

    Having all major DB, middleware, and web software ported to
    your platform (BEA Tuxedo, Oracle 8i, Cold Fusion, Dream
    Weaver, etc.)

    Best (or atleast very competitive) TPC price per transaction
    (performance benchmark) - in other words, competitive pricing.

    An available workforce trained in the equipment (Active support
    of education market, this is how Unix broke into the corporate
    market, recent college graduates championed it at their new
    employers after graduation).

    And a manufacturer with a solid reputation to stand behind
    your decision.

With these elements in place, an IS Director can propose an
Alpha solution knowing: he is not the first to do so, he will not
be locked into one set of tools, the cost will be in-line with
the other suppliers, and if he has a problem, it will get fixed.
Those are business concerns, not technical abilities. SGI
made sure to showcase their systems graphical abilities
because it was a differentiator, that was what made their
systems stand out. Their successes were in graphics
machines, not backend servers, IIRC.

    SGI's prize for focusing on graphics/desktop front-end
machines was a need to enter the WinNT market and
compete with PC-class hardware.
