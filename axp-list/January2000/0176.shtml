On Tue, 11 Jan 2000, Paul B. Brown wrote:

> > If you want to push further, perhaps into the home market, or
> > especially into schools .. you have to start making boxes _cheap_ ..
> > real cheap. Just like we have "generic" shit box Intel motherboards
> > with buggy chipsets, we'd start seeing that sort of thing coming out of
> > workstation makers.

> Ah, so you are saying that they designed these chips to be "workstation"
> class and thus not for mass market appeal? Hummmm . . . perhaps so. I
> guess that's why they are always in need of corporate investment. Their
> market isn't big enough to support their efforts?

Yeah, basically -- lets face it; even a decade ago anyone could see the
writing on the wall: Intel makes the CPUs, and Microsoft makes the OS.
If you want to have a shot at mass-market appeal, your chip better run
Intel code -- throw in other bells and whistles if you like to get
high-end users.

Why go with a non-Intel compatible instruction set, unless you are
tweaking performance for the niche user's. With a "non-standard" CPU
you are begging for MS (read: intel-centric) to be unable to port their
code to you, and you are begging for a portable system like UNIX to
be the lifeblood of your CPU line.

As it turned out, as far as I can tell, MS helped keep the workstation
class hardware alive by not introducing a viable UNIX solution. High-end
users want UNIX and fast CPUs .. they couldn't get a full-blown UNIX
on Intel at all so the speed question was irrelevent. Sun was able to
carve out a market.

Enter Linux and BSD -- now you can get a full blown UNIX compatible
system on Intel hardware; and the question becomes how much speed do you
want, and how much coin do you have. Do you _really_ need an UltraSparc's
performance, or will a PII and Linux do the job at a fraction of the cost?

When you have loads of cash, Alphas and Sparcs make sense: they perform
better, but cost more. The price/performance of the Alpha doesn't come
into play in my book, because the Intel machines have a longer lifespan.

When the engineers want a PIII on their desk, you can take their PII's
and trickle them down to the technicians, or the secretaries once they
are a generation or two behind.

In the long run, you can save a lot of money with Intel machines. They
cost less up front, and two generations from now they can be put into
fine service as office machines.

> I understand your response, however, I was simply making the point that
> the real money is not with the individual but with the corporate sale.

You're right -- and corporations use lots of office products: enter MS
Office and the supremacy of Intel.

Were I to try and take market share away from Intel while still aiming
for the high-end user, I'd probably make a line of chips with a superset
of Intel's instruction set. Maybe get nuts and do vector processing in
hardware. :) The engineers, scientists and the hard-core games will take
a serious look at the platform, dump some capital in Id and other game
makers to accelerate for my CPU, and I'm in a position to make money.

The money is with corporations; so you have to make a product that gets
touchy-feely with a corporations one nerve ending: its bottom line.
