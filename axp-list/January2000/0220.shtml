> Why go with a non-Intel compatible instruction set, unless you are
> tweaking performance for the niche user's. With a "non-standard" CPU
> you are begging for MS (read: intel-centric) to be unable to port their
> code to you, and you are begging for a portable system like UNIX to
> be the lifeblood of your CPU line.

This is true only if you want a business desktop and not a education,
research, development, or engineering workstation. This is the Mips/Alpha
niche. What I'm saying is that there may not be enough market there.
Also, with the cash that Intel puts into research, if the SGI's and DEC's
of the world cannot keep up, the Intels will crush them. Intel promises a
Ghz chip with a > 133 Mhz bus speed by the end of the year. They are also
working on their own RISC processor. If AMD keeps up the pressure, Intel
will, sooner or later, overtake some, if not all, of the workstation class
EOM's.

Economies of scale, my dear peers, faster, cheaper, easy to obtain, lots
of software, multiple target markets. Hummm . . . sounds like Intel,
huh?

> As it turned out, as far as I can tell, MS helped keep the workstation
> class hardware alive by not introducing a viable UNIX solution.

This is because Micro$loth wants to push UNIX out of the server and
mid-tier market with a version of Windows. I believe, as time passes,
Windows will mutate into something very UNIX-like at the backend while
maintaining the inefficient but user friendly front end.

Who will win? Who knows . . . . The UNIX philosophy or Bloatware? It's
our choice.

> High-end users want UNIX and fast CPUs .. they couldn't get a
> full-blown UNIX on Intel at all so the speed question was irrelevent.

I think Linux, FreeBSD, SCO, and others do offer a full-blown UNIX. Only
the most bleeding edge high availability is not available for Linux but
that is in the pipeline.

> Sun was able to carve out a market.

Yep, but it was development workstations and Internet (mid-tier) servers.
This is not mass appeal but it's not bad. Sun definitely has an edge over
everyone else in Internet servers.

> Enter Linux and BSD -- now you can get a full blown UNIX compatible
> system on Intel hardware; and the question becomes how much speed do
> you want, and how much coin do you have. Do you _really_ need an
> UltraSparc's performance, or will a PII and Linux do the job at a
> fraction of the cost?

Yep, my point exactly. HP offers the Brio line now. Marry one of those
machines with Linux and you have a decent Internet server for under
$1,000.00. Now that's bang for the buck.

> When you have loads of cash, Alphas and Sparcs make sense: they
> perform better, but cost more. The price/performance of the Alpha
> doesn't come into play in my book, because the Intel machines have a
> longer lifespan.

I look at price/performance if all others things are equal. This would
include using the same software on each (Ex. Apache), same type of OS
(ex. Linux or Solaris), the same netwrk bandwidth (ex. 10 or 100 BaseT),
same disk architectures and sizes (ex. 9.1 GB Ultra SCSI II), etc. If I
can get 1 CPU per U of rack space, I'm pretty happy.

For my money, the 1U and 2U, rack mountable, 1 or 2 CPU, high availability
servers based on Intel and Linux or FreeBSD are probably the best bang for
the buck for Internet or Intranet servers. By the end of the year there
will be 900+ Mhz based servers or even a Ghz based server on the market
for far less that you can get Sun Ultra 250/450/3000/3500/etc, HP
L1000/2000, HP N4000, or any other RISC processor servers.
 
> When the engineers want a PIII on their desk, you can take their PII's
> and trickle them down to the technicians, or the secretaries once they
> are a generation or two behind.

Exactly.

> In the long run, you can save a lot of money with Intel machines. They
> cost less up front, and two generations from now they can be put into
> fine service as office machines.

Again . . . Exactly.

> You're right -- and corporations use lots of office products: enter MS
> Office and the supremacy of Intel.

Yep, hence their ability to offer a cheap product. Again, economies of
scale.

> Were I to try and take market share away from Intel while still
> aiming for the high-end user, I'd probably make a line of chips
> with a superset of Intel's instruction set.

Not exactly. Remember the Linux phenomanon. We have emulators for
Windows and DOS but how many actually use them. I sure don't. I like
UNIX and it's variants. It think they are most efficient and get the job
done. The point being, you can go up against WinTel with something like
LinTel, UltraLinux or AMDLin, etc, if you have the marketing strategy and
the force of will.

> Maybe get nuts and do vector processing in hardware. :) The engineers,
> scientists and the hard-core games will take a serious look at the
> platform, dump some capital in Id and other game makers to accelerate
> for my CPU, and I'm in a position to make money.

My bets are on the engineering, research, and development shops instead of
the gaming shops. Sorry, it's just my own point of view.

> The money is with corporations; so you have to make a product that
> gets touchy-feely with a corporations one nerve ending: its bottom
> line.

Yep.

> Indeed. I have 4 machines up and running right now (read: _running_
> now) and I have 7 operational machines total (plus loads of parts) one
> machine comes from my "employer" (its not a real job, its an internship
> but thats irrelevent :)

Sounds like me and most of the gang here . . . . However, we are
engineers and not "normal people".

> Well, I'm on the other end.. but I have tatoos, wear my hair like a
> 14th century Samurai, and am never seen without (rather decorated)
> leather jacket. So, I'm out in the extreme tail anyhow.. it only
> makes sense that my machines look weird. :)

HA! The funny thing is, whether you wear a 3 piece suit and a marine
haircut or a ponytail, earings, and a leather jacket, our industry
tolerates it because we want intellegence, motivation, self-starting,
inovative, independent thinkers that can "get the job done". We don't
want, just another "yes-man" in a suit.

> Indeed. Ultimately I am in the same category -- I tack on decoration
> as an afterthought, really -- but for the people who treat computers
> like home appliances, they'll buy a CD deck that matches the rest of
> their stereo..

HA! Yep. To each her own.

> In my mind it is a decent strategy by Apple: their market is a niche
> Mac-lover market to begin with, highly individualist by nature, so
> they are preserving the share that they have, and maybe make some
> waves and get new customers: we are both talking about buying one.

I'm not saying that they should not stay with their niche market. What
they should so is offer some PowerPC looking boxes for corporate use.
Make sure that the basic software exists for it and then push it as the
corporate alternative to "Evil Bill Gates". As long as they refuse to
offer cheap computers can cannot be connected to corporate LANs they will
not be a real player.

> Plus, this is how Apple operates; they aim to the individual. Remember
> their Big Brother ad? "Think different." .. thats their gimmick.
> Whether it is enough to sell machines or not is another question,
> personally, were I Apple, I'd take my own advice and "Think different"
> .. but hey.. who the hell am I .. I'm just a poor college kid with
> tatoos .. :)

Yeah, right. The poor college kid with tatoos that will be making more
than most of his peers if he is motivated, smart, keeps an eye on the
industry, and has a plan for his future.

Just ramdom thoughts . . . .
