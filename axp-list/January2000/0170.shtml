> Don't get suckered into thinking that games are irrelavent.
Phil, there is a fine line between games and graphics demos.
The games you have in mind are not relevant outside of the home
entertainment market which is dominated by Wintel and game consoles.
Demos have a broader spectrum but lack the plot, interactivity
(to a degree) and replay value of games. They do what they were
coded to do - show off the features of a graphics engine at
the highest possible resolution.

High-end SGI boxes I used to work with came with several demos
for everything from the RE to the Cave. They were fun things to
play around with, but you'd never hear one of the researchers gasp
in delight at the prospect of seeing the skyfly or crayonland demos
for yet another time.

> 2) Most games demand full resources, and work the system hard. Any
> program that stresses many elements of the system (network, graphics,
> sound) will find bugs that affect programs in other areas.
Oh yeah, nethack and angband really works my system hard ;-)
 
> 3) Most games require real-time like response. Any program that requires
> such a reponse will benefit from problems fixed by working on a game.
The realtime you're talking about is not the same realtime referred
to 'in the real world' with embedded systems. If it were, QNX, OS-9
or p-SOS would be the preferred gaming platforms of the day.
The only place you see the melding of realtime systems and
something game-like is in simulators (the type you're strapped into)
at an amusement park where the coordination between gear movement
and visual display has to be good enough to fool your visual cortex.
 
> As for the demo, I think that Alpha has the potential to wow the
> crowd. A nice quick, graphical program (such as a game) would make
> it really obvious that it is a fast little box. ;-)

Many moons ago last year, I tried to get some guys at Compaq
interested in a demo for LW2k using a capture board, cheap ccd camera,
and a simple OpenGL demo. Take the streaming video from the camera,
map it as a texture to geometric shapes, and morph it around.
Drivers exist for the brooktree cards, and code exists for
mapping video input to everything from nurbs to other video
streams. SGI was able to accomplish these types of tricks
with hardware mpeg/jpeg handling; a real test of the Alpha's
power would be to do all this on the main processor(s).
