>
> Well, I obviously can speak only for myself :-)
> I write articles for a german Unix magazine ( called UnixOpen ) on a regular
> basis. Although this is not my main job, it rapidly consumes more and more
> of my time. I have already in the past done *several* articles on the
> Alpha arch in general, as well as running Linux on Alphas - you
> guessed right: Alpha *is* my personal favorite arch and Linux is among
> my favorite OSes. Currently, I am doing a rather big article series
> on the UP2000 covering EV6, the Tsunami chipset, Linux & NetBSD and a
> performance comparison with an Athlon based machine.
> I cannot really comment on the situation in the US, but I assume it is not
> really that much different compared to Germany.
> The mainstream Linux is definitely with x86, so most of the overall press
> coverage goes into this. In addition, most articles cover things like
> installation and comparing various distributions. Personally, I've never
> seen too much sense in this approach, mainly for the following reasons:
> 1. People do not install Linux every day, they rather tend to run it, once
> it is installed :-)
> 2. Honestly, most distributions are alike. They run the same kernels, most
> of the apps are the same and I would regard the differences between
> rpm, dpkg and the like to be of minor importance in a production
> environment.
>
> I found the main reason for the lack of coverage on alternative archs
> to be the lack of competent Editors. You'll find a *lot* of people
> writing articles about how great their Linux runs on a 500Mhz Pentium-II,
> that is overclocked to 501Mhz - yes, I too think that sucks. But you
> are in *serious* trouble, if you want to find someone covering things like
> the difference between ARC and SRM. Back in 1997, I wrote an article
> about the PC164, which was fairly new at that time. Among a lot
> of other things, it contained infos about which PCI cards run on that
> board and how easy it was to set up a decent system using that board
> with standard PC-type peripherals and how easy it was to get Linux
> on this machine. I was stunned by the amount of reader-feedback we
> got for this article. People were amazed to learn, that running an Alpha
> is really not bizarre black-magic. The very same was repeated for
> the SPARC arch - with the same result. Based on the boatload of
> x86-based systems being shoved through my home-office for testing,
> I know almost as well as anybody else on the mailing-list, how much
> more advanced about *any* other architecture is ( I confess - I did
> PC mainboard devel for a big taiwanese company in one of
> my previous lives... ). Although the x86 chips got *a lot* better in the
> past three or four years, the chipsets and mainboards are still crap
> ( or are you telling me, that there is a *technical* advantage in
> having a 64bit memory-bus in a system running two Pentium-III
> at 800Mhz ).
> Based on my experience in that past years, finding a competent
> techwriter is the main problem for about anything that is not
> x86 or MS. It is definitely *not* the lack of interest in the reader
> base of any decent computer mag.
> I *strongly* suggest, that anybody being interested in writing stuff
> for things like Linux-Alpha should approach a magazine with that
> idea. As stated above, I can only speak for Germany, but I cannot
> believe, that it is that much different in the US.
>
> As a popular commercial stated: Just do it.
>
> My $ .02.
