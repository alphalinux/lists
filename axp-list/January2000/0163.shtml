> 1) Some games require a working, optimized 3D enviornment. Many
> scientists would benefit from being able to use this to look at
> their climate data, genetic data, financial data in new ways.

You are thinking of Graphical or visual outputs to processed or raw
data streams? I absolutely agree. Look at NOAA and the National
Weather Service.

> 2) Most games demand full resources, and work the system hard. Any
> program that stresses many elements of the system (network,
> graphics, sound) will find bugs that affect programs in other
> areas.

Yep, I agree with this too. Also, I believe good hardware/software
testing procedures developed by EOM's can also be used for the same
purpose.

> 3) Most games require real-time like response. Any program that
> requires such a reponse will benefit from problems fixed by
> working on a game.
>
> Games are a fun way to make sure of all the above works.

True but this would be a hard-sell to a corporate suite. To them, games
are a waste of time. You may be able to stress the hardware and software
but well written testing programs can do this same *and* give valuable
output testing data as well. This data can be used to make standards or
help governent officials make recommendations for standards.

> As for the demo, I think that Alpha has the potential to wow the
> crowd. A nice quick, graphical program (such as a game) would
> make it really obvious that it is a fast little box. ;-)

Now your talkin'! :-)
