On Fri, 19 Feb 1999, Eric Lee Green wrote:

> Well, hmm. First of all, no good browser :-(. The good news, though, is
> that KDE 1.1 does compile straight out of the box (you can drop by our
> booth at LinuxWorld Expo and see it running on a 164LX-533) and the KDE
> 1.1 browser isn't half bad (even handles cookies now).

I am using mozilla from... (somewhere??) with lesstif-0.87.1-2 it feels as
stable as the x86 version with the exception that sometimes it renders
some HTML incorrectly but usually does okay if you click "refresh" and
also whines occasionally that it can't find "BtnUp and BtnDown" but other
than those two things IMHO it is as stable as netscape for x86 Linux and
for sure, more stable than Internet Exploder on Windoze 95. It even
renders the mess they call HTML at startrek.com as well as the x86 version
but much faster!

>
> Secondly, Linux on the Alpha is very much a hacker's platform. It's the
> way that Linux was four or five years ago on the Intel, where you had to
> compile things all the time and hack the kernel all the time to deal with
> weirdness. For example, you'll note that the LX box at the show doesn't
> light up its little hard drive LED when it's accessing its hard drive.
> That's because I had about an hour free this morning to get KDE 1.1 and
> the 2.2.1 kernel going on it prior to it being packed up for the show, and
> I did not have time to go hunt down a patch to the kernel's initialization
> routine for the Symbios 53c875 chip that would enable the hard drive
> light. The patch exists, I have seen it referenced on the kernel-list, but
> the fact is that you don't have to do that kind of thing on the Intel
> platform. In short, the Alpha is still for the adventuresome. Those who
> want to turn on their Linux box and have it just plain work are probably
> better off sticking with the Intel. But if you're bored with Intel, and
> want some speed, well, Alpha will give you excitement and speed a'plenty.

Umm, I don't think we are 4 to 5 years behind the x86 group... 1 to 1 1/2
year(s) sounds a bit closer. Most problems come from 32 --> 64 bit issues.

BTW all you IA64 lovers out there (if there are any) are going to have the
same problems but most likely worse as the compilers and friends will be
"green".

Alpha + Linux is not perfect (yet) and I would not recommend an Alpha
Linux box to someone "green" to Linux.

BUT! If you are at least willing to learn some "C" (if you want to port 32
bit apps to Alpha), are not afraid to build your own kernel (when you out
grow the Red Hat one), and want the fastest platform out there - then
there is now doubt an Alpha is the way to go.

"..but the fact is that you don't have to do that kind of thing on the
Intel platform."

I would have to disagree, I find myself having to do that on both
platforms. There are a lot of x86 systems that wont run Linux at all. The
only Alphas I know of that wont run Linux are the 7 year old TURBO channel
machines. Although I've heard/read that work is being done to port Linux
to them.
