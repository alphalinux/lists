On Thu, 18 Feb 1999, Gary A. Allen wrote:

> The AlphaPC 164LX motherboard has incorporated within it two EIDE ports. It
> is my understanding that SCSI is a faster disk access bus on a Linux system
> than EIDE because each of the SCSI devices can function autonomously thus
> reducing the work load of the CPU. However it is my reasoning that if only
> one device such as a CD-ROM is on the EIDE bus while the hard disk(s) reside
> on the SCSI bus then the CD-ROM and hard disks could still function in
> parallel without bogging down the CPU. EIDE CD-ROM drives with write/rewrite
> capability are significantly cheaper than their SCSI versions. If there
> is no penalty in having only one CD-ROM drive on the EIDE bus then purchasing
> an EIDE CD-ROM drive makes sense.

        I like to think of my SCSI bus as a MULTI-ACCESS bus, and my IDE
bus as a SINGLE-ACCESS bus.

        SCSI equipment usually has logic built into them that acts as a
sub processor/traffic cop for the scsi bus. IDE does not have this
hardware (I have heard that you can buy some advanced PCI EIDE cards that
do caching and sub-processing -> Promise Technology?).

        IN this way, SCSI wins hands down over EIDE in a server
environment as it's bus can have multiple devices all being accessed in an
asynchronous or synchronous fashion(transactions essentially
simultaneous, but not quite).

        If your system will be multi-user (ie: doing processing and
transactions for multiple users at once), or you be doing lots of
multi-tasking (ie: RUUNNING multiple programs using cpu time at the SAME
time), I would suggest SCSI. If it's just your workstation, and you are
not in need of extremely high levels of performance, EIDE will work just
fine...

        My own system has:

        EIDE channel 1: 4 gb UDMA hard drive (/,/home, /etc partitions ->
very static)
        EIDE channel 2: 24x EIDE cd-rom
        
        Qlogic UW Scsi-3: 2x4gb 7200rpm fujitsu UW Scsi 3 drives

                The scsi system is where /usr/local is, and is where I do
all my work. Also, swap is mounted on a partition on one of these
drives to max swap performance.

        A little bit unconventional, but I could barely afford the scsi
disks I bought. The money I saved by buying EIDE components was enough to
buy another 128mb of RAM, and I do not think I sacrificed much useability
for my particular environment ( I need RAM much more than disk).

        To summarize, using EIDE like you propose can provide excellnet
price/performance ratios (DEPENDING on your processing environment).

>

        You will encounter pundits for pure SCSI, and those for pure EIDE,
but finding a mix that fits in your budget can provide some nice
performance.
