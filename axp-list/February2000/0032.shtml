> > > something is wrong. I just noticed my wtmp files have
> > > a gap in them and inetd was restarted and I didn't do
> > > it on my firewall system.

> > > (where to start looking in general...)

> >

> > http://www.cert.org/tech_tips/root_compromise.html
>
>
> Compared to the other sites, CERT is not particularly
> up to date nor Linux specific.

True, but some might argue that it is more accurate and generic. For
example, a lot of sites recently posted a load of rubbish about supposed
tcp exploits saying they were linux vulnerabilities (search for
"stream.c"), when in fact they were only a problem in FreeBSD. Cert
was accurate. Similarly the oft-repeated assertion that "users
without resource limits can bring down the system". Yes they can, so
use resource limits.

> What I read in the
> section you mentioned is basically common sense that
> I already knew to do.

Well, that's good.

> I'd say the sites previously
> suggested are a better view of what's going on in
> Linux security. Makes you wonder how many sites see
> trouble and don't report it to anyone.

Well, I don't know that linux has that many specific issues, rather
than those that relate to packages, which are thus generic unix
problems. The specific linux exploits are usually discussed and
resolved on the kernel list long before any so-called security experts
find out about them. You could check for these on

http://kt.linuxcare.com or
http://www.lwn.net

...if you don't want to directly subscribe to the kernel list.

> I really dislike those security bulletins that say
> "Program X has a buffer overflow exploit, please
> contact your OS vendor for a solution." CERT many
> times just says disable the service until your vendor
> provides a solution. Not what I want to read/hear.

Perhaps not what you want to hear, but correct. You can then go to
SuSE, or RedHat or whoever, and get the updates. You need to get an
update from somewhere. You do check your vendor's site for security
updates, don't you?

> YMMV

It does.
