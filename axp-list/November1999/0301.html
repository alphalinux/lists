I just wanted to apologize to both Greg and the AlphaLinux community for the waste
of bandwidth and time that moronic rant about SPEC95. As I have been corrected by
multiple people, SPEC _is_ a benchmark consisting of "real-world" applications,
albeit only a subset of them. My intended point was that, while SPEC is indeed a
good starting point, everyone should recognize that things have and continue to
change. As things continue to change, so do the benchmarks that are meant to model
them in some fashion as SPEC has/will with SPEC2000.

As I quickly learned, Greg is much better versed in these areas than I (heck, he
works at HPTI, what else do you want?). Two lessons learned: 1) don't take
arguments from the work day onto 'net mailing lists, and 2) don't try to type
reasonably argued messages on Micro$oft systems at home without the benefit of
coffee. I'm going to go back under my locking hole now and hope that everyone can
keep doing all those things they do without my moronic interference...*sigh*
coffee......

 - Pete

> Greg Lindahl wrote:
>
> > > Very true. The scary part of benchmarks like SPECint95 is that CPU
> > > manufacturers may target benchmark performance specifically to get an edge
> > > over the competition, which does not necessarily benefit real-world code
> > > to the same degree.
> >
> > That's why SPECint and SPECfp are mostly composed of real-world codes.
> >
>
> And that's why people that know better only consider SPEC95 as a _very_ relative
> measure of specific performance and not a good systemic view of any system.
> Anyone who even talks of things in terms of SPEC95 as if it's a "real-world"
> measure of actual performance just plain doesn't understand SPEC. That's the
> biggest reason SPEC2000 is (hopefully) just around the corner. There are a
> plethora of reasons that SPEC95 is no longer relevant to modern computing loads,
> but I won't get into them here.
>
> Suffice it to say that if you're going to look at SPEC marks, look at the full
> disclosure statement, and also try matching the specific elements of the
> benchmark to the loads you're going to be running; the full disclosures are
> quite often very enlightening. SPEC is a tool, not the toolbox.
