With regards to your message at 05:15 PM 11/7/99 -0500, Peter Rival. Where
you stated:
>Greg Lindahl wrote:
>
> > > Very true. The scary part of benchmarks like SPECint95 is that CPU
> > > manufacturers may target benchmark performance specifically to get an
> edge
> > > over the competition, which does not necessarily benefit real-world code
> > > to the same degree.
> >
> > That's why SPECint and SPECfp are mostly composed of real-world codes.
> >
>
>And that's why people that know better only consider SPEC95 as a _very_
>relative
>measure of specific performance and not a good systemic view of any system.
>Anyone who even talks of things in terms of SPEC95 as if it's a "real-world"
>measure of actual performance just plain doesn't understand SPEC. That's the
>biggest reason SPEC2000 is (hopefully) just around the corner. There are a
>plethora of reasons that SPEC95 is no longer relevant to modern computing
>loads,
>but I won't get into them here.
>
>Suffice it to say that if you're going to look at SPEC marks, look at the full
>disclosure statement, and also try matching the specific elements of the
>benchmark to the loads you're going to be running; the full disclosures are
>quite often very enlightening. SPEC is a tool, not the toolbox.
>
> - Pete

A good example:
The LX motherboard, equipped with 2MB cache and a 633 CPU got a spec95 fp
of 21.4
Stick the same CPU in an LX with 4MB cache and you get 26.8
So, a 2MB cache difference makes this machine 25% faster? No, I really
don't think so..
