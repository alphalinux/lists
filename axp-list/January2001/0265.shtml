On Thu, Jan 18, 2001 at 12:11:33PM -0500, SteveCraft wrote:
>
> Greg, what do you mean by "different fragmentation properties"? I am
> curious as to why if Linux/Unix is unfragmentable, Microsoft didn't steal
> the idea for their own stuff.

Nobody said "Linux/Unix is unfragmentable" but that an explicit
defragmentation will rarely buy you anything or enough on ext2 ("rarely"
does not mean "never" but then rewriting a file system could be simpler
and more effective).

FAT file system, as used by MS-DOS, is very primitive. This actually
has advantages on machines like the original IBM PC. A simple code, low
overhead on a creation of new files, even if reading their contents may
get painful after a while, you can flip a big red switch any moment and
be relatively certain that a file system survived, and things like that.
But there are trade-offs, a need for periodic runs of 'defrag' is one of
these, and things like VFAT become later hairy hacks which will induce a
puke if you will look too close. Still one has "back-compatibility" to
contend with.

Various Linux, and in general Unix, file system were designed with
different goals, a performance one of them, in mind.
