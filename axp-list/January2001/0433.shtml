Maurice Hilarius wrote:

>Actually the largest 1" now available are the IBM and Seagate 73GB in
>1" form. 2GB is maximum RAM. For over that a board needs dual memory
>controllers. An example of this is the DP264 planar board ( same as
>used in our Hard Data DP264 and Compaq DS20 machines) but these are far
>from 1U. 6U is the typical rack form factor of those. We find no
>problems on a _properly_ configured Alpha system with 4GB. The term
>properly does not mean dead stock Red Hat 6.2 or 7, btw.
>
>Of course you could also buy Compaq ES40 machines and get over 4GB. And
>for the cost of those machines you could also buy a Rolls Royce and
>have the data driven to Florida.. But they aren't 1U either. In terms
>of rackspace versus power the CS-20 is about as dense as one can buy
>nowadays,

This is the conclusion I kind of came to myself earlier. In addition
to the greater capacity, I like the large memory bus that the DP264
gets from the dual controllers. However, I'm wondering if it can run
CPUs at greater than 667 MHz. It seems to me that if there is twice
the memory bandwidth, the bus should be able to support faster CPUs.

Yes, I am looking for the most compute power per cubic volume, but I
also need lots of memory as well (don't we all?). Like a lot of other
problems, ours become less efficient as the data is distributed due to
the higher percentage of the time moving the data among the processors,
instead of actually processing them.

Actually, Sun's new US-III blade 1000 workstations can hold up to 8GB
to support 2 CPUs. Too bad their performance are a little low, and
their availability is somewhat scarce (due to chip yield problems, I
hear).

Thanks for your reply.
