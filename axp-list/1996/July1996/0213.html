Regarding the statement that it's illegal to even provide hooks to
support an externally supplied crypt... Unless my research was insufficiently
thorough, no case exists where that has ever been tested. I think it's more
a matter of intent of this administration, though I'm still sifting through
the details of the various statutes involved. But there are, of course,
answers. 1) let someone outside the U.S. develop the utilities. 2) support
legal encryption utilities via hooks, and let the encryption utility developers
use *those* hooks. 3) let encryption developers use harmless hooks, such as
"wrapping" the encrypted data into something that looks like uuencoded data,
or gif data, or whatever.

Eventually this government will recognize that the tail, how ever large,
cannot wag the dog. In the meantime, we'll just have to be aware of the
differences between laws of various nations. (And remember, you may be
subject to jurisdiction where you inject your work into the stream of
commerce, etc.--and I'm not even going to attempt to describe the ways
commerce gets defined.)

I'm hoping that a recently formed group of legislators (conservative and
liberal) dubbed ProCODE will prevail and loosen export restrictions on
data encryption technology after the elections... There's a website on
this, I think at the Center for Democracy and Technology...
http://www.cdt.org
