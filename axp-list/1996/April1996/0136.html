Stephen Gaudet of DCG Computers Inc. <sjg@dcginc.com> writes:
> So, bottom line it has a two year warranty on all parts and labor. And as
> you well know we've been clocking chips up since the first Jensen rolled off
> the line and NEVER replaced a burnt out processor yet!

In my opinion, if the only ill effect of exceeding the rated speed on a
processor was burning them out from time to time, that would be entirely
trivial. If I could had an application where I actually *needed* a
10-15% performance increase, and only had to replace burned out processors
once in a while, I would seriously consider it.

But the reality is that the integrity of my data is of far greater
importance to me.

The Alpha's built in ECC does a great job of detecting errors in the
rest of the computer. But what will detect errors in the Alpha?

Semiconductor manufacturers invest a great deal of effort in characterizing
the performance of each new chip they design, and each change to the
fabrication process. They also tests on each part they ship in order to
guarantee that it meets the rated specfications over the entire operating
voltage and temperature ranges.

The designers know exactly where the critical paths in the chip are, so they
know how to test for worst case conditions.

On the other hand, most third party vendors just pop the chip into a
board, crank the clock speed up, and verify that it seems to run Doom
or Minesweeper properly. Of course, this doesn't give any indication as
to whether some critical path in the chip is close to the limit and likely
to cause occasional errors. After all, once the third party vendor sells
the box, what does he care if one in ten billion add instructions gives
the wrong answer (for example). After all, if the customer notices the
failure at all, he'll have a hard time proving it and demanding a refund.

In my experience, all semiconductor vendors are willing to evaluate OEM
customer requests for deviations from specifications. In some cases,
they are willing (for a price, or a guaranteed sale) to expend the
engineering effort needed to determine that a particular specification of
a part can be safely exceeded under controlled conditions. For instance,
it is not uncommon to get a specification waiver stating that a part can
be operated at some particular higher clock frequency provided that another
specification such as voltage or temperature is restricted to a narrower
range. Perhaps DCG has such an arrangement with DEC? Of course, if they
did then what they are doing isn't really overclocking at all.

Perhaps I am being overly harsh. Perhaps DCG Computers Inc. does in fact
have some state-of-the-art semiconductor test equipment, and perhaps they
actually have licensed a good set of test vectors from Digital Semiconductor.
I'm sure Mr. Gaudet will be happy to explain to us what steps DCG takes to
insure the integrity of their overclocked systems.

Until then, I am going to have to content myself with Alphas that merely
run at their rated clock speed.

IMNSHO, any reseller selling overclocked systems *without* some sort of
comprehensive testing approved by the semiconductor manufacturer should
be stripped of their "authorized" status.

I would by lying if I claimed never to have overclocked any systems or
chips myself. But I do it out of curiousity, in an attempt to guage (in
an extremely rough sense) the amount of margin in the specifications.
And I only do it for long enough to satisfy that curiousity. I certainly
don't sell overclocked products; it is bad engineering practice and a
possible source of liability, not just for my company, but for me
personally.

Eric
