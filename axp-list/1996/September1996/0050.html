At 09:50 AM 09-03-96 -0700, you wrote:
>I've been listening in on the Alpha listserve and your comments caught my
>attention. I have a few question that I hope would be able to clarify
>before I purchase a UDB.
>
>You mention that you got better performance using the Digital Unix compiler.
>I can undestand that as I would assume that Digital would know it's
>processors better than GCC. Would it be possible/recommended to compile the
>Linux kernal and take advantage of this optimization for the kernal?
>
>You mention that the UDB was never intended for performance work. That's
>ok, cause I just want a web server to act as a virtual web host. I see the
>system demands being mostly TCP/IP and disk access. There may be databases
>queries at some point in time but I dont see this as be very heavy. The
>server software is multi-threaded not fork().
>
> Do you feel that this would be an acceptable use of a UDB 266?
> If I compiled the server deamon with the digital compiler, should I
> get a boost in performance (vs. gcc)?
If you are thinking of buying a UDB 233 then you would probably be better
off with a system based on the "noname" or AXPpci33 board. This is the 21066
chip on a AT factor PCI/ISA motherboard which many DEC TOEMs, (including
ourselves) integrate into an enclosure as a more similar system to a normal p.c.
This way you can have your choice of components, and no upgrade, hardweare
choice issues. Finally, DEC UNIX is not available on the UDB, so this would
be your least expensive route to DEC UNIX on Alpha.
Bear in mind in either case that the "older" 21064 and 21066 chips are not
very fast, and from a performance point of view a Pentium may be a better
choice in the lower end.
If all you are doing is a small server then the 166MHz UDB is not a bad
machine. I have one site where we are handling 54 Samba users, Smail, Simeon
mail IMAP server, and NIS to the head office on a DEC 3000-300 which is an
older 21064 100MHz machine with 96 MB RAM, and it keeps up just fine.

>Would you compare Linux and Digital Unix? I'm looking for things like
>performance, ease of administration, etc.
Linux is experimental, so what I say today is probably obsolete next week.
Still it is a good platform in general, albeit new. Performance of compilers
and some libraries ( esp. floating point and math in general) is much better
on DEC UNIX, although Digital has promised us a compile of their advanced
math libs soon for Linux users. Admin? Depends on what you are used to.
Digital does not make a simple UNIX, but if it is what you are accustomed to
it works just fine.
>
>If I have the GCC libs, can I use the Digital compiler "out of the box"?
Ummm, not exactly

>How is the alignment requirement handle?
Depends on what you are doing and how current your patchlevels on software are.

>Does the Digital compiler correct alignment errors or at least notify me
>during compile?
Usually.

>Are there any hardware compatibility issues that I should be aware of?
>Which TGA model does the QS1 motherboard have?
DEC 21030 chip usually bundled with 2MB DRAM and Brooktree RAMDAC. Bear in
mind this is _not_ a video card, the chips are part of the UDB motherboard
package. It is _similar_ to a TGA in that chips are the same. This is an
8bit color video setup.

>Does v3.2 come with an X server that supports the TGA ZLXp-E2 or E3?
>And lastly, what is list price for v3.2c?
DEC UNIX does support these, please note that current rev. is 4.0.

UNIX license cost varies with CPU.
2 user DEC UNIX license pack and SRM for a AXPpci233 board is about $800
( U.S.dollars).
The package with CDs and DOCS costs about $275, and does not vary with CPU.

3 Year upgrade and docs subscription costs additional.

Support agreements, ditto...

Conclusions:
Cheapest Linux platform at Pentium level or better: UDB 166
If you are thinking of a 233UDB buy a Pentium or Cyrix 6x86. More bang for $.
If you want faster yet buy a 20164A or 21164. Not very cheap, but beats
Pentium Pro by amount varying with $ spent.
A UDB 166 ( VX40B-F2) cost under $800 U.S.

A good Pentium 166 system costs about $2000 or less.

A decent 21066 - 233 axpPCI (noname) system costs about $ 4000 with Linux,
and about $ 5000 with DEC UNIX ( 64MB, 1GB SCSI, better video, ether,
CDROM,etc..)

A 21064A-275MHz in an equivalent system costs about $6000 to $8000.

This is sort of a quick price/performance scale.
Hope this helps.
