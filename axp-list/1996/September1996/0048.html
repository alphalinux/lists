There have been patches to g77 that make it work on Linux/Alpha systems
for a couple of months now, but available primarily to a small group of
testers.

Mostly what is needed now is a fair amount of work fixing bugs (mostly
packaging issues) in g77 in general, and planning, then executing, a
series of two or more releases of new g77 versions that culminate in a
version of g77 that supports Alphas without breaking other machines.

That's because the changes needed to g77 to support Alphas, and other
machines g77 has had a bit of trouble with, are of the "generally likely
to break other systems in surprising ways" variety -- such as disabling
the use of the gcc back end's "complex" data type to implement Fortran's
COMPLEX type.

So, a version 0.5.19 that fixes bugs in 0.5.18, provides _options_ to get
the "iffy" changes that make Alpha support work, and so on seems a
necessary step prior to releasing a version (say, 0.5.20) that has all
the appropriate Alpha support right out of the box. If 0.5.20 broke other
systems, users would have a decent alternative (0.5.19) to fall back on
while the bugs were worked on, and hopefully a 0.5.21 or .22 would be
sold as "the version everyone can upgrade to", at which point work can
start on 0.6 in earnest.

Remember to "finger -l fortran@gnu.ai.mit.edu" (or whatever works for
you -- it's in the g77 docs) for sometimes somewhat moldy information
on g77.

Finally, yes, without efforts on the part of certain Craftworks employees,
and substantial support from Carrera Computers and DCG (you've all seen
Steve Gaudet's contributions to this list, for example), there probably
wouldn't even be a working version of g77/Alpha floating around at _this_
point, much less a couple of months ago.
