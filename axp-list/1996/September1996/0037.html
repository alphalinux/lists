>>If you are thinking of buying a UDB 233 then you would probably be better
>>off with a system based on the "noname" or AXPpci33 board.
>
>I thought a UDB *is* a system based on the AXPpci33 board, or did you
>mean instead of buying a UDB, buy the motherboard and put together
>your own system? Or is a UDB based on something else entirely?
No, a UDB is a 12.5"x12.5"x2.75" box with a _tiny_ little board with d*mn
near everything integrated on it. It has no slots, but a riser, which, in
_some_ variants, sprouts a PCI slot. It has 4 SIMM sockets, usually
factory-filled with 24MB (2x8MB and 2x4MB SIMMs) in a VX40 ( 166 MHz) and,
believe it or not, usually 24 MB (2x12MB SIMMs) or 32MB ( 4x8MB SIMMs) in a
VX41 or 42 (233MHz) .
The UDB is an attempt by Digital to build a low-cost, small p.c.. It does
come as the Multia with Alpha chips, and also a Pentium version is available.
When one takes off the cover and looks, it is almost like a laptop built by
a committee. They usually use those tiny 3.5" floppy drives you see in
notebooks, 340MB hard drives with a notebook style hard cable and no power
connector.
The reason they are such a popular topic right now is that when Digital
discontinued the VX40s they "dumped" them on the market at a _very_ low
price, with a limited warranty and support. This is a "dead" product, as far
as Digital is concerned.

The AXPpci33 or "noname" is the first Digital Alpha offering of an AT form
factor motherboard. Integrators and OEMs like us use it build systems.
It has some "gotchas" in regrds to just putting it in a regular p.c. case:
It needs 3.3V power as well as 5 and 12.
It sometimes comes with the PS/2 style mouse and keyboard connectors on the
motherboard, so the keyboard hole in a typical case is wrong.
It uses 36bit SIMMs , like the rest of the Alpha products, so more common
non-parity 32 bit SIMMs for Pentiums and 486's don't work.
