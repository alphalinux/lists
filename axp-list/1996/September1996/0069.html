On Wed, 4 Sep 1996, Terran Lane wrote:

> Richard Gorton spake unto me, saying:
> >
> > I'm trying to collect information about the Linux/Alpha market,
> > with the intention of trying to make Linux/Alpha much preferred
> > over Linux/Intel by virtue of commercial application availability.
> >
> > If there were a single commercial application that you would request
> > to become available on Linux/Alpha, what would it be?
> > Are you an end-user, or do you sell Linux/Alpha hardware?
>
> Descent II, definately. ;-)
>
> But seriously, I'm an impoverished grad-student who spent most of his
> available money to get the system in the first place (made possible
> only by the fact that there's a great deal of good free software and a
> free OS -- my deepest thanks to all the great developers out there
> doing this). But if I did have money to buy software, things that
> would be useful to me would be:
>
> Matlab
> Mathematica

Definately! Though I want DPML and a working g77 first.
Math is the "killer app" for the academic crowd, IMO.

> And don't underestimate the power of games. They may not be the
> biggest market share, but they _are_ high profile, and some people
> _do_ make hardware decisions based in part on being able to play the
> newest thing by ID, etc. (At least, in the limited scope of my
> experience).

Considering that, one might also think about porting WordPerfect or some such
easy to use analog.

It may seem silly, but I can think of several labs I have worked in that
had a half dozen workstations for number crunching, and Wintel boxes
that just sat around for word processing. It's certainly more efficient
to pay a couple hundred for a WP licence than $1500 for an overkill word
processor & you have better integration too, if you do any data analysis
on the workstations. Of course, if Linux-AXP is ever to make an impact
on the non-nerd home computer market, some kind of word processing will
be needed. Most people hate and/or are unable to use TeX.

I'm somewhat suprised that business WordProcessing on Unix machines never
took off; maybee for lack of good software. Wang made a bundle with an
analagous idea way back when & it seems to me, things like Lotus Notes do
the same thing a networked Unix box with metamail, hypertext browser & a
word processor would...

-Scott
