Hi,

I just got a new Alpha and joined this list to get the last word on getting
Linux up and running...

I saw a post from Akio Morita on f2c (now in the redhat archives).

F2c is not just for small programs. I have used it for 4 years now on various
platforms where I don't have a native f77 compiler. I have compiled literally
millions of lines of Fortran, in large programs as well as small. F2c combined
with gcc has proved to be one of the most reliable "fortran compilers" I have
used and frequently finds bugs in programs that other compilers miss. Note
that f2c does no optimization, only translation. Any optimization is in the C
compile of the translated code, so the C compiler is the key. The g77
compiler, although a native compiler that could do more optimization since it
starts with fortran source, produces slower code than f2c/gcc and has only
recently gotten debugged enough to compile large applications without having to
substitute f2c/gcc compiles for the buggy modules. The speed of code from the
f2c/gcc combination is typically 70%-90% of that from a native fortran
compiler. This speed difference is greater for RISC machines (such as the HP,
the MIPS processors or the Alpha) where the consequences of bad instruction
scheduling are more serious and factors of 2x or 3x are possible between
f2c/gcc and native f77 compiled code.

Comparing DOS or NT native compilers to any of the GNU compilers (g77,f2c/gcc)
shows that the GNU compiler optimizations on the Pentium are not as good as
those from vendors that have used Intel's optimizations. On a 90 mhz Pentium
Watcom F77 under DOS or NT maxes out on Linpack at 18 MFLOPS or so, f2c/gcc
produces 11 MFLOPS, g77 gives 9 MFLOPS. There is obviously much room for
improvement here.

One bright spot on the Linux compiler horizon may be that MicroWay (Kingston,
MA) is porting their NDP optimizing compilers (F77, F90, C, C++, Pascal) to run
under Linux on both the Pentium and on the Alpha. They are not ready yet, I
talked to them yesterday, but they are working on the library compatibility
issues now and tuning the Alpha code generator. These are obviously commercial
compilers, not freeware, but may offer a way to produce much faster code under
Linux. In the meantime, f2c/gcc and g77 are the only way to compile fortran
under Linux on the Alpha. Hopefully, I should have some timing comparisons for
Linux f2c/gcc vs DEC f77 in the next week or so.
