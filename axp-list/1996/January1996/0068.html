Well, I do have a suggestion, but you may not like it...

This may very well be just one instance of a generic problem associated
with the BIOS emulator that is currently built into most MILO's. It started
life as an x86 emulator, and has had [234]86 instruction handling patched
in. It is far from robust, but seems to work well enough for most BIOSes.

As VGA controllers were moved to the PCI bus for performance, the BIOS that
went with them was upgraded to expect more machine underneath, ie at least
a 386 processor, and maybe now, even 486. These BIOSes are more problematic
for our emulator; some cards in particular, ATI Mach32 and Mach64, seem to
be dependent on the more advanced 386/486 instructions.

The solution I've had to adopt, is to eschew FLASHing MILO and rely on the
more robust BIOS emulation of the ARC console (SRM is fine, too). This also
requires generation and use of a MILO *WITHOUT* BIOS emulation built-in.
The extra steps in booting, I believe, are offset by the flexibility and
robustness of the ARC console's emulation.

Early MILOs did not have emulation builtin, and thus depended on power-up
initialization on the cards themselves to get into a usable (or non-usable)
state. Perhaps the early MILO that works with the I-128 is one of those; if
not, then later emulator tweaks seem to have broken the I-128 support.

Craig, since your machine is a CABRIO, I believe, the ARC console is still
present in the FLASH; contact Dave Rusling (rusling@rdgeng.enet.dec.com), or
Erik Troan (ewt@redhat.com) for details as to how to reactivate it.
Generating a MILO without BIOS emulation is not hard, you just need to
fiddle a bit with the MILO config files. Let me know if you need any help.

Dave Rusling is looking into the possibility of using the emulator code from
the ARC/SRM consoles in MILO, but this is far from a done deal, and would
utilize/distribute only emulator binaries. The source code for it would
*NOT* be available, as it is proprietary with little or no hope of becoming
unencumbered.
