At the risk of re-opening a contentious subject that has faded out of sight for
a while...

After the last barrage of overclocking discussions I decided to experiment a
little with the 300Mhz 21164 Microway Screamer board for my Alpha. I installed
a 33.333 mhz crystal and increased the PLL clock divide ratio to 10x (to give a
33 mhz PCI clock). The system has run flawlessly at 333mhz for 2 weeks, no
burps at all.

Being curious, I wondered how far you can push a good thing. I next tried a
34.2+ mhz crystal (with a divisor of 11x for the PCI clock). This runs at 342+
mhz and boots and runs fine (at least for the time I played around with it,
about an hour).

Just to find out where this thing fails I tried a 35.0mhz crystal with the 11x
clock divide. At 350mhz the system boots up and runs Unix fine for about 5-10
minutes, then I get a kernel panic and the system reverts to the monitor. Given
the heat that the processor generates I wonder if the failure is not due to the
processor or support chips internal timing shifting slightly as the board
reaches steady state temperature.

I have reinstalled the 33.333 mhz crystal and am running the system at 333 mhz
in the interest of reliability. Note that the processor chip was supposedly
rated to 300 mhz.

On the basis of this admittedly limited test it appears that the 10%
overclocking referred to in previous discussion is workable.

10% overclock works fine
14% overclock works fine (limited test time)
17% overclock marginal (fails in 5-10 min)

It also appears that (this) processor, when driven overspeed by significantly
more than the 10% overclock, will fail after booting and running normally for a
time, it does not simply fail to boot at all if overclocked too much.
Obviously you can get too greedy here. If you have enough crystals lying
around, you can get some idea for the margin of operation by progressive
overclocking.
