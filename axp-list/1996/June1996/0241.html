Just got my 166 Multia on monday. I couldn't install linux, however, as it came with a broken
floppy drive it seems, however I did have the NT 4.0 beta 2 CD's which I promptly installed.

Always interested in performance, I grabbed the MSVC 2.0/Alpha compiled byte native benchmarks
from byte's web site, and tried it out. Here are the scores for all of my currently up-and-running
machines:

Except for the NeXT (68040) all machines had 256kb L2 cache. 1.0 = a Dell pentium-90. So my
multia-166 is about 1.5 times faster than a pentium-90 at integer work, and 1.31 times faster at
floating point, according to the byte native benchmarks. It seems to fit solidly with the Power
Mac 8100/80AV (PPC601/80), and is quite a bit faster than the 6x86/100 (which cyrix calls a
P120+). It looks like a 166 MHz multia performs about pentium-133 or a touch better in integer,
and about pentium-120 in floating point. However, I don't personally feel byte native benchmark is
a good estimation of how applications will perform on the machine. The OpenGL screen savers run
MUCH slower on the multia than they do on the pentium pro-200. Interactivly, the multia feels
fast, but most of the few alpha applications I've yet downloaded are slower than their pentium pro
counterparts. 640x480 jpeg decoding is practically instant on the ppro-200 (with imageview95), and
takes 5-10 seconds on the alpha with DEC's jpeg decoder. I need to get ahold of an NT based
Alpha-emitting compiler....

I wish FX!32 were available, I'd love to try out the binary translator.

Anyway, my advice is: Grab a multia. The 166's are quite a bit faster than a 486 in any case, and
the 233 ought to be into high pentium (if not quite pentium pro) territory.
