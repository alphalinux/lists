Vareck Bostrom <bostrov@teleport.com> writes:
> The one machine that I really like is a "UDB" or "noname" I think you guys
> call it,

Some confusion is evident here. The UDB is a complete system (well, except
for monitor and keyboard) that is otherwise the same as a Multia, but without
MS Windows NT. It has only one PCI slot and two PCMCIA slots, but this isn't
too limiting since it has built-in SCSI, ethernet, video, sound, serial, and
parallel ports.

The "noname" is a motherboard which Digital sells to OEMs. You can put it
in a normal PC case, and use normal PCI and ISA cards.

Both the UDB and the noname can be configured for either 166 MHz or 233 MHz,
although apparently the only standard configuration of the UDB is 166 MHz.

> does anyone have an idea of what a 166 21066 or 233 21066 would compare to
> performance wise to a linux/intel machine?

My 233 MHz Multia seems somewhat slower than my 133 MHz Pentium Amateur, but
that may be partly due to a faster disk drive on the Pentium Amateur. However,
I don't run any floating-point intensive software, for which the 233 MHz
21066a ought to run circles around the Pentium Amateur.

I am using the Multia as a web/ftp/mail/etc. server; it is colocated at my
ISP's POP. It is such a small package that my ISP didn't even notice I had
installed it until I mentioned it to them!

I really wish Digital would design a new UDB/Multia motherboard with the
21164a; anyone from Digital listening? :-)
