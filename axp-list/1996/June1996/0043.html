I wrote:
> The reason for a hierarchy of cache is that it is impractical to make
> (and interconnect) a very large amount of very fast memory.

Vareck Bostrom <bostrov@teleport.com> wrote:
> No, you may want differing cache policies across differing cache levels.
> Perhaps a fully assocative small L1 cache, and a 4-way assocative L2 cache,
> and an even larger 2-way or direct mapped L3 cache. You don't want to spend
> a microsecond searching tag every time you want to see if somethings in your
> L1 cache.

Ah, but the reason you want differing cache policies is (as you suggest in
your last sentence) *because* you can't build big, fast caches with the
policies you want.

No high-end microprocessors that I am familiar with implement fully associative
caches at any level, because they would take too many transistors and be
slower. Even a set associative cache is inherently slower than a
direct-mapped. Given a particular transistor budget, it may make more sense
to have separate I&D direct-mapped L1 caches and a unified 3-way
set-associative L2 cache (i.e., the 21164 approach). The same transistors
could have instead been spent making the L1 cache bigger or making it
set-associative, but either approach would have increased the hit rate at the
cost of increasing the cycle time.
