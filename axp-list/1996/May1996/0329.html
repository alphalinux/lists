Dominique Avatravaux <dom@cwi.nl> wrote

I'm a newbie to Alpha programming, and I often feel lost in it... For sure it
is part because I am french and Slackware-addicted :-), but the information
seems to me more private than on Intel : you must *purchase* the technical
documentation from Digital ! And the FAQ's seem slim to me : what is a PALcode
? And why does Milo need to be rebuilt as often as the kernel ? And how is the
limit between Milo and the kernel set ? And why must we wait for Digital to
implement it's own version of ELF instead of working with them on that topic
?...

Does it have to do with DEC compatibility ? Well, I think that if Linux/Alpha
chooses to enforce Digital Unix compatibility, this means that Linux
programmers choose to satisfy their clients prior to themselves -- which was
never the case before. This will bring it further and further from the original
"Linux attitude". See the people posting on this list : no newbies around, only
kernel/libc/Fortran/gcc/hardware hackers ! Where are the happy Sunsite disk
fillers ?

I would like to put things in some perspective here. You are saying
that Intel/Slackware environment is easy compared to Alpha stuff---but
this is only because they are way up there on the maturity curve.
Linux on Intel dates back to a prehistoric 1992; Slackware appeared
towards the end of 1993. Actually, for some time there was NO compiled
distribution; one bootstrapped a Linux system by cross-compiling the
kernel, shell and the compiler, and assembling all the source packages and
compiling them in turn. It was a big development when binary
distributions appeared (MCC, SLS---remember those?). Booting Linux
from the hard disk was a black art (shoelace was one appropriately
named package); then, LILO appeared and things were easy ever since.

Consider that (thanks to Jim Paradis' Blade and RedHat folks) Alpha
had a binary package early on. The boot problems are due mostly to a
large variety in Alpha hardware designs (jensen != NoName != Mustang
!= Cabriolet != EB164 != UDB etc.); compare this to a mostly uniform
Wintel/BIOS environment. I don't think you can blame anyone for lack
of 'Linux attitude'---it is just that the problems are hard and we are
still early on the curve. I bet you that 3 years from now Alpha Linux
will be as easy as your Slackware today.
