In that sense, I've been a "pro" since I was about 10, and perhaps in

everything's just fine", rather "Here are several reasons things are

>Please remember that newbies and cheap Intels originated the Linux movement,
>and that if computers had always been a matter of professionnals, the idea of
>code freedom would never have made such a long way -- see in what state HURD

I think you're confusing several things above. Cheap Intels indeed were
a vehicle for the Linux movement, and several newbies (who actually had
quite a bit of pro knowledge, as demonstrated by their handling of the
project -- e.g. Linus) did indeed start things going, but -- I want to
remind you -- _that has all happened before, many times_. There have
been in history _many_ such projects that got that far, or even further,
on real hardware, etc., the reason for Linux getting _way past_ that point
has more to do with the pros than otherwise.

I remember back when friends at Cygnus first suggested I consider Linux
on an Intel as a machine for doing g77 work on instead of trying to acquire
some kind of "pro" workstation. They weren't particularly interested in
the hackerish nature of Linux so much as the fact that it was a quickly
maturing, _real_ Linux that made a cheap PC much more like an expensive
workstation. My interest included much more of the idea that maybe, sometime,
I'd be able to tinker with the OS myself, like I used to do a couple of
decades ago (boy, I miss those days!), but Linux was gaining ground (along
with FreeBSD) back then due to meeting _pro_ needs. CD-ROMs didn't start
getting pressed just to meet needs of amateurs, though I think the
amateur market is probably more substantial at this point.

Code freedom came from a very _professional_ basis, IMO, at least the
way Richard Stallman (the father of the FSF and the GPL, if you will)
viewed it. Without that pro basis, he'd have just distributed stuff as
PD in a marginal state that only other programmer/hackers could deal with,
leaving more suit-and-tie types to twist in the wind trying to
package it for public consumption. His vision of free software included
the necessary ability for _non-programmers_ to freely exchange source
code not because they modified it themselves, but because they might
want to have a friend (or employee or contractor) do so.

I don't know what you mean about the Hurd. I do know the prominence of
a professional GPLed product like Linux has taken substantial pressure
off of the Hurd's release timeline, and I do know the people who are working
on it also are trying to meet pro standards as far as making sure the
Hurd is, when publically released (as vs. the alpha state its in now, I
assume -- the kind of state you should love!!), won't require most of its
users to be as knowledgable about its internals as its programmers.

As far as your dig against Digital and making docs available, that
gets into sociopolitical discussions that aren't worth pursuing here.
Suffice to say that it _might_ just be possible that one or two individuals
at Digital are personally as excited about being able to make otherwise
proprietary information more public, in conjunction with AXP, as you are
about the idea of knowing every nook and cranny of your system. And,
if sharing such information was so obviously the right way to go from
a corporate standpoint, that doesn't explain companies like HP that don't
(despite being considered excellently managed companies, even good
"corporate citizens", whatever that means). Maybe you see Digital as
just following an obvious path, but in the corporate-computing arena --
including all the people who make the chips you'll ever want to use --
they're blazing a new trail somewhat, and I suspect more than a few
suits in boardrooms are expecting/hoping Digital has made a mistake by
being as open as they are.

After all, I really doubt the DEC employee who drove to my house to
deliver a manual did it solely because it'd improve his salary down
the line.

You definitely haven't written a flame, not even much of a criticism,
and I hope you haven't taken my responses that way. Again, I have
an emotional empathy with much of what you have to say, so I've just
been hoping to help you understand some of what _I_ think is going on
that bothers you, because maybe as a result it'll bother you less,
maybe you'll do something positive about it, maybe it'll even energize
you, etc.

IMO, none of us own the workforce of free-software development.
That's one of its greatest assets.

I still think your best strategy at this point is to get involved more
in the actual operations of Linux, MILO, and so on. Surely the
experience of reverse-engineering what you can't find info on
would be very helpful, and we could all benefit from your discoveries
and the ways you might use your knowledge down the road.

You should know that my own history includes starting out as a fairly
young hacker. If it wasn't for free availability of source code and
general availability of (often poor) documentation on things, I wouldn't
have gotten my start in programming until a decade or so later, and
never known as much about how software actually works. (E.g. I learned
about recursive-descent parsing of infix arithmetical expressions by
reading the assembler code for a BASIC interpreter when I was 13 years old;
no way I'd have grasped the beauty of that by sitting in a classroom
being introduced to software engineering as a 19-year-old computer
student, that's not the way I learn.) I used probably the very first
version of Emacs, because I happened to be doing some hacking at the
MIT AI lab when I got email from rms about this new collection of "TECO
macros" he'd assembled to make editing easier (I tried it out, thought
it was neat, but decided I knew TECO well enough to be more proficient
at that -- besides, these macros wouldn't support the KSR33 teletype
I used from home, so why learn an editing system I couldn't use most
of the time?) -- as I recall, it was email sent to a list, though maybe
it was a post to the bulletin-board system they had online. Even
back then, I mostly wanted to write programs for people who didn't
have to know as much as I did (after all, I knew so much -- ha ;-),
and so did many of the other hackers. Writing g77 as a volunteer is
my payback to the community for providing that environment. Many of
us have both a sense of debt/gratitude for the past and a sense of
responsibility for doing more things in the future, and it's in this
spirit that we work on free software like Linux/Alpha, g77, the GNU
project in general, and so on, in as professional a way as we can
muster given rather limited resources. Even on my current temp
job doing proprietary RISC-compiler software, if they made their
stuff GPLed it'd be hard for me to not do the same, or more, work for
free, it's all so much fun.

I think the fact that there's a group of us so large that we have
gotten to where we overwhelm what used to be the predominant non-
corporate computer group, hackers of software for hackers, says a
lot of good things about the progress of notions like community
service in this world, or at least this industry. After all, for
the first time in history, an average literate citizen of an
industrial country can spend less than 1/12th of his annual income
on computer hardware and obtain incredibly inexpensive software,
with source, that, with not much more learning and frustration
than $$$ software, do all sorts of interesting and important
things _without_ becoming an expert programmer/hacker!

But, I recognize that this means perhaps the loss of influence of the
purely explorational hacker as the primary mover and shaker of free-
software solutions worldwide, at least, in the arena of solutions
for operating systems, compilers, and whatnot. I'm just not so
sure that's a terrifically bad thing -- any more than the loss
of the grizzled machine coder as the primary applications programmer
was in the late '50s and early '60s as these newfangled things called
"compilers" took over.

Personally, I'm happy you've chosen an AXP over Intel as a machine
whose guts you wish to explore -- you'll learn a lot more about
a machine that looks a lot more like what we'll need good programmers
for in another 10-20 years that way, and you won't be seeing nearly
as much really badly designed code (not that negative examples aren't
good teaching tools, but...). Please don't let a few frustrations
get in the way of your enjoying the new machine. If you have to wait
to get info on MILO for a couple of months, for example, you could
learn some of the ins and outs of RISC instruction scheduling --
I think every Alpha chip offers some educational benefit there
(aren't they all at least two-issue?). Surely there are plenty of
things you could learn about right in the evolving Linux kernel itself
as well, maybe you could study the code gcc outputs for code you
think is critical and see how you could improve it...then try it
out as editing .s code and if it is noticable, let us know, we might
find a way to get gcc to generate that better code, or maybe you'll
do that as well!
