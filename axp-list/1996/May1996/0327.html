seems to me more private than on Intel : you must *purchase* the technical
documentation from Digital ! And the FAQ's seem slim to me : what is a PALcode
? And why does Milo need to be rebuilt as often as the kernel ? And how is the
limit between Milo and the kernel set ?
>
> Hmm, sorry? I think that we (Digital) have been incredibly open
> with this (don't even try and get me started about how open Digital
> Unix is versus HPUnix). We've made everything that we can widely
> available. Do Intel give away the sources to their BIOS?
>
> The FAQs are constantly evolving but right now they are pretty good.
> Your PALcode question is briefly answered in my Milo HOWTO but maybe
> David Mosberger-Tang should include a section in his Alpha FAQ. I
> also covered PALcode in the article that appeared in the Linux
> Journal this January. Generally speaking, you do not need to worry
> about PALcode, take what there is in Milo and use it.
>
> Milo does not have to be rebuilt as often as the kernel. It depends
> on services in the kernel (eg interrupt handling), therefore as the
> kernel evolves I produce new versions of Milo. For the last few
> months there have been no new features in Milo so the only reason for
> rebuilding it would be to add in new drivers. Right now the Qlogic
> fast and wide SCSI driver is being worked on and I have just built
> all new Milo images including that (and the NCR) SCSI drivers.
> Just like the kernel I would expect a large proportion of people to
> never ever rebuild Milo but to use whatever was on the web/CD.
> Building Milo takes no more expertise than building the kernel
> (hacking it doesn't either).
>
> Which 'limit' between Milo and the kernel? Functional? Memory?
> Milo does a full system set up (eg PCI) and tells the kernel how
> much memory there is and what sort of system this is (see /proc/cpuinfo).
> Generally, the kernel then re-does the set up as it takes over
> complete control of the system
>
Dave
