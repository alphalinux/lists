GNU Fortran (g77) needs to be improved to support the DEC Alpha
to a level on par with its support for other instruction-set
architectures, such as SPARC, ix86, and so on.

As g77 author, I've known of Alpha problems for quite a while
(over a year now, I believe), but although I made an abortive
attempt to address them via net access to an Alpha several
months ago, I've not had the time to make progress on this front.

On April 24, Mauro DePalma <mauro@craftwork.com> of Craftworks,
an organization that develops Linux distributions, contacted me
about improving g77 the Alpha. I explained that, while future
plans for g77 version 0.6 included improvements that would
likely address most, if not all, Alpha-related problems, the
lack of ongoing easy access to an actual machine made it
quite unlikely that I, personally, would do the work prior to
0.6 (which will take a few months to do, for other reasons).

Mauro responded by contacting Carrera Computers and DCG and
working up some kind of deal.

As a result, Saturday morning May 4, I received a DEC Alpha
Cabriolet running Craftworks Linux/AXP 2.1. The system's
pretty complete, though I did augment it with the necessary
monitor (I bought a Nanao T2-17).

This system is being loaned to me for the express purpose of
getting g77 "up to snuff" on it, so people won't have g77
as an excuse to not buy Alphas anymore. (There are other
aspects to the loan that I won't discuss here, but they're
highly favorable to my likely being able to continue working
on g77 support for Alpha, perhaps even replacing my Linux 486/33
with the Alpha as my primary development machine for g77
as the software matures.)

I've had only a little time to work on it so far, but I have
played with it a bit and installed X. (Well, I always figured
I'd be able to beat my chest someday when I said "I installed
X on a system, and it works!!" -- but Craftworks Linux makes
that so easy I probably shouldn't mention that I installed it
at all.)

I'm still holding down a temporary full-time job at Cadence,
so this week I expect the only progress I'll make is, say,
installing Emacs, getting a modem up and running on the new
machine, maybe connecting it and my Linux 486/33 either
via serial cable or, down the road, via networking. (For
space reasons, it's not possible to place them side by side
and work on both of them comfortably, but we are looking into
finishing our basement to make this sort of thing much easier.)

I have already built the gcc-2.7.2 and g77-0.5.17 shipped
with the system, just to observe that process (and see how
fast it goes -- that's what I mean by "playing").

This weekend, I plan to start work on getting g77 to work
better on the Alpha. I don't know how long this will take,
but I'm fairly confident that I'll find and fix most or all
of the g77 problems within a couple of weekends. Most of
my uncertainty has to do with the degree to which libf2c, gcc,
gdb, and so on need juicing, since I have less expertise on
their internals than on g77.

So, while I can't promise any particular _dates_ at this point,
and won't be working 40 hours a week on this for at least
another few weeks, it's _possible_ that Craftworks will have
a version of g77, based on 0.5.18 or 0.5.19, with patches
that greatly improve its support for the DEC Alpha in the
2-6-week time frame. (Actually, I think that timeframe is
more than possible -- likely, even.) I'm probably going
to run them through a quick alpha-test (using the tried and
true g77 alpha testers) first, but "alpha" here refers
to testing, not computers, and that's done mostly in private
for g77 still, to make sure I haven't something important out.

After the Alpha patches to g77 prove themselves, I certainly
plan to integrate them into the appropriate FSF-approved
release. That might be g77-0.5.20, depending on timing, etc.

I have to say, I'm quite impressed with the responsiveness
demonstrated by Craftworks, Carerra, and DCG. It suggests
to me a long-term commitment to the DEC Alpha chip as
a solution for their customers, because, after all, they could
have saved a bunch of cash, time, and trouble by just waiting
for g77 Alpha support to "happen" or for someone else to
loan me a machine. (In fact, I still have the email address
of someone at Digital I was going to contact when I felt I
was nearing the point of being able to commit 40 hours a week
in "payment" for being loaned, given, or given a great deal
on a machine -- so it's not like I'm saying nobody was willing
to get me one, because I hadn't asked anyone to yet, just that
these three organizations really were quite aggressive about
doing so.) So, I conclude they plan to be in this business
for quite a while, to make this sort of investment worth making.

People you might want to thank for this (and, yes, I expect
they'll be listed in the "Contributions" section of the
g77 documentation in versions that support the Alpha):

Mauro DePalma, Craftworks <mauro@craftwork.com>
Mary Cortani, Craftworks
Bruce Faust, Carerra
Steve Gaudet, DCG <sjg@dcginc.com>

As usual, watch the fortran and burley .plan files at the
FSF ("finger -l fortran@gnu.ai.mit.edu", etc.). Email
fortran@gnu.ai.mit.edu if you have trouble accessing them,
asking for help.
