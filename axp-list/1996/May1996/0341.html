> I'm a newbie to Alpha programming, and I often feel lost in it... For sure it
> is part because I am french and Slackware-addicted :-), but the information
> seems to me more private than on Intel : you must *purchase* the technical
> documentation from Digital !

strange; for me, it's just the other way round. all the DEC hardware reference manuals
and other docs are for free, only for the Architeture refman 2nd editions
I'd have to pay (order/buy it in a bookstore), even the old 1st edition was free.

on the other hand I spent quiet some money for the Intel Programmer's RefMans
which are not free at all (and other manuals like the Peripherial RefMan is $$$ too).

> Does it have to do with DEC compatibility ? Well, I think that if Linux/Alpha
> chooses to enforce Digital Unix compatibility, this means that Linux
> programmers choose to satisfy their clients prior to themselves -- which was
> never the case before. This will bring it further and further from the original
> "Linux attitude". See the people posting on this list : no newbies around, only
> kernel/libc/Fortran/gcc/hardware hackers ! Where are the happy Sunsite disk
> fillers ?

even as a kernel and X11 (and other basic applications) hacker I'd be very happy
to use DECs programming tools and try to compare results to them of DEC compilers.
as a developer I'm not only happy when my applications run faster, I'd be even
happier when building applications for others (or debugging) them would take
less time.

building gcc stage1 with "dec-cc -O2" takes only 365 cpu-secs, "gcc -O2" needs 647 secs.
and building gcc stage2 with a dec-compiled gcc takes "only" 599 secs (ok, that's only
7.5% difference...)

also I think that some sort of competition with the DEC tools are good for
the performace and functionality of Linux (nothing can be that broken that
I can't learn anything from it).

as long as we don't have to bend Linux or through away great ideas from Linus
how to do things right and as long as it doesn't take too much effort
I'd really like to see DEC Unix compatibility as a design goal for
the *current* Linux/AXP development since we're still at least very close to
it and trying to get back to some compatibility level in one or two years
from now without any synchronisation in the mean time may be much more hassle...
