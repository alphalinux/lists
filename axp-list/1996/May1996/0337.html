Dominique,

Although I can sympathise with the feelings expressed in your
reaction to Craigs mail on this list [ I could only afford a
computer when I was 24 - before that I used a programmable
calculator, and before that slide rules and log tables ] I have to
take issue with some of the thoughts, bringing up points that Craig
probably won't get into by himself.

> The respective lengths of our messages and the arguments
> developped therein may be summed up in a few words :
> you are a professionnal and I am a 20-year old student.
> You foresee very far in the future, and I am only
> concerned in my immediate pleasure, (unfortunately ?)
> consisting in lots of NIH, and a delicious sensation of
> domination over the Machine by understanding the least
> detail of it's behaviour. You are ready to make balanced
> compromises, but I am an idealist : I take GPL and POSIX
> for divine speech, and I want *all* the knowledge
> (software and documentation) for $0 (not $50, not $1 --
> please remember that I am a french student and that
> dollars are expensive to me :-). And I am ready to drive
> Linux into an economical dead end (just like the Amiga,
> the comparison was right) or to damage its future in any
> way if for the sake of code beauty.

Note that there is an implicit assumption in the above paragraph
that 'a professional is someone who doesn't mind spending gobs of
money on XXX'. When I bought my first computer - 16 years ago - it
cost the equivalent of several summers jobs of work (I only was a
poor student at that time). We didn't have a free OS, or free
software anyway, back then; you got a paper listing of the ROM
monitor - take it from there :-). Especially Craig is someone, for
whom the above quoted misunderstanding _doesn't_ apply, and this is
something that should be hammered into the heads of all the
[good-willing] hot-heads in the Linux community: There is NO
relation between the amount of money you make and 'professionalism'.
Professionalism is an attitude towards 'problem-solving',
'treating "customers" - even if they're not paying', 'design', etc.

One of the problems facing Linux (and all GNU-type free software)
is, that people view upon it as "not costing any money". This,
however, is not what is meant by "free", at least not in the GNU
sense; that is about "freedom", i.e. being able to use the software
(and its sources) freely. The problem in the first "definition" is
that it equates to "cheap", "second-hand", "throw-away job", etc. I
do not have any problem (financially) supporting the FSF, because I
_know_ this organisation aspires to quality - but I wouldn't give a
dime for the "it costs $0 so that's what I'm gonna use because I'm
a poor student" mentality.
