You (Stephen J. Gaudet <sjg@dcginc.com>) wrote:
> Digital does not stamp a Alpha processor with a speed UNTIL they are sold.

This seems extremely unlikely. Can you please provide a contact at Digital
Semiconductor with whom I can verify this claim? It is normal practice at
semiconductor companies for manufacturing to be told that sales needs x parts
at a lower speed and y parts at a higher speed. But you would have me believe
that if I order 25 chips at 233 MHz, they are going to take some unmarked
chips and run them through a tester to fill my order.

This would, however, explain why the chips are so expensive :-)

All the semiconductor vendors I'm familiar with do the device parametric
testing immediately after packaging. They don't want a bunch of untested
parts sitting around waiting for orders, because they would have no certainty
of being able to satisfy those orders. Many semiconductor vendors even give
a flow chart of the production flow in their data books, although I haven't
seen one for Digital Semiconductor.

It is often the case that many parts are sold as lower speed parts that
were untested at the higher speed but would have passed. However, since they
are untested there is no way to be certain. So many people will run the
part at the higher speed. And if there are no *obvious* problems, they
assume that it will work fine. But what if critical signal paths in the chip
are right at the edge? Can you really determine that the chip won't drop a
bit once in a while? When a semiconductor company rates a chip at a
particular speed, they actually test it to an even faster speed (and under
the full range of operating specifications) to make sure that it isn't right
on the hairy edge. But when you just crank the clock and see that it *seems*
to work, how can you have any confidence in it?

In the case of the Multia/UDB, what people are doing is even more questionable.
They are taking 21066A parts that Digital has tested for 233 MHz, and running
them at 266 MHz. However, Digital doesn't even *offer* 266 MHz parts, so
there's not even a theoretical basis for expecting them to work reliably at
that speed.

Some people have argued that since the 21066A uses the same core CPU and
process as the 21064A, it should run as fast. And Digital does offer faster
21064A parts. But even if the 21066A die was exactly the same as the 21064A
die, it still wouldn't mean that you could expect to run them as fast. The
21066 and 21066A were deliberately designed using a lower-cost PGA package,
which reduces the maximum operating speed for several reasons:

Given DECchip 21066 package cost constraints, designers ruled out
several package features used in previous designs, notably the
DECchip 21064 design. For example, on-package decoupling
capacitors were considered too expensive, since they would have
added approximately 50 percent to the package cost. To limit the
package size, the number of available package pins was
constrained to 287. Specifically, the number of package supply
pins was reduced to 66 percent of the number implemented on the
DECchip 21064 microprocessor. The number of internal package
supply planes was reduced from four to two. Looser tolerances on
internal package geometry allow the DECchip 21066 packages to be
manufactured in the package vendors' less-costly process lines;
however, less-than-optimal supply routing results.

-- "Digital's DECchip 21066: The First Cost-focused Alpha AXP Chip"
by Dina L. McKinney, Masooma Bhaiwala, Kwong-Tak A. Chui,
Christopher L. Houghton, James R. Mullens, Daniel L. Leibholz,
Sanjay J. Patel, Delvan A. Ramey, and Mark B. Rosenbluth
Digital Technical Journal Volume 6 Number 1, Winter 1994
Copyright 1994 Digital Equipment Corporation

> Therefore, you in most cases have a chip capable of running a good 10% above
> the intended clock speed listed on the top of the chip.

And with no practical way to verify it.

I notice that you did not take up my previous challenge to explain exactly
what testing DCG performs to verify that the chip won't experience subtle
errors due to overclocking. If the chips truly have such good yields at
higher clock rates over the full range of operating specifications, one would
think they would want to sell that speed grade (for a premium price, of
course).

My new challenge to you is even simpler: provide us with a contact inside
Digital who is willing to state for the record that Digital's Alpha chips
will operate reliably at 110% of the rated clock speed.

Randy Perry <randyp@aspsys.com> wrote:
> Fairly reliable? If my pacemaker was fairly reliable I would not have a
> good feeling about it

To which you (Stephen J. Gaudet <sjg@dcginc.com>) replied:
> Not fairly reliable...solid. With more than a 1000 systems out in the field
> from DCG, Carrera and another source. I can speak from facts not fiction.

Until you are able to satisfy one of my challenges, I continue to believe that
the analogy by Randy Parry was quite apt and relevant. There may certainly be
more than 1000 overclocked systems in the field, but there is no evidence to
support your claim of solid reliability, other than the anecdotal evidence
that you apparently haven't gotten complaints. All this proves is that any
errors which occur are subtle and/or somewhat infrequent, which quite frankly
does not inspire much confidence.

Eric
