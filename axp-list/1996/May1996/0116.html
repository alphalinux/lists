On Tue, 7 May 1996, David Mosberger wrote:
>
> It really is time somebody write that paper entitled "Why Low-End
> Multiprocessing is a Dumb Idea (unless your a nuclear physicist or
> Intel)".

Umm.. I have to speak up for intel here.

The PPro-200 is definitely not low-end, and it squarely beats all alpha
EV5-300's I've seen on some things (and I'm not even counting software
availability). The PPro usually has a reasonable memory subsystem (the same
can't be said of the ev5's I've seen), and it has a very reasonable price.

A quad P6 box isn't low-end by _any_ standards, unless your standards run
toward Cray or similar. Four CPU's is also a reasonable number in that you
have a chance in h*ll to actually get some useful work done with Linux on
them (ie it's few enough that the current Linux SMP implementation scales
reasonably for many things, but quite as importantly it's also few enough
that you can fill them up with a reasonable mix of jobs even without any
special hacking).

With four CPU's, "make -j3" or similar works fine, and you are likely to get
some boots when running X etc. In short, a _normal_ developer is likely to
notice, not just people using PVM.

Let's face it - intel did very well indeed with the PPro. It does badly on
floating point (but "badly" here means that it kicks ass when compared
against just about any chip that is a few years old and people who are
unhappy with PPro in that department probably wouldn't really be thrilled by
anything smaller than a 8-CPU ev5-machine), but it's very impressive indeed
on integer and most impressive on price.

Thanks to the large internal caches on the PPro, a quad PPro box is not only
cheap to produce (built-in logic for just about everything you need so you
just connect them together), but it actually should give reasonable
performance. It may not be up there in the "serious computing" league, but it
probably makes most machines that cost 10 times as much very nervous indeed.

When compared to the PPro, the alpha has got a few things going for it:
- the "fun" factor. It's more interesting than a PC.
- the "64 bit" factor. This can be the "sine qua non" in some
circumstances.
- the ev5 _is_ actually faster on basic arithmetic floating point and
many integer operations.

But anybody calling the PPro "low end" is seriously deluding himself (I can
compare numbers between Linux on a EV5-300 and Linux on a PPro-200, and they
are _close_. As far as the OS is concerned, the EV5-300 looks like it's about
10-20% faster, and that's not a huge margin).

Don't take me wrong, I love the alpha, but I'd love to have a PPro-200
_too_. And the PPro I might actually have enough money to buy..

Linus
