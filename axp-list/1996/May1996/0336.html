Dear Craig,

The respective lengths of our messages and the arguments developped therein
may be summed up in a few words : you are a professionnal and I am a 20-year
old student. You foresee very far in the future, and I am only concerned in my
immediate pleasure, (unfortunately ?) consisting in lots of NIH, and a
delicious sensation of domination over the Machine by understanding the least
detail of it's behaviour. You are ready to make balanced compromises, but I am
an idealist : I take GPL and POSIX for divine speech, and I want *all* the
knowledge (software and documentation) for $0 (not $50, not $1 -- please
remember that I am a french student and that dollars are expensive to me :-).
And I am ready to drive Linux into an economical dead end (just like the Amiga,
the comparison was right) or to damage its future in any way if for the sake of
code beauty.

Of course, this attitude undergoes all sorts of criticisms. But consider this
point : were the first Linux developpers professionnal ? Of course they were
not newbies... And of course the software has gone professionnal since 0.99;
but did you take into account that the Linux developpers are getting older too
? I am running Slackware 3.0 and I am happy (even ideologically happy) with it
since there are still jokes in the code (try a "strings -8
/usr/src/linux/vmlinux" : very impressive :-), that's what I like.

I don't exclude professionnal programming from the "Linux attitude", and I
apologize if you thought I did. But except from the very root of the Linux
system (kernel, netkit and GNU software), all the programs available for free
under Linux are written by *newbies*, who don't happen to use autoconf but
prefer Makefile-configured #ifdef's (if any :-). And I like the incorrectnesses
which often arise in the code I recompile : they aren't the result of the
incurable incompetence of people designing or coding just to make MONEY, but
the youthful deeds of people hungry to learn more. I don't prefer Slackware to
RedHat because it's smaller, but because of the "satan, snake, gonzo" story :-)
and because you get 6x more CD's for 6x less bucks (at least in France).

According to this amateurism and idealism, I should have stuck with an Intel
box as you said. But always being an idealist, I don't really want to give my
bucks to Intel who, in order to break the bridge Linux (and other freebies)
just threw across the client machine/server machine gap, sells "only Doom"
no-parity-but-sound-onboard pieces of crap to all my unfortunate friends.
Aren't the AXPpci boards aimed at concurrencing Intel ? So I made use of my
citizen and consumer rights, it cost me 2000 Francs ($400) more, not counting
the additionnal cost of buying parity RAMs and SCSI disks. Well. But it makes
me feel better.

You say you (the professionnals) need a free OS, and that you can't wait for
the newbies to develop it if you want it to be optimally desingned and coded ?
Please remember that newbies and cheap Intels originated the Linux movement,
and that if computers had always been a matter of professionnals, the idea of
code freedom would never have made such a long way -- see in what state HURD
is. Digital is not making us a present by giving away its docs : it only feels
the wind turning and does not gain any consideration from me therefore -- just
plain consumer interest.

Back to the primary topic : do we need to follow Digital ? I understand that
it is a guarantee for the future of Linux, and that it is far better than
letting Linux sink and Intel reign alone... But it is somehow a kind of
violation of the spirit of the GPL since the Linux code will always belong to
the amateurs who originated it -- Source freedom is granted by the GPL, but not
documentation freedom, and that's what I fear for the future : Linux/AXP may
become so difficult to understand due to optimization considerations that the
source code would become helpless. It is already the case for MILO, I fear...

In the ideal case, this issue should not be solved only under technical or
political considerations : the vox populi should be heared too. But the real
problem is that AXPpci@166MHz owners and EB164 owners don't want the same
thing... And that the Alpha/Linux programmers own the latter. Owning the
workforce, you own the choices, I respect this and fear above all that you
interpret this answer as an insolent flame; I realize that I don't have my word
to say in that story, and that the only alternative I have is to start my own
kernel implementation -- which would be as painful a schism as the GNU/Lucid
Emacs story, if I could only find the necessary enthusiasm to develop something
which hardly presents any interest at all.

With best regards,

--
