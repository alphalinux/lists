Morten wrote:

> It seems to me that all the 'official' specmarks are based on
>DEC Unix or NT rather than linux-axp. Are there any 'private' specmarks
>floating around wrt. linux-axp?

No, at least not here. SPECint9[25] is composed of C applications, so
we could generate SPECint ratings for Linux/Alpha. But SPECfp9[25] is
mostly composed of Fortran code. Since no Fortran compiler exists for
Linux/Alpha, there is no VALID way to generate SPECfp results.

No one is shipping a f77 compiler for Linux/Alpha (yes I know Microway
is working on one, but it isn't ready - at least not as of a couple of
weeks ago), and f2c is not a valid choice, due to issues with language
semantic differences between FORTRAN and C, with respect to optimization.

But this brings up a meta-question. What are meaningful benchmarks for Linux?
Not just Linux/Alpha, but Linux on all the rest of the platforms?
In the realm of business processing systems, TPC benchmarks are important.
For UNIX engineering environments, SPEC is important.
For general PC desktop stuff, Winstones and BapCO are important.
So what is important in the Linux world, as a performance metric?
The length of time to perform a scratch kernel build?
I find that I compare the various Linux systems I deal with based on
how long it takes me to do a kernel build, but then, I'm messing about
with kernels and compilers.

I have thought about this quite a bit, and have yet to come up with a
good answer to the problem. Any suggestions?
