Phil Carmody wrote:
>For every architecture, where e = machine epsilon:
>(-1 + 1) + e == e
>but
> (1 + e) - 1 == e

To be precise, e is the smallest number such that 1+e != 1. So if we
take 0<e'<e, then

    (-1 + 1) + e' == e'
but
    (1 + e') - 1 == 0

>The a rule of thumb in these matters is to try to split the 'small'
>data from the 'large' data, and handle all the 'small' data first before
>it gets swamped by the 'large' data.
>
>
>Oh - I was incorrect with my previous reference:
>"What every computer scientist should know about floating point
>arithmetic" is by Goldberg, not Kahan. However, it's 'in the style' of
>similar things by Kahan.

You can find some of Kahan's work at http://www.cs.berkeley.edu/~wkahan/.
For example,

  How JAVA's Floating-Point Hurts Everyone Everywhere
   http://www.cs.berkeley.edu/~wkahan/JAVAhurt.pdf

and

  Miscalculating Area and Angles of a Needle-like Triangle
   http://www.cs.berkeley.edu/~wkahan/Triangle.pdf

The difference in the floating point calculations on the x86 vs the
Alpha trace back to the hardware implementations. The extra precision
in the x86 makes life much easier---it narrows the window of numerical
instability in almost every situation.
