Alphas have many benefits over Intel (about as many as Intel has over
Alpha). Generally, there is MUCH more of a difference between an Alpha
processor and an Intel processor than just clock speeds. For instance,
I
personally prefer programming on RISC machines (Alpha, MIPS, PowerPC,
etc) for many reasons that I won't get into. Intel's 80x86 processors
are
all CISC (later models have a RISC core, I believe, but it's still
accessed through CISC means) and, while this can be a benefit at times,
it
can also be a royal pain at other times.

Secondly, Alphas have FAR superiour floating point performance, even
compared to an Intel with the same clock speed. For instance, my old
UDB
ran at 166MHz, which was equivalent to a Pentium 75-100 in terms of
overall performance, BUT this same little machine with terrible memory
bus
bandwidth always bested our Pentium Pro 180 in terms of floating point
calculations (that's real-life performance, not benchmark). Now that
I've
stepped up to a 164SX w/533MHz 21164PC, I've yet to encounter even a
Pentium II-class processor which can outperform it in floating
point-intensive applications (to give you an idea, I'm averaging 10.5
hrs
on a seti@home packet...and this machine is considered to be "low end"
by
modern Alpha standards). Not bad for a machine that I only paid $500
for
:-)

Of course, Linux on Alphas is still in the "getting serious" stage, to
be
honest. Digital UNIX's compilers and tools for the Alpha produce vastly
superiour binaries that average between 20-50% better performance
compared
to Linux-compiled versions (for obvious reasons...DEC invented these
things, so writing compilers for it is easier for them).

> do the program's
> in use utilize the 64 bit technology effectively?

Which brings me to my last point: true 64-bit operations and 64-bit
general purpose registers (it's about time). Any software that's
properly
coded (basically, it should be portable and not rely on fixed type
sizes)
can immediately take advantage of the benefits of 64-bit machines,
including Alpha. Of course, porting applications can be a pain, but I
personally love doing it (which is why I keep buying Alphas...to port
packages for Debian). I don't know how much programming that you do
personally, but you'd be amazed at the performance hits that an Intel
machine takes to manipulate large amounts of 64-bit data. Alphas don't
suffer from this at all. The downside, of course, is that stricter
alignment rules must be adhered to at all costs (to work around them
costs
you DEARLY in cycles).

In all, I love working on my Alphas and use them for just about
everything
that I like to do (short of games...which I still keep a Windows PC
around
for). A few years ago, when I started on Alphas, I would've told you
that
you shouldn't get an Alpha unless you liked living on the edge and
pushing
the envelope (Linux-wise, that is...Digital UNIX was always rock-solid),
but these days, I consider it to be a stable platform for many
applications.

Why alpha?

Several reasons:

        Mostly faster clock rates, but also orders of magnitudes larger
        primary and secondary CPU caches.

        64 bit applications are in use, but also 64 bit kernels solve
        a lot of other problems more gracefully than synthetic 64 bit
        values.

        A very much nicer chip and MMU architecture.

        An instruction set that makes sense and doesn't haul around a
        ridiculous notion of 'modes' for legacy reasons.

        Also, like even supporting Sparc still, Intel should not be
        allowed to have it all.

Matthew Jacob [mjacob@feral.com]

Its a combination of reasons for us. We do biological database
searching
and a 533Mhz Alpha is faster than a 550Mhz Intel and at current prices
the
Alpha is cheaper. That being said, the difference is only around
30-40%,
where the fun really starts is when we use a 64 bit coding technique

32 bit code through this method and I have been awarded a Royal Society

are going to sell linux-axp systems preinstalled with RedHat and our
software as a turnkey solution for labs wanting to perform
Smith/Waterman database searches at speeds which would require >10 Intel
550Mhz boxes. That is why we use Alphas.

Greetings,

I use Alpha for engineering simulations because of floating-point speed
relative to Intel. I don't know the details, but I think it has
something to do with the number of registers, in addition to the clock
speed- for egcs-compiled code.

If you can code in asm, then Alpha really shines. Kazushige Goto has
written some amazing linear algebra routines that I use on a daily basis
which take advantage of Alpha's quad-issue capability, i.e. four
instructions per cycle, and they run at about 3/4 of the theoretical
maximum speed of the processor, about 760 MFlops for a $2K 533 MHz 21164
box. It simply blows away everything else in price/performance, by a
very long way!

Also in this category are the fast math libraries, such as libffm (free
fast math) by Joachim Wesner and K. Goto, and the Compaq Portable Math
Library, both of which are very fast.

When the Compaq compilers for Linux are released (late summer/early
fall), Alpha will become even more appealing...
